### [Scale: State][Type: Canon][Status: Restated] Garka — Stable State Overview

**Primary Destination:** `wiki/states/garka.md`
**Secondary Destination:** `wiki/geography/nereth/western-maritime-nereth.md`
**Standalone Page?:** yes
**Expected Action:** create
**Already in Wiki?:** no

#### Summary Line
- **Garka is a tiny Nordic coastal polity whose internal order remains wholly Norse and tribal even though foreigners style it the “Duchy of Garka”; it endures less because neighbors think it ought to exist than because it is too small, cohesive, fortified, and low-yield to be worth the trouble of extinguishing.**

#### Core identity
- Garka does not understand itself internally as a feudal duchy. “Duchy of Garka” is a foreign diplomatic rendering used only with outsiders for legibility and formal standing.
- In native terms, Garka is a communal tribal-sacral polity of the Four Winds.
- Garka is culturally Nordic and religiously Skrosenist with unusually high internal cohesion.
- Garkans place great importance on the fact that no Norse people have ever been permanently ruled by non-Norse peoples and treat this as evidence of divine favor.

#### Geography
- Garka occupies a small rugged forested littoral pocket north of Bramia, centered on a sheltered fortified harbor and a very limited coastal-interior body rather than a broad territorial interior.
- Its geography makes it locally useful and reasonably defensible but neither rich enough nor central enough to tempt sustained major-power annexation.
- The capital, Raudar, is a fortified port and should be understood as the state’s governing and military heart.

#### Politics and governing order
- Every Garkan belongs by birth to one of the Four Tribes, known collectively as the Four Winds.
- Tribal elders are elected within their own communities. Outside the Þing of the Four Winds, they rarely function as a single governing body.
- Most Garkan social and political life is communal, local, and tribally ordered rather than centrally administered.
- The Four Winds exist chiefly for intertribal coordination when common action is required: war and common defense, settlement of disputes between tribes, and management of shared obligations such as levies, tithes, or other contributions owed for matters concerning Garka as a whole.

#### The sacred office
- The ruler is the **Goðbróðir**; when a woman holds the office, she is the **Goðsystir**.
- The office is held for life, never nominated, never campaigned for, and treated as a sacred burden rather than a prize.
- When the office falls vacant, a six-month interregnum follows. After that, the elders gather in the Þing of the Four Winds and each casts a single lot.
- The lots are tallied and the chosen elder is named to the office whether willing or not, since the result is understood as divine indication and must be obeyed.
- Women are fully legitimate holders of the office, though rare in practice; historically there have been only five Goðsystirs.

#### Accountability and challenge
- Any elder may challenge the reigning Goðbróðir / Goðsystir to single combat only after securing majority support in the council of elders of each of the four tribes.
- Falling even one vote short in any tribe disqualifies the challenge.
- An elder who attempts such a challenge without the required support is judged disloyal and is expected to die by ritual suicide.
- The challenged ruler may name a champion to fight in their stead. This carries no stigma for an aged or infirm officeholder, but is deeply taboo for a young and vigorous one and would likely provoke further challenges.

#### Spouses and eligibility
- The spouse of a Goðbróðir / Goðsystir holds no second office and no co-rule by right.
- The only formal privilege of the marriage is elevation to elder-status within the spouse’s own birth tribe.
- This elder-status makes widows and widowers of sacred rulers fully legitimate future candidates for the office.
- Historically, most Goðsystirs had first been the wives of former Goðbróðir.

#### Economy / society / foreign posture
- Garka is not a major trade state. Its economy likely rests on fishing and coastal labor, timber and forest products, small-harbor traffic, limited local dues, and modest subsistence support from its rough littoral hinterland.
- Socially it should feel proud, local, hard-edged, and suspicious of outsiders, with little need for foreign validation.
- To outsiders, Garka is often regarded as quaint, rustic, or backwater; internally, Garkans regard such judgments as irrelevant.
- Garka survives because surrounding powers gain too little from abolishing it and would face disproportionate friction in trying to rule it, especially given Norse hostility to foreign domination.

### [Scale: State][Type: Canon][Status: New] Laups — Stable State Overview

**Primary Destination:** `wiki/states/laups.md`
**Secondary Destination:** `wiki/geography/nereth/western-maritime-nereth.md`
**Standalone Page?:** yes
**Expected Action:** create
**Already in Wiki?:** no

#### Summary Line
- **Laups is a broad Nordic upland kingdom whose strength lies less in cities or maritime wealth than in holding together a large, rough northern country through a few strong royal nodes, a cohesive Skrosenist culture, and a monarchy built for sparse settlement and long endurance.**

#### Core identity
- Laups is one of the Nordic highland kingdoms of western Nereth and belongs firmly to the broader Nordic / Skrosenist world.
- It should be understood as a broad, sparse, rough northern kingdom: cohesive, under-urbanized, and territorially weighty rather than commercially glamorous.
- Laups is coastal, but it is not a coast-first maritime kingdom. Its identity comes more from holding country than from funneling trade.

#### Geography
- Laups occupies a large northwestern shoulder of western Nereth, with a long exposed coast, a rough upland and broken interior, harsher northern country, and a somewhat more workable southern/southeastern belt.
- It is better read as a broad upland kingdom with forests, rougher central and northern belts, and more usable approaches in the south than as a pure mountain-wall state.
- The kingdom’s true political nerve-center lies at its southern hinge, where the main approaches, waters, and border relationships compress.

#### Capital and major nodes
- Though not the kingdom’s largest town, **Aldved** serves as Laups’s royal hinge-capital.
- Aldved sits where sea access, inland waters, and the kingdom’s most important external routes converge, making it a royal-defense and contact center rather than merely a commercial capital.
- **Hvenesbak**, as the larger town, should be understood as the stronger commercial/demographic node without displacing Aldved’s royal and strategic primacy.

#### Economy
- The kingdom’s wealth rests on linked but modest systems—upland production, forestry and woodland resources, limited coastal traffic, southern hinge exchange, and the seasonal use of remote northern crown holdings—rather than on any single dominant commercial center.
- Laups has coastal access without maritime centrality; its low urbanization and tiny port count argue against reading it as a major sea-gate state.
- The kingdom’s material basis should therefore be imagined as broad, rough, and distributed rather than highly concentrated.

#### Flaland frontier
- Laups’s border with Flaland is generally peaceful and socially porous.
- Frontier districts interact across it more readily than with distant capitals, and the Laups–Flaland line forms part of a living shared Nordic and Skrosenist border society rather than a hard civilizational frontier.
- Minor grievances are sometimes discharged through bounded ceremonial battles rather than open war.

#### Northern crown holdings
- **New Laups Colony** and **New Bostingia Colony** are not failed settler provinces but seasonal northern crown holdings.
- They are maintained for temporary maritime, extractive, or other periodic use without permanent settlement or standing civilian population.
- Their existence gives Laups a thin northern maritime reach without turning it into a heavy settler-colonial power.

#### Society / religion / foreign posture
- Laups is culturally Nordic and religiously Skrosenist with little sign of major internal ethnocultural fracture.
- Socially it should feel austere, proud, local outside the main nodes, and more customary than bureaucratic.
- To neighbors, Laups appears solid, somewhat hard, and more respected for endurance than for brilliance: a northern weight rather than a glamorous power.
