**Editorial Convention**

Unless a passage is marked as a *GM Note*, the main text is written as
known, broadly knowable, or reasonably reconstructable history within
the world of Eutheria: the kind of material an educated scholar, cleric,
state archivist, or well-informed adventurer might encounter.
Blockquoted *GM Notes* identify hidden truths, campaign secrets,
restricted lore, or behind-the-scenes realities not generally available
to players or ordinary in-world observers.

For downstream use in a mixed public-and-GM reference system, the
working distinction is simple: the main text presents public-facing
canon or history that can reasonably be reconstructed, while *GM Notes*
preserve hidden causality, restricted truths, and designer-facing lore.
Not every *GM Note* requires its own wiki article. Some function better
as campaign prompts, hidden context, or internal guidance.

The material that most clearly belongs in GM-only treatment includes:

- restricted manuscript traditions and their suppressed historical
significance
- hidden lines of causation beneath major historical crises
- concealed anti-institutional infiltration within major religious
structures
- the compromised condition of the current Pontif
- the hidden chronology beneath the public history of the Fracture
- strategic truths about the rarity of apex lawful divine casters and
the scale of a full Great Old Ones awakening

Where a topic appears in both the main text and a *GM Note*, the main
text should be regarded as the public layer and the *GM Note* as the
hidden explanatory layer.

**Part I: Geography**

**Chapter 1: The World of Eutheria**

Eutheria is a large terrestrial world dominated by vast oceans and four
major continents. Its physical character — mountain ranges, river
systems, lake basins, biome distributions, and ocean currents — sets the
conditions within which all civilizations develop. The world is
pre-industrial in character, with the overwhelming majority of all
populations living in rural settings.

At the heart of the known world lies a vast body of water known as
Oceanus Centralis, often called the Central Ocean. This ocean separates
the major continents and serves as the primary medium through which they
are connected. Island chains and deep-water passages cross its expanse,
creating natural maritime routes. Its currents and prevailing winds
shape sailing conditions and influence which routes are navigable at
different seasons. All long-distance intercontinental movement depends
on seafaring.

**The Four Continents**

**Valthera** (Northwest) — A large and geographically varied continent.
Its northern reaches are dominated by tundra, glacial terrain, and
subpolar conditions. Moving south, the landscape transitions through
boreal forest and temperate woodland into more productive plains and
river basins. Interior mountain chains divide the continent into
distinct drainage basins. Its southern coast faces Oceanus Centralis,
giving it natural access to maritime trade. Valthera is notably
lake-rich, particularly across its central and eastern portions, which
has historically supported agriculture and inland waterborne commerce.
The continent is home to Tengcian, Cheoni, and Dwarven
peoples.

**Nereth** (Northeast) — A geographically significant continent
encompassing a wide range of terrain: broad river plains and deep
alluvial soils in its lowlands, several major mountain ranges crossing
the interior, and varied coastal terrain. A major mountain range running
across the continent acts as a hard boundary between its eastern
heartland and western coastal regions. The continent's interior contains
some of the world's highest peaks. Nereth is home to Flandric,
Veltric, Monzian, Alaian, Sinzian,
Lienian, Fernish, Olmagan, Westric,
and Dwarven peoples.

**Kasmora** (Southwest) — The most agriculturally productive of the
known continents. Vast interior plains, reliable river systems, and deep
alluvial soils make it exceptionally well suited to large-scale farming.
The terrain is relatively flat across much of the interior, with uplands
appearing mainly at the margins. A mountain range separates the northern
coast from the agricultural interior. The continent's rivers drain
outward toward the coast, providing both irrigation and transport.
Kasmora is home to Salan, Kordemelian, Rawran,
Harian, Dukanese, and Likian peoples.

**Golsta** (Southeast) — The most inhospitable of the known continents.
Much of its landmass lies under permanent ice or permafrost, and its
mountainous interior prevents easy travel or settlement. The habitable
zones are limited to certain coastal margins and lower-altitude valleys.
These conditions prevent agriculture at any significant scale. Golsta's
wilderness is vast and largely unexplored by humanoid peoples. It
supports populations of cold-adapted megafauna and contains the largest
surviving concentrations of dragons in the known world, particularly
white and silver dragons suited to glacial environments. Golsta has no
significant humanoid population.

**The Central Island Chain**

A chain of islands situated in the strait between Valthera and Kasmora.
The islands are controlled in their entirety by Likia, a Likian-culture
naval state of Kasmoran coastal origin. Likia's dominance over this
chain gives it significant leverage over maritime passage between the
two continents. Not all islands in the archipelago fall under full
Likian authority — numerous smaller islands host pirate enclaves and
smuggling ports that operate beyond the reach of Likian patrols,
maintaining a shadow economy that complicates regional maritime
politics.

**Chapter 2: Valthera — The Northwestern Continent**

**Geography & Terrain**

The northern reaches of Valthera are covered by glacier and tundra,
rendering them largely uninhabitable. Moving southward the continent
transitions through taiga into temperate deciduous and rainforest zones.
The interior contains significant mountain ranges where Dwarven culture
is concentrated. Valthera is the most lake-rich continent, with large
lake systems spread across its central and eastern portions providing
the foundation for inland waterborne commerce. River systems are
well-developed throughout the southern half. The southern mountains of
Valthera are the site of the Battle of Trelhelm (9034 BLC), the decisive
engagement that ended the Thousand-Year War.

**Biomes**

Northern Valthera: glacier, tundra, taiga. Central and southern
Valthera: temperate deciduous forest, temperate rainforest, wetland,
grassland. The lake-dense interior creates unusually productive
agricultural and commercial zones relative to the continent's northerly
position.

**Peoples of Valthera**

**Tengcian culture** — The dominant culture of Valthera, occupying
the largest territorial extent. Naval in character, with significant
lake and river systems underpinning commerce and military power.

**Cheoni culture** — A significant culture occupying the western
portions of Valthera.

**Dwarven** — Present as a highland culture in the mountain ranges of
the interior, occupying steep terrain rather than open lowlands. The
Dwarven highland streak runs through the border zone between Tengcian and
Cheoni cultural territories, creating intimate contact between Dwarven
and Cheoni communities that has had significant religious consequences.
Dwarves are also present in Nereth's mountain ranges.

**Chapter 3: Nereth — The Northeastern Continent**

**Geography & Terrain**

Nereth's northern coast is glaciated. The continent's interior contains
the highest elevation peaks on Eutheria. These mountain ranges create
natural barriers that have contributed to significant political
fragmentation throughout history. A major mountain range divides the
eastern heartland from the western coastal regions, historically acting
as a hard cultural and political boundary. The Dwarven realms control
the passes through this range and dominate trans-mountain trade. This
division has produced two entirely distinct historical spheres — western
Nereth developing independently of the Veltric Empire that dominated the
east.

**Biomes**

Northern Nereth: glacier, tundra, taiga. Interior highlands: temperate
rainforest, grassland. Eastern lowlands: temperate deciduous forest,
wetland. Western coast: temperate deciduous forest, temperate
rainforest. The eastern lowlands' fertile river valleys provided the
agricultural foundation for the Veltric Empire.

**Peoples of Nereth**

**Flandric culture** — Highland culture occupying the northwestern
portions of Nereth.

**Veltric culture** — Present in central and eastern Nereth. The
cultural origin of the Veltric Empire.

**Monzian culture** — Present in central and eastern Nereth.
Integral to the Veltric Imperial sphere.

**Alaian culture** — The most populous culture on Nereth,
occupying large swaths of the southern continent including tropical
forest and jungle regions of the southwest. Peripherally influenced by
the Veltric Empire but never fully conquered. Ports in the Alaian
southwest were historically the terminus for Likian maritime trade
entering Nereth.

**Sinzian culture** — Present in the western regions of Nereth.

**Lienian culture** — Present on the western coast of Nereth,
facing Oceanus Centralis.

**Fernish culture** — Small nomadic-type presence on Nereth.

**Olmagan culture** — Small river-type culture presence on
Nereth. Closely integrated with the Veltric Empire as a frontier
province.

**Westric culture** — Small highland-type culture presence on
Nereth.

**Dwarven** — Highland culture occupying the great mountain range that
crosses Nereth's interior. The Dwarven realms control the passes through
this range and are the indispensable intermediaries for all
trans-mountain trade and travel. Their strategic position has made them
increasingly wealthy since the disruption of the southern Likian trade
route.

**Chapter 4: Kasmora — The Southwestern Continent**

**Geography & Terrain**

Kasmora's interior tends toward grassland and savanna biomes south of a
northern mountain range that separates the coast from the agricultural
heartland. The terrain is generally more open than Nereth, supporting
nomadic and semi-nomadic lifestyles across large portions of the
continent. The northern coast of Kasmora faces the strait containing the
Likian island chain, giving northern Kasmoran coastal states natural
access to Oceanus Centralis maritime routes. Likia originated on the
northeastern coast, separated from the agricultural interior by the
northern mountain range, developing independently as a maritime power.

**Biomes**

Northern coast: temperate deciduous forest, temperate rainforest.
Interior: grassland, savanna, with some hot and cold desert. Southern
margins: tropical seasonal forest, tropical rainforest. Kasmora's rivers
are its economic arteries — draining outward toward the coast, providing
both irrigation and transport for the world's most agriculturally
productive landmass.

**Peoples of Kasmora**

**Salan culture** — Nomadic type. The savannah biome of the
interior supports large herds but insufficient reliable water
concentration to anchor dense permanent settlement everywhere, making
mobile pastoralism the dominant Salan lifestyle.

**Kordemelian culture** — Settled (Generic type). Controls large
central territories including the Kordemelian Khaganate.

**Rawran culture** — Settled (Generic type). Present in
eastern Kasmora.

**Harian culture** — Present in Kasmora.

**Dukanese culture** — Present in Kasmora.

**Likian culture** — Naval type. Likian culture emerged from the
intermingling of Flandric seafarers and Kordemelian Kasmoran
coastal peoples over centuries. It produced Likia, which controls the
central island chain. Present on Kasmora's northern coast, including
territories now absorbed by the Kordemelian Khaganate.

**Chapter 5: Golsta — The Southeastern Continent**

**Geography & Terrain**

Golsta is the southernmost and most inhospitable of the known
continents. Much of its landmass lies under permanent ice or permafrost,
and its mountainous interior prevents easy travel or settlement. The
habitable zones are limited to certain coastal margins and
lower-altitude valleys. These conditions prevent agriculture at any
significant scale. Golsta's wilderness is vast, largely unexplored by
humanoid peoples, and constitutes the largest contiguous dragon
territory in the known world.

**The Dragon Population**

Golsta supports the largest surviving concentrations of dragons in the
known world. The glacial environments are particularly suited to white
dragons and silver dragons, which are the most commonly encountered
species. The extreme and varied terrain across the continent supports
other species as well, though population estimates for any given type
are speculative given the difficulty of systematic survey.

**The Council of Uindossethba**

The only governing body of any kind in Golsta is the Council of
Uindossethba — not a state or political institution in any humanoid
sense, but a convocation of the most ancient dragons of the continent.
The Council convenes once every hundred years to address matters of
importance to dragonkind: territorial disputes, the management of
hunting grounds, the resolution of conflicts between major lineages, and
any external threats to the continent's draconic population.

The Council has no permanent membership beyond the criterion of age and
power. Any dragon of sufficient antiquity may attend. Its decisions
carry the weight of tradition and the authority of the most formidable
beings on the continent, but no enforcement mechanism exists beyond the
collective will of those present. Between convocations, Golsta is
effectively ungoverned — each dragon manages its own territory according
to its own judgment.

The Council convenes at a location known as Uindossethba, the name by
which the broader draconic culture of Golsta is also identified. The
site's exact nature and location are unknown to outsiders.

> *GM Note: The Council of Uindossethba is an extremely rare point of
> contact between the outside world and Golstan dragons. Its convocation
> cycle means that truly urgent matters affecting dragonkind may go
> unaddressed for decades. A dragon powerful enough to call an emergency
> session outside the regular cycle would be extraordinary indeed.*

**Chapter 6: Likia — The Maritime Power**

Likia is the most strategically important naval state in Eutheria.
Despite its modest territorial size — a population of approximately
898,000 across an island chain and a narrow Kasmoran coastal strip — its
geographic position astride the central island chain of Oceanus
Centralis allows it to exert enormous influence over international
commerce entirely disproportionate to its physical scale. The island
chain it controls lies directly across the principal sea routes linking
Nereth, Valthera, and Kasmora. A large portion of intercontinental trade
therefore passes through Likian-controlled waters.

Likia was founded at Year 0 LC — an event that established not only a
new state but the international calendar that the entire world now uses.
Its founding at 0 LC was not a dramatic revolution or conquest but a
peaceful formal declaration of independence from the Flandric
peoples of Nereth from whom the Likian founders descended, acknowledging
a cultural and political separation that had already become reality
through centuries of drift. The Likian Calendar has since become the
international standard for chronology across the known world. The
current year is 1026 LC.

For a full account of Likia’s origins, geography, government, political
factions, settlements, religion, and key relationships, see Part VII:
Likia.

> *GM Note: Likia’s control of the strait between Valthera and Kasmora
> makes it the single most strategically positioned state in the world.
> Any power wishing to move goods or military forces between the two
> southern continents must either deal with Likia or navigate around the
> archipelago at significant cost in time and risk.*

**Part II: Cosmology & The Planes**

**Chapter 8: The First Moment & The Structure of Reality**

The ordered cosmos began in a single instantaneous event known as the
First Moment. Three events occurred at the exact same instant: the
Pleroma came into existence; the primordial entity known as Free Will
was expelled from that realm; and the entire metaverse — the Outer
Planes, the Inner Planes, and the Material Plane in its infinite
entirety — was formed. These were not sequential events. They were
different expressions of the same moment of creation.

This cosmological beginning should not be misunderstood as implying that individual worlds or their living species appeared fully formed at that instant. Within the Material Plane, universes still unfold through ordinary natural law across deep time. Eutheria therefore possesses real geological history and biological evolution over millions of years, including the evolution of dragons and most other living things, even in a cosmos whose magical permeability is unusually high.

**The Pleroma**

The Pleroma exists outside the normal structure of the metaverse. It is
not a plane of existence accessible through spells, portals, or planar
travel. Instead, it represents a state of primordial unity that stands
apart from the separation of reality into individual forces — complete,
undivided, and untouched by the fracturing that produced the ordered
cosmos. Within the Pleroma dwell entities known as the First Principles
— metaphysical intelligences that embody the fundamental aspects of
existence itself. The Pleroma is also the ultimate metaphysical source
of all souls. Every soul that inhabits a mortal creature originates from
this primordial realm and the rarest, most transcendent souls ultimately
return to it.

**Free Will & Its Fracturing**

Among the First Principles was one that embodied choice, individuality,
and deviation from predetermined order — Free Will. Because the Pleroma
exists as a state of absolute unity, the existence of genuine choice was
incompatible with its nature. At the First Moment, Free Will was
expelled. This expulsion was not an event that occurred after creation —
it was an intrinsic part of creation itself. Once separated from the
Pleroma, Free Will fragmented into nine emanations corresponding to the
nine fundamental alignments: Lawful Good, Neutral Good, Chaotic Good,
Lawful Neutral, True Neutral, Chaotic Neutral, Lawful Evil, Neutral
Evil, and Chaotic Evil. Eight of these emanations became vast cosmic
forces associated with the Outer Planes. The ninth stabilized into the
Material Plane, representing the point of equilibrium between all
alignment forces and the natural home of mortal life across its infinite
universes.

**The Council of the Eight**

The eight alignment emanations of Free Will are collectively known as
the Council of the Eight. These entities are not gods in the traditional
sense. They do not rule celestial kingdoms, demand worship, or intervene
in the daily affairs of mortals. They are the metaphysical embodiments
of the eight non-neutral alignments. Each member of the Council
generates a corresponding Outer Plane which expresses the nature of that
alignment in its purest form. Their existence maintains the dynamic
tension between alignment forces that structure reality.

**The Free Will Identity Theory**

The most profound cosmological theory — one that no being within the
metaverse can confirm or refute — is that the metaverse is not merely
the product of Free Will's fracturing but is Free Will itself: the
actual body of that Principle as it exists outside the Pleroma,
differentiated and expressed across infinite universes. Under this
interpretation, every soul, every choice, every alignment force, every
civilization that rises and falls within the Material Plane is a facet
of the same primordial entity — Free Will experiencing itself through
the only means available to it outside of perfect unity. Whether this is
true is, by definition, unknowable from within.

**Chapter 9: The Planes**

**The Outer Planes**

The Outer Planes are regions of existence in which alignment is not
merely a moral description but a fundamental property of reality. Each
corresponds to one alignment and embodies its principles in their purest
form. The Outer Planes are also the origin of outsiders — creatures
whose existence is defined by the alignment of their home plane.

| Lawful Good     | Heaven    | Pure law and benevolence    |
| Neutral Good    | Nirvana   | Goodness without constraint |
| Chaotic Good    | Elysium   | Freedom and virtue          |
| Lawful Neutral  | Utopia    | Order without moral bias    |
| Chaotic Neutral | Limbo     | Pure randomness and change  |

| Chaotic Evil    | Abyss     | Destruction and ruin        |

**The Material Plane**

The Material Plane is not a single world or universe. It is the
equilibrium point of the cosmic alignment system, and within it exist an
infinite number of universes — each one a distinct expression of the
balance between the alignment forces. Eutheria's universe is one of
these. The alignment structure, the soul cycle, the Pleroma, and the
pressure of the Kenoma apply equally to every universe within the
Material Plane. The metaphysical machinery described here is not local
to Eutheria — it governs all of ordered existence.

**The Inner Planes**

The Inner Planes represent the fundamental building blocks of physical
reality, created simultaneously with the Material Plane. They consist of
six planes: the four Elemental Planes (Air, Earth, Fire, Water) and the
two Energy Planes. The Positive Energy Plane contains the fundamental
life energy of the universe — responsible for life, healing, growth, and
the animating force in living creatures. The Negative Energy Plane
contains the opposing force: entropy, decay, and the draining of life.

**The Astral, Ethereal, and Shadow Planes**

The Astral Plane surrounds the entire planar structure and functions as
the cosmic medium connecting all planes. Travel between planes typically
occurs through the Astral Plane. The Ethereal Plane is a ghostlike layer
that overlaps the Material Plane, acting as a spiritual echo of the
physical world. It serves as the domain of ghosts, incorporeal beings,
and dream fragments. The Shadow Plane is a dark reflection of the
Material Plane — mirroring the structure of the physical world in a
distorted, gloom-filled form. It is infused with negative energy and
serves as the source of shadow magic.

**Chapter 10: Souls & The Cycle of Existence**

Every mortal creature possesses a soul — a divine spark originating from
the Pleroma that inhabits a physical body within the Material Plane. The
soul is not the creature itself; it is the metaphysical essence that
animates it. Mortal life is the arena in which the soul develops
alignment, identity, and spiritual resonance. When mortal creatures die,
their souls pass into the Astral Plane. From there, three primary
destinies exist:

**Reincarnation** — Most souls eventually return to mortal life. Their
spiritual alignment is too mixed or imperfect to be drawn permanently
into any single Outer Plane.

**Transfiguration** — Some souls become so perfectly aligned with one of
the Outer Planes that they are drawn into it permanently, undergoing
transfiguration into a native outsider of that plane. This process
explains the origin of outsiders.

**Return to the Pleroma** — A rare few souls achieve perfect harmony and
balance, escaping the cycle entirely and returning to the primordial
source from which they originally emerged.

**Chapter 11: The Kenoma & The Outer Gods**

**The Kenoma**

The Pleroma is perfect fullness. The Kenoma is not merely its opposite —
it is its precondition. Metaphysically speaking, not temporally, the
Kenoma was before the Pleroma. The Pleroma did not push back something
that arose alongside it. It asserted itself against something that had
always been there — the eternal ground of incompleteness within which
the impossible miracle of ordered existence briefly flickered into
being. The Kenoma is not a place, not a dimension, not darkness or void
in any sense the ordered cosmos can meaningfully describe. It is the
absence of the substrate that makes description possible. It has no
intelligence, no purpose, and no will. It does not seek to consume
existence. It simply is the condition in which existence cannot be — and
existence is the anomaly, not the Kenoma. The metaverse exists in the
space the First Moment carved out of what had always been and what, the
Kenoma's eternal nature suggests, will eventually be again.

The Negative Energy Plane, undead, entropy, and destruction are all part
of the ordered metaverse — they are natural, they have alignment, they
participate in the cycle of existence. The Kenoma is categorically
different. It does not destroy within the system. It annihilates the
system's substrate itself.

> *GM Note: A creature destroyed by negative energy is dead —
> potentially raisable, potentially undead, its soul still in the cycle.
> A creature touched by the Kenoma is not dead. It is erased. There is
> no soul to raise, no body to animate, no identity to persist. This
> should be extraordinarily rare and treated as categorically different
> from any other form of death in the game.*

**The Outer Gods**

Just as the Pleroma contains the First Principles, the Kenoma has given
rise to its own native expressions: the Outer Gods. These are not beings
in any conventional sense. They do not think. They do not pursue goals.
They consume, mindlessly and without end, not because they choose to but
because that is what they are. They are simply incompatible with
existence. The Outer Gods cannot be reasoned with, bargained with, or
defeated in any conventional sense. They have no Bestiary entries — they
cannot be statted, detected by alignment-based magic, or fought.

**The Great Old Ones**

Great Old Ones are crystallizations of the Outer Gods — localized
expressions of Kenoma-derived pressure that have achieved a terrible
coherence within the ordered cosmos. They can be encountered, interacted
with, and in principle fought — though fighting them is rarely the right
framework for understanding the encounter. They have Bestiary entries
and Pathfinder rules function normally when dealing with them. But the
information gained through Knowledge checks, divination, or direct
contact may be incomplete, contradictory, or misleading — mortal systems
of magic, theology, and planar scholarship cannot fully explain what
they are.

**Chapter 12: Summary of Cosmic Structure**

The full structure of ordered existence — the metaverse — bounded on all
sides by the Kenoma:

- Pleroma — perfect fullness; beyond the metaverse; source and
destination of souls

- Outer Planes — alignment emanations of Free Will; eight non-neutral
forces in constant tension

- Inner Planes — elemental and energy foundations of physical reality

- Material Plane — equilibrium point of the cosmos; an infinite
multiverse of universes

- Astral Plane — cosmic medium connecting all planes within the
metaverse

- Ethereal Plane — spiritual echo overlapping the Material universes

- Shadow Plane — dark reflection of physical reality; source of shadow
magic

- Kenoma — the eternal precondition; beyond and before the metaverse;
the ground of incompleteness within which ordered existence is the
anomaly


**The Underrealm**

Beneath the surface of Eutheria lies a vast subterranean realm known as
the Underrealm. This is not a plane of existence in the cosmological
sense but a physical domain within the Material Plane — an immense
network of tunnels, caverns, and underground ecosystems extending deep
beneath the surface world. The Underrealm is the primary domain of the
Drow and the Hryngar, as well as numerous other subterranean peoples and
creatures. Surface communities in regions with known cave systems
sometimes maintain folk traditions about whether local caves connect to
the Underrealm, though such connections are rarely confirmed.

**Part III: Divine Power & Magic**

**Chapter 13: Divine Power**

Although divine magic exists in Eutheria, the universe contains no
literal gods who create or grant supernatural power. Clerical magic,
paladin abilities, and other forms of divine spellcasting derive from
the cosmic alignment forces that structure the metaverse. Religions
therefore represent cultural interpretations of metaphysical reality,
not the worship of individual divine beings.

**The Council of the Eight**

The cosmological structure described in Chapter 8 — the eight alignment
emanations of Free Will collectively known as the Council of the Eight —
is the shared metaphysical substrate underlying all divine magic in
Eutheria. Every organized religion in the world is a cultural
interpretation of this same structure. The gods are real as cosmological
presences and expressions of alignment powers, not independent creators.

Theological disagreement between religions is genuine and meaningful at
the cultural level, while the mechanical substrate remains identical
across all faiths. A cleric of the Skrosenist deity Valdrun and a cleric
of the Rocciaganel Church drawing from the same lawful good alignment
force are, cosmologically speaking, drawing from the same source. Neither
tradition is obligated to acknowledge this, and most do not. No religion
in Eutheria is objectively and demonstrably correct in its theology,
though some traditions have arrived closer to cosmological truth than
others through reason and inquiry.

**The Source of Divine Magic**

Divine magic originates from the alignment forces embodied by the Outer
Planes. Clerics draw power from these forces by dedicating themselves to
the philosophical principles associated with a particular alignment,
acting as conduits through which the power of that alignment enters the
Material Plane. Because these forces are metaphysical rather than
personal, they do not grant spells in the manner of a conscious deity.

**Religious Traditions and Organized Polytheism**

Although divine power ultimately originates from alignment forces, all
cultures interpret those forces through religious traditions. These
traditions often personify alignment principles as gods, saints,
spirits, or sacred archetypes. The metaphysical reality behind those
beliefs remains the same regardless of how it is framed. This allows
multiple religious traditions to exist for the same alignment — two
different faiths might worship entirely different divine figures while
both drawing power from the same plane.

Organized polytheism, the most common form of religious organization in
Eutheria, is a flexible category rather than a single theological model.
Different organized polytheisms may be henotheistic, full-pantheon
polytheist, civic, syncretic, or other forms. Where a tradition
acknowledges a supreme deity, that figure represents the most culturally
central or widely worshipped deity within that tradition; regional
prominence varies and this is not a universal doctrinal claim.

**Domains, True Neutral Clerics & Outsiders as Divine Agents**

Domains represent specialized aspects of alignment philosophy. Each
religious tradition determines which domains are available to its
clerics. True Neutral clerics derive their power from the equilibrium
represented by the Material Plane itself, often serving philosophies
centered on natural cycles and cosmic balance. The role of divine
servants is fulfilled by outsiders associated with the Outer Planes, who
sometimes serve as intermediaries between mortal clerics and the cosmic
powers those clerics channel. When a cleric casts a spell such as
commune, the questions are answered by a powerful outsider associated
with that alignment.

> *GM Note: Divine magic functions exactly as it does in the rules, but
> the ultimate source of that power lies in the structure of the planes
> rather than in personal deities. A cleric’s alignment must remain
> within one step of their deity’s alignment.*


**Chapter 14: The Magic System**

**The Astral Substrate**

The foundation of all magical activity is the astral substrate that
permeates the metaverse. Eutheria's universe contains a greater
concentration of astral substance within the fabric of reality than many
universes within the Material Plane — this is what allows magical forces
to interact with the physical world here with unusual ease. Spellcasters
interact with this astral substrate when they perform magical acts,
shaping the patterns of underlying metaphysical structure to produce
effects that alter matter, energy, perception, and even time.

**Arcane Magic**

Arcane magic is the direct manipulation of the astral substrate through
knowledge, instinct, or symbolic expression. Wizards rely on disciplined
study and written formulae; sorcerers access power through innate
connections to magical bloodlines; bards manipulate magical patterns
through music, language, and symbolic expression. Arcane magic is
organized into eight schools — Abjuration, Conjuration, Divination,
Enchantment, Evocation, Illusion, Necromancy, and Transmutation. The
school of Illusion draws upon the Shadow Plane, using quasi-real shadow
substance to give magical effects physical weight and partial reality.

**Innate Magic, Bloodlines & Magical Items**

Some creatures possess magical abilities that arise naturally from
biological adaptations allowing direct interaction with astral energy.
Because astral substance permeates living organisms, certain magical
traits can also be inherited as bloodlines — particularly among
sorcerers. Magical items function by storing stable patterns of astral
energy within physical objects, maintaining their properties
indefinitely unless the astral pattern within them is disrupted or
destroyed.

> *GM Note: Magic is an inherent feature of the world, not a rare or
> inexplicable phenomenon. It should be viewed as a natural extension of
> the universe's structure rather than as an external force imposed upon
> it.*

**Part IV: Major World Religions**

**Chapter 15: Skrosenism**

Skrosenism is the world’s largest organized religion, with approximately
16.5 million believers across three continents and an expansionism score
of 9.2 — the highest of any faith. It is the ancestral religion of the
Flandric people, now practiced across a wide range of cultures from the
highland kingdoms of western Nereth to the maritime Principality of
Likia and Flandric diaspora communities throughout the known world. Its
central feature, and the source of much of its durability, is the
absence of central doctrinal authority: the pantheon is universal, but
which deity is primary is determined locally, shaped by the economic
life, history, and character of each community.

**Skrosen and the Founding**

The religion takes its name from Skrosen, a minor Flandric high-king of
the pre-LC period. Within the faith, Skrosen is understood as prophet,
priest, and king simultaneously — a figure who communed directly with
the gods, learned their true names, and transmitted that knowledge as
the foundation of the organized religion. This founding narrative is the
source of Skrosenism’s theological authority: the names of the gods as
used within the tradition are held to be the names Skrosen received in
communion.

The educated and skeptical interpretation, common among Likian scholars
and some Flandric intellectuals, regards Skrosen as a historically
significant figure whose genuine temple-building activity in the Flandric
highlands has been mythologized over centuries into a divine communion
narrative. The internal theological debate over the literal truth of the
founding event is live and unresolved. No doctrinal authority exists to
settle it. In the Flandric heartland the debate ranges from devout
literalism to sophisticated positions that treat the narrative as
spiritually authoritative without requiring literal historicity. In
Likia the rationalist position predominates: the gods are real as
cosmological forces, the founding narrative is pious tradition, and the
question of Skrosen’s literal communion is considered mildly interesting
rather than existentially urgent.

> *GM Note: Skrosen was a minor Flandric high-king who constructed
> primitive temples to proto-pantheon figures. The organized religion
> bearing his name evolved from this temple-building activity over
> subsequent generations; Skrosen himself did not found Skrosenism in
> its current form. His actual seat of power lies in a buried ruin in
> what is now Laups territory, unknown to any living tradition. Garka’s
> legendary founding association is real in a general sense — the oldest
> surviving temples are there — but the precise founding geography is
> genuinely lost, and the ruin in Laups holds whatever Skrosen actually
> left behind.*

**The Pantheon**

Skrosenism is a full alignment-mapped pantheon polytheism: nine deities
correspond to the nine alignment positions, each with consistent
identity and domains across all cultural traditions that practice the
faith. The names of the deities vary across cultures — a natural
consequence of the faith spreading through peoples with different
languages — but the gods themselves are identical. Flandric names are
the original forms, deriving from Skrosen’s tradition. Likian names
evolved from Flandric originals through a thousand years of linguistic
development shaped by Kordemelian and Dukanese contact, producing
cognate forms and thematic replacements rather than foreign
reinterpretations. Westric and Lienian names follow the same pattern of
phonological descent from Flandric originals.

Good and lawful deities dominate mainstream worship across most
Skrosenist communities. Valdrun and Sigreth are the most broadly
beloved: Valdrun as the patron of oaths, kingship, and sacred bonds in
the Flandric highland tradition; Sigreth as the deity ordinary people
turn to in crisis, almost universally depicted as female, whose shrines
are present even in communities whose primary deity is another. Hrunvar
holds the primary position in maritime communities, above all in Likia.
Halmor, the full-cycle deity of nature and the dead, anchors Skrosenist
funerary tradition across all cultural variants.

Halmor’s alignment is True Neutral rather than evil. Death is the most
neutral fact in existence: universal, impartial, inevitable. Skrosenism
has always understood this. Virnath (NE), female in all traditions,
represents the perversion of the death cycle — decay, corruption, and
predatory consumption — and is the deity necromancers draw from, not
Halmor. Molgur (CE) is the genuine antagonist of the faith: not merely
deprioritized but institutionally opposed, the deity of purposeful
destruction and unmaking whose worship Skrosenist clergy actively
resist. Halmor and Molgur are the two deity names that change minimally
across all cultural traditions — too old and too weighty to drift.

**The Grey Shore**

The universal Skrosenist afterlife concept is the Grey Shore: a
transitional realm where the dead wait, tended by Halmor, until
whatever lies beyond receives them. What that beyond is, Skrosenism
does not say. It is Halmor’s knowledge alone, and mortal theology
deliberately does not reach past the Shore. The faith makes no promise
of paradise and offers no threat of damnation. It promises only that
the dead are received and tended, and that they are not simply gone.

The Flandric and Likian traditions diverge on how the Shore receives
the dead. In Flandric practice the soul must be formally given to
Halmor through the funerary rite — the living release the dead into his
custody. Without that release the soul is in unresolved tension between
the living world and the Shore. The totem tradition exists for cases
where a body is unavailable: a physical object standing in for the
deceased performs the transfer symbolically. This creates genuine
pastoral anxiety around battlefield dead, disappearances, and deaths
without witnesses. In Likian practice the soul was always Nerathos’
to begin with; the ceremony acknowledges what has already occurred
rather than enacting a transfer. This makes Likian sea-dead rites
theologically coherent without compromise — Nerathos requires no body.
Neither tradition formally condemns the other, but the underlying
disagreement is real.

Necromancy in Skrosenist theology constitutes a double transgression:
it violates Halmor’s custody of the dead and invokes Virnath’s
predatory power to animate what Halmor holds. This gives Skrosenist
opposition to undead a precise dual theological basis rather than a
vague moral one.

**Institutional Structure**

Skrosenism has no central doctrinal authority and no figure equivalent
to the Rocciaganel Church’s Pontif. The base unit of organization is
the autonomous local temple, whose senior clergy govern practice and
doctrine within their own community without reference to any external
authority. When matters of shared concern arise — theological disputes,
questions of clerical recognition, collective obligations — regional
assemblies of senior clergy convene as needed. Their decisions are
persuasive but not binding. A temple that disagrees is not compelled
to comply, though social and reputational pressure is real.

Influence flows through prestige rather than formal hierarchy. The
oldest temples, those on historically significant sites, and those that
have produced respected clergy over generations carry disproportionate
weight without holding formal power over others. Garka’s temples occupy
the apex of this prestige hierarchy: the oldest active Skrosenist
temples in the world are there, and Garkan clergy are regarded across
the Skrosenist world as the most authoritative voices on matters of
traditional practice.

**Garka and the Founding Association**

Garka is universally acknowledged as the legendary birthplace of
Skrosenism — the land where Skrosen walked, where the first temples
were raised, where the founding communion occurred. This association is
not contested by any mainstream Skrosenist community. The precise
founding geography within Garka has been lost through centuries of oral
transmission; the association is with the region, not any specific site.
The oldest active Skrosenist temples in the world stand in and around
Raudar, and clergy who travel to Garka for study or formation naturally
assume they are walking where Skrosen walked. Skrosen is understood to
have traveled widely within Flandric lands — his realm extended into
territories now belonging to neighboring Flandric states. The tradition
that he traveled beyond Flandric lands is maintained by a minority of
Garkan zealots and is not credited by mainstream Skrosenist communities.

**The Likian Denomination and the Akrosites**

Likian Skrosenism represents the faith’s most rationalist cultural form.
Mainstream Likian practice understands the gods as real cosmological
forces rather than personal beings who spoke to a priest-king; the
founding narrative is treated as pious tradition rather than literal
event; and the question of divine reality is considered mildly
interesting rather than existentially urgent. Religious practice centers
on practical domains: commerce and contracts, naval safety, weather,
healing, celestial navigation, and rites for those lost at sea.

The Akrosites are the literalist minority within Likian Skrosenist
clergy — revered as the most religiously serious practitioners in the
tradition, maintaining closer ties to the Flandric mainland than
mainstream Likian practice does. Their name derives from the ancient
tradition of siting temples on the highest point of a settlement; the
practice is now historical and the name has become an honorific
referring to the summit of religious seriousness. Akrosites refer to
the gods by their Flandric names in Likian contexts — Valdrun rather
than Phaldron, Sigreth rather than Sigreia — which mainstream Likians
find mildly anachronistic and affecting, though the underlying reverence
is genuine.

**Pilgrimage**

Skrosenism has no formal pilgrimage tradition equivalent to Ayedism’s
relationship with Tuwaid. The cultural conditions that produce major
pilgrimage institutions are largely absent from Flandric and Likian
cultures, where movement is commercial, military, or seasonal rather
than devotional. Skrosenism’s decentralized structure produces no single
sacred center toward which all communities are gravitationally oriented.
Clerical visits to Garka for study and formation, sailors’ offerings at
significant coastal temples during normal voyages, and occasional
individual lay journeys to historically significant sites are the closest
equivalents — personal and uncommon rather than institutionalized.

**Clergy and Religious Office**

Skrosenist religious leadership is organized through a system of titles
that varies by cultural tradition. In Lienian communities the hierarchy
runs from the parish **Render** through the regional **Aldrender** to
the capital council known as the **Hallmaster**, whose presiding chair
is called the **High Render**. The title of Render designates a
recognized religious office — not a character class or a guarantee of
divine spellcasting ability. Many Renders are learned, pious community
figures capable of performing the full range of religious rites without
any divine spellcasting power. Actual clerics among the Render class are
a minority, particularly in rural areas, because the adventuring life
required to gain and advance clerical levels is structurally
incompatible with the stable, community-rooted existence of institutional
religious life. A Render who is also a functional cleric carries
additional community authority as a result.

In Lienia, the Hallmaster is an ecumenical body based in Bolcescast
that manages the institutional and civic face of Skrosenism across the
kingdom — temple construction, appointment of Aldrenders, significant
clerical disputes, and the relationship between organized religion and
the crown. It is Skrosenist in character but not partisan to any single
deity within the pantheon. The crown’s influence over the institution is
exercised through a single lever: the selection of the High Render from
among the existing Hallmaster membership. Hallmaster membership itself
is by promotion from the Aldrender tier. The career path runs from
Render to Aldrender to Hallmaster to High Render.

How religious authority operates below the Aldrender level varies
considerably by region and by the character of local lords. Some counts
and earls nominate candidates for parish positions directly; others leave
the process almost entirely to community tradition and the Aldrender’s
endorsement. The reeve plays no role in religious appointment — civil
and religious authority remain structurally distinct even at the village
level.

**The Totem Entrust**

Skrosenist communities maintain a tradition known as the Totem Entrust:
at a significant life moment — most commonly at the coming-of-age
acknowledgment, sometimes at marriage — a person formally entrusts a
small personal object to their Render’s keeping. The object is something
genuinely theirs: a worn coin, a carved piece of bone, a button from a
garment, a lock of hair sealed in wax.

If that person dies with their body present, the totem is returned to
the family. If they die without witness — lost at sea, missing on the
road, fallen in battle with no body recovered — the Render uses the
totem to perform the funerary rite in absentia. The totem is the body
for purposes of the rite: not a symbol of the deceased but their
physical substitute in Halmor’s custody transfer. Whatever would be done
with the body is done with the totem instead — typically burial,
occasionally burning. The totem’s ritual status is consumed by the rite;
it is not preserved or returned afterward.

A person who has never completed the entrust and who dies without
witness creates a genuine pastoral problem for their Render, who must
improvise with a family-provided object — a workaround that carries mild
theological unease. The tradition is customary rather than formally
required, but established community members are expected to participate.

**Saints**

Skrosenism has no formal canonization process. Figures become locally
venerated through community memory and accumulated tradition rather than
institutional declaration. A locally remarkable person — particularly
devoted, dramatically memorable in their death, or associated with a
significant place — retains their name in the landscape and eventually
in religious practice. Such figures are called saints, though the title
carries no formal theological weight. A saint in one district may be
entirely unknown elsewhere. The Hallmaster neither confirms nor denies
local saints; they fall outside institutional scope. Renders incorporate
local saints into parish life without any formal sanction being required.


**Part V: Peoples of Eutheria**

**Chapter 16: Common Origins**

The peoples of Eutheria share a common origin: all humanoid races
descend from a single ancient lineage that diversified over long ages
into the distinct species found today. Elves, dwarves, gnomes, and
halflings represent the oldest branches of this family. Humans and orcs
emerged later from an elven ancestral population. Half-elves and
half-orcs arise from interbreeding between closely related branches of
the lineage.

**Chapter 17: Humans**

Humans branched from an ancient elven lineage relatively late in the
history of humanoid life. Compared to elves and dwarves, they are
short-lived, reproduce quickly, and adapt rapidly to new environments.
These traits make human populations highly dynamic — capable of rapid
growth, expansion, and cultural change in ways that longer-lived peoples
rarely match. Humans are the most numerous and geographically widespread
humanoid people in the world, found on Nereth, Kasmora, and Valthera in
significant numbers. Golsta has no significant human population.

**Chapter 18: Elves**

Elves are one of the oldest surviving humanoid species in the world,
descending from the most ancient branch of the humanoid lineage and
ancestral to humans and orcs. Elves possess exceptionally long lifespans
often lasting several centuries, keen senses, natural grace and agility,
and a strong affinity for magic and the natural world. Their numbers are
far lower than those of humans owing to extremely slow reproductive
rates and a preference for low-density wilderness environments — ancient
forests, mountainous wilderness regions, remote valleys. Elven culture
emphasizes continuity of tradition, preservation of knowledge, and
long-term stewardship of the land. Due to their close evolutionary
relationship with humans, elves can produce half-elf offspring with
them. Elves cannot interbreed with orcs.

**Chapter 19: Dwarves**

Dwarves adapted to life in mountainous environments over long ages,
developing cultures centered on fortified strongholds, clan loyalty, and
mastery of stone and metal. They are physically robust, long-lived
relative to humans, and known for exceptional craftsmanship and
engineering. The greatest dwarven populations are found in the mountain
ranges of Valthera and Nereth. Dwarves construct vast underground cities
behind fortified surface citadels, supporting large and stable
populations over long periods. Trade with neighboring peoples supplies
agricultural products that dwarven lands cannot easily produce, creating
strong commercial ties.

**Dwarves of Valthera** — Known for particularly rigid and disciplined
traditions. Repeated contact with the Hryngar has made these communities
more suspicious of outsiders, emphasizing vigilance, strong defenses,
and strict adherence to tradition. The intimate proximity of Valtheran
Dwarves and Cheoni communities has had significant religious
consequences — the Tongj Apostates faith emerged from this contact.

**Dwarves of Nereth** — Occupy a strategic position controlling the
great mountain passes of the continent. This location has fostered
extensive trade networks and commercial wealth, making Nerethan dwarves
more accustomed to diplomacy and long-distance commerce. They are the
indispensable intermediaries for all trans-mountain trade and travel
across Nereth, growing increasingly wealthy since the disruption of the
southern Likian trade route.

**Chapter 20: Gnomes**

Gnomes are close relatives of elves within the ancient humanoid lineage,
retaining a strong affinity for magic and the natural world while
displaying a tendency toward curiosity, improvisation, and
experimentation that contrasts sharply with elven patience and
restraint. Rather than maintaining large territories of their own,
gnomes tend to settle among other peoples — especially in cities, trade
towns, and ports — forming urban neighborhoods within larger settlements
rather than independent states.

**Chapter 21: Halflings**

Halflings descend from the ancient humanoid lineage, small humanoids
known for agility, resilience, and strong communal instincts. They
prefer fertile and stable environments — agricultural regions, rural
valleys, and temperate countryside near larger civilizations. Halflings
strongly prefer forming their own communities rather than living
permanently within settlements dominated by other races, maintaining
independent villages near but separate from human settlements.

**Chapter 22: Orcs**

Orcs diverged from an ancient elven ancestral population, developing
toward greater physical power, aggression, and a social structure built
around strength and dominance. Orc society is organized around clans led
by the most powerful individuals, largely nomadic or semi-nomadic,
ranging across frontier regions, mountain territories, and wilderness
areas. Orcs were among the Deep Folk who waged the Thousand-Year War
against the surface civilizations. Following the Free Folk's victory at
the Battle of Trelhelm, orc populations did not withdraw permanently
underground as the Drow and Hryngar did, instead remaining on or near
the surface in frontier regions adjacent to humanoid civilization.

**Chapter 23: Half-Orcs**

Half-orcs are the hybrid offspring of humans and orcs, possessing
exceptional physical strength, endurance, and imposing physical
presence. Most are produced through orc raiding practices — captured
humans used in breeding programs designed to produce powerful hybrid
warriors, raised among orc clans and trained from an early age for
warfare. Within orc cultures, half-orcs occupy an ambiguous social
position — officially often regarded as property, but valuable for their
combat effectiveness, sometimes rising to positions of influence through
personal strength or battlefield success.

**Chapter 24: Drow**

The Drow are an ancient humanoid people who dwell almost entirely within
the vast subterranean realms beneath the surface world, sharing distant
ancestry with surface elves but adapted to lightless underground
conditions over millennia. Drow society is deeply stratified and highly
competitive, concentrated in large underground cities with architectural
and magical traditions stretching back thousands of years. The Drow were
among the Deep Folk who waged the Thousand-Year War. Following the Free
Folk's decisive victory at the Battle of Trelhelm, the Drow withdrew
fully into the depths. Their relationship with the surface world is one
of mutual avoidance punctuated by occasional trade in rare underground
materials or conflict in borderland regions.

**Chapter 25: Hryngar (Duergar)**

The Hryngar — also known as Duergar — are the subterranean counterpart
to surface dwarves, sharing common ancestry but diverging dramatically
over thousands of years of separate development in the deep underground.
Their culture is austere, deeply hierarchical, and intensely suspicious
of outsiders, organized around labor, strict discipline, and the
authority of ruling castes. Dwarves of Valthera in particular, whose
mountain strongholds border Hryngar-controlled deep regions, have a long
history of conflict and uneasy border management with their subterranean
kin. Full reconciliation between the two peoples has never been
achieved.

**Part VI: Creature Origins**

**Chapter 26: Overview**

The diversity of creatures found throughout Eutheria arises from four
broad categories: natural evolution within the Material Plane;
manifestation from planar forces; transformation through supernatural
energy; or deliberate artificial creation. Eutheria's universe contains
a greater concentration of astral substance within the fabric of reality
than many universes within the Material Plane, allowing magical forces
to interact with the physical world with unusual ease and producing a
wide range of magical creatures through natural selection over millions
of years.

**Chapter 27: Dragons**

Among all naturally evolved magical organisms, dragons represent the
most advanced and powerful lineage — and the oldest. Dragons are
believed to descend from a single ancestral lineage of linnorm-like
proto-dragons. Through sustained exposure to astral energies, these
ancient reptiles developed powerful innate magic, intelligence,
sentience, and a limited form of free will. Dragons were the first
mortal creatures in existence. Unlike most mortal species, each dragon
is innately bound to a specific alignment, and because dragon souls are
so perfectly aligned at death, they always undergo transfiguration —
when a dragon dies, its soul is drawn to the corresponding Outer Plane
and transfigures into a native outsider. Dragons are therefore believed
to be the first mortals whose souls became outsiders, and the original
source of the outsider lineages that now populate the Outer Planes.

**Dragon Lineages**

**Chromatic Dragons** — The most common evil-aligned species. Principal
species: black, blue, green, red, and white.

**Metallic Dragons** — The major good-aligned species. Principal
species: brass, bronze, copper, gold, and silver.

**Imperial Dragons** — A separate evolutionary branch associated with
cosmic forces. Believed to be extinct or nearly so in the current era.

**Primal Dragons** — Not native to the Material Plane. Extremely rare,
usually appearing only through summoning or planar magic.

Modern kobolds and lizardfolk are not descended from dragons. Despite
their reptilian appearance and persistent cultural myths claiming
draconic ancestry, both peoples arose from magical unions between
reptiles and humanoids long after the Draconic Age. The largest
surviving dragon populations inhabit Golsta, where vast wilderness and
extreme climates limit the spread of humanoid societies.

**Chapter 28: Other Creature Types**

**Magical Beasts** — Evolutionary branches in which animals developed
limited magical capabilities through interaction with astral energy.

**Fey** — Arise in environments where the boundary between the Material
Plane and the Ethereal Plane becomes unusually thin, closely tied to
natural environments saturated with magical energy.

**Aberrations** — Creatures whose biology has been altered by unstable
interactions with magical or planar forces.

**Elementals** — Originate from the Inner Planes, composed primarily of
elemental substance rather than biological tissue.

**Outsiders** — Originate from the Outer Planes, arising through the
transfiguration of mortal souls. Dragons were the first mortals whose
souls underwent this process.

**Undead** — Arise when negative energy from the Negative Energy Plane
animates or corrupts a creature after death.

**Constructs** — Artificial beings deliberately created through magic
and craftsmanship.

**Giants** — Share a common proto-humanoid ancestor with the smaller
humanoid races, diverging toward extreme physical size. Once the
dominant civilization after the Draconic Age, devastated in the
Dragon-Giant Wars. Modern giants are the remnants of this once-dominant
lineage.

**Part VII: History**

**Chapter 29: The Pre-Humanoid World**

**The Age of Dragons**

Long before humanoid species arose, dragons dominated the world.
Descended from a single ancestral lineage of linnorm-like proto-dragons,
these were the first intelligent beings on the Material Plane. They
shaped the early ecological and magical environment of the world across
vast stretches of time. Their souls, being perfectly alignment-bound,
transfigured upon death into outsiders — making dragons the original
source of the outsider lineages that populate the Outer Planes.

**Reptilian Civilizations of the Draconic Age**

During and following the Age of Dragons, ancient reptilian societies
arose across the world — composed of reptilian creatures, draconic
servants, and other non-humanoid species. They built large temple
complexes, ceremonial plazas, and ritual sites treating dragons as
divine or ancestral beings. Their ruins survive primarily in tropical
and equatorial regions, which were less affected by later glaciation.
Modern kobolds and lizardfolk are not descended from these ancient
civilizations.

**The Proto-Humanoid Divergence**

At some point following the Draconic Age, a proto-humanoid ancestor
species evolved. Two major branches diverged: one toward extreme
physical size, producing the giants; the other toward smaller bodies,
faster reproduction, and greater dietary flexibility, producing the
earliest elves. From the elven lineage, dwarves, gnomes, and halflings
later diverged as distinct species. Humans and orcs represent still
later branches that split from an elven ancestral population.

**The Age of Giants & The Dragon-Giant Wars**

Following the Draconic Age, giants rose to become the dominant
intelligent civilization, constructing enormous monumental structures
across the world — cyclopean fortresses, colossal monuments, and great
stone roads that are among the oldest surviving physical artifacts.
Competition for territory between dragons and giants eventually
escalated into catastrophic conflict. The Dragon-Giant Wars devastated
both civilizations, creating a massive power vacuum that allowed the
smaller humanoid peoples — the Free Folk — to expand across the
continents. Elves rose to particular prominence in this early period,
their long lifespans and advanced magical traditions making them the
dominant surface civilization.

**Chapter 30: The Great Catastrophe — 9034 BLC**

**The End of the Ice Age**

For tens of thousands of years prior, large portions of the northern
world were dominated by glaciers and extreme climates. The Thousand-Year
War itself was fought across this frozen world — populations on all
sides already compressed into narrower habitable zones by the ice,
competing for constrained and contested territory. This may have
contributed to the war's extraordinary length and ferocity. At 9034 BLC,
the Battle of Trelhelm brought the war to its decisive conclusion. By
coincidence of timing, this was also the moment the Great Withdrawal
began — the long period of glacial retreat during which ice sheets
slowly retreated poleward over the following centuries. Glaciers
receded. Sea levels rose as meltwater filled the oceans. River systems
expanded across newly exposed landscapes. Great forests spread into
lands previously barren. These environmental changes opened enormous new
territories for settlement, transforming the world's geography across
the very generations that were simultaneously recovering from the most
catastrophic conflict in humanoid history. The exhausted victors of
Trelhelm emerged into a world that was physically growing around them.

**The Deep Folk Crisis & The Thousand-Year War**

At the same time the Ice Age was ending, a crisis had been building
beneath the surface of the world. In the deepest regions of the
underworld, pressures of unknown origin had begun to destabilize the
subterranean societies of the Drow and the Hryngar. Both peoples found
themselves increasingly unable to sustain their civilizations within the
deep earth alone. The Drow and the Hryngar formed a military alliance
with powerful orc tribes — the Dark Folk armies — launching large-scale
invasions against the civilizations of the surface world. The resulting
conflict drew in nearly every major civilization of the time and lasted
approximately one thousand years. Many ancient cities were destroyed,
entire cultures collapsed, and at several points the Free Folk came
dangerously close to complete defeat.

The causes of these deep pressures remain unknown in ordinary
historiography. Surviving records speak only of mounting strain in the
underworld and of surface peoples suddenly facing coordinated invasions
from enemies that seemed driven by more than territorial ambition.

**General TarKath & The Battle of Trelhelm**

In the later stages of the war, the Dark Folk alliance came under the
leadership of a powerful orc warlord known as General TarKath, who
unified the disparate Dark Folk forces and pushed the surface
civilizations to the brink of collapse. The war culminated in the
decisive Battle of Trelhelm, fought in the southern mountains of
Valthera at what would later be calculated as 9034 BLC. During this
battle the armies of the Free Folk finally halted TarKath's forces.
TarKath himself was defeated and the Dark Folk army was routed. The
defeat shattered the invading coalition. Orc power collapsed. The
surviving forces of the Drow and Hryngar withdrew back into the deep
underworld. The victory at Trelhelm marked the end of the Thousand-Year
War and preserved the civilizations of the Free Folk.

**The Dispersal of Peoples**

Following the victory at Trelhelm, as the Great Withdrawal slowly
transformed the world's geography around them, each of the major peoples
withdrew into regions where they could rebuild their shattered
societies. The elves retreated into the great forests, establishing
enduring forest realms focused on cultural preservation. The dwarves
returned to their ancestral mountain realms and rebuilt their
subterranean cities. Humans expanded rapidly across the newly habitable
lands opened by the retreating glaciers, spreading widely across plains,
river valleys, and coastal regions. The Dark Folk — Drow, Hryngar, and
the fragmented orc tribes — withdrew into the depths of the earth or
retreated to frontier wilderness regions.

Much knowledge from the war was lost in the collapse of ancient cities,
the fragmentation of postwar archives, and the long dark age that
followed. Later ages inherited a victory whose deepest causes were only
partially remembered.

**Chapter 31: The Archaeological Landscape**

The world contains several distinct layers of ancient ruins. The oldest
are relics of the Draconic Age itself. Above these lie the ruins of the
ancient reptilian civilizations that worshipped dragons, concentrated in
equatorial and tropical regions. Rarer still, and often the most
physically imposing, are the megastructures of the Age of Giants.
Finally, layered atop all of these are the many strata of humanoid ruins
from post-glacial periods. The distribution of ruins is shaped
significantly by glaciation — northern ruins tend to be younger, while
the truly ancient sites are in the south and the tropics.
Dragon-associated sites are the rarest of all.

**Chapter 32: The Geological Timeline**

**Deep Geological History**

The planet formed through the accretion of rocky material,
differentiating over immense time into a metallic core, a silicate
mantle, and a primitive crust. Repeated cycles of tectonic activity
built and destroyed continental landmasses, eventually assembling them
into a single great supercontinent. One particularly significant
tectonic episode produced a mountain belt known as the Great Polar
Orogeny, whose legacy persists in the elevated, cold-facing sectors of
the two northern continents. The supercontinent eventually broke apart —
Nereth, Valthera, Kasmora, and Golsta are the principal fragments of
this breakup. The island chains between the northern continents were
produced by volcanic arc activity associated with subduction and may
still be tectonically active.

**The Last Great Ice Age & The Great Withdrawal**

In the geologically recent past, the planet entered a major glacial
phase. At its height, the northern continents were much more heavily
glaciated than today, sea level was lower, and Golsta carried more
extensive ice cover. Roughly ten thousand years ago — coinciding with
the Battle of Trelhelm — the Great Withdrawal began: the long period of
glacial retreat during which large ice sheets retreated poleward,
causing rapid meltwater discharge, isostatic rebound in formerly
ice-loaded northern crust, and northward migration of forests. This
process unfolded over centuries, gradually transforming the world's
geography. Residual ice persists across the far north of both northern
continents, in high northern mountains, and across Golsta's cold sector
— the Great Withdrawal continues in diminished form to the present day.
The modern planet is in a mixed tectonic state: continental interiors
largely stable, old mountain systems surviving as eroded uplands, and at
least one active convergent margin existing between the two northern
continents.

**Chapter 33: Post-Trelhelm History — The Road to the Present**

**The Recovery Period — 9034 to ~2000 BLC**

The centuries following Trelhelm were marked by profound devastation on
Nereth and Valthera — dark ages of scattered subsistence communities,
oral tradition, and minimal population. Kasmora recovered faster. Its
more equatorial position had made it less affected by the Ice Age, and
agricultural knowledge there had never fully died out, merely
contracted. This continuous thread of farming knowledge provided Kasmora
with a head start in recovery that the northern continents could not
match. Small agricultural settlements persisting through the war and the
glacial period began growing again, gradually developing into towns and
then small kingdoms along Kasmoran river systems. Nereth and Valthera
followed more slowly, tribal consolidations eventually giving rise to
small kingdoms around the most productive river valleys.

**The Rise of Civilization — ~2000 to ~624 BLC**

Population growth and improved agriculture eventually allowed stable
kingdoms to emerge across all three inhabited continents. In eastern
Nereth, the Veltric culture emerged as the dominant power in its
region through a combination of agricultural productivity, geographic
position, and organizational sophistication. Over centuries of
dominance, the Veltric polity gradually formalized into a republic
— a sophisticated governing institution that would eventually become the
foundation for the greatest empire Nereth had seen. On Kasmora, the
pattern of fragmented river kingdoms, nomadic confederacies, and coastal
trading states solidified into the continental mosaic that persists to
the present day. No Kasmoran power ever achieved continental dominance.
On Valthera, geographic fragmentation produced by lake-and-mountain
terrain and the lasting trauma of Trelhelm's final phase prevented any
single power from dominating the continent.

**The Veltric Republic — ~624 to ~124 BLC**

Around 624 BLC, the Veltric civilization of eastern Nereth
formalized into the Veltric Republic — a sophisticated republican
institution governing an already dominant regional power. Over the
following five centuries, the Republic expanded its influence across
eastern Nereth, developing administrative systems, legal codes, road
networks, and commercial infrastructure that would later form the
backbone of the Empire. The Republic never crossed the central mountain
range — the Dwarven realms controlling the passes acted as a natural and
deliberate barrier, trading with both sides while allowing neither to
dominate the other. Western Nereth knew the Republic existed, traded
with it through Dwarven intermediaries, and was culturally influenced at
a distance, but was never absorbed into it.

**The Veltric Empire — ~124 BLC to ~876 LC**

Around 124 BLC, the Veltric Republic underwent its defining
transformation: the coronation of the first Emperor. The Republic became
the Veltric Empire, and with this transition Eutheria's most significant
land power was formally constituted. At its height the Empire spanned
the entirety of eastern Nereth — Veltric and Monzian cultures
forming the heartland, Olmagan culture closely integrated as a
frontier province, and Alaian cultures peripherally
influenced but never fully conquered, protected by the dense jungle
terrain of the southwestern coast.

The Empire's relationship with Likia, which later rose on Kasmora's
northeastern coast, was distant and commercial rather than political or
military. Likian goods reached Imperial markets through a long chain of
intermediaries: Likian ships sailed to Alaian southwestern ports,
and from there goods chained eastward along the southern Nerethan coast
through Imperial merchant channels. The Empire and Likia never dealt
directly and never found themselves in significant conflict — they were
separated by an entire continent and an ocean, connected only by
commerce.

The Empire lasted approximately one thousand years. Its zenith was
marked by administrative coherence, road and river infrastructure
enabling efficient commerce, and a stable institutional framework that
governed the lives of tens of millions across eastern Nereth. Over time,
however, the patrician and merchant classes grew increasingly resentful
of Imperial taxation and administrative overreach. Religious tensions
between the old Veltric traditions and the Empire's official faith
contributed to the growing discontent. By its final centuries the Empire
was hollowing out from within — its institutions nominally intact, its
underlying authority steadily eroding.

**The Veltric Collapse — ~876 LC to Present**

Around 876 LC, the Emperor died in Marcici without an heir. The
succession crisis that followed proved to be the killing blow for an
institution already weakened by centuries of internal resentment.
Marcici — in what is now Polbium — had long served as the joint seat of
both Emperor and Pontif, the political and ecclesiastical heart of the
Empire. In the immediate aftermath of the Emperor's death, the Church in
Marcici declared itself the de facto governing authority of the Empire
until lawful continuity could be restored. Many provincial governors,
who had long held the real administrative power in their territories,
viewed this as ecclesiastical usurpation and refused obedience. What
followed was not a single war but a widening fracture: rival claimants,
provincial coalitions, and Church-backed assertions of legitimacy all
collided at once. The fighting was brutal but inconclusive — no single
governor proved capable of reunifying the empire by force, and no
coalition proved stable enough to govern jointly. The Veltric Empire
ceased to exist as a political entity. No successor state retained the
Veltric name.

The collapse is historically recent rather than ancient. For humans it
belongs to remembered history rather than living memory; for elves,
dwarves, and other long-lived peoples it remains within personal or near
personal recollection. Imperial roads, administrative systems, and legal
traditions remain in use not because the Empire survives, but because
its replacement has been partial, uneven, and incomplete. The question
of Imperial restoration is still politically live. Some successor states
trace their legitimacy through Veltric heritage. Others have explicitly
rejected that heritage as part of asserting their independence. The
ruins of Imperial infrastructure are everywhere in eastern Nereth, daily
reminders of what was lost.

**The Immediate Collapse — 876 to ~900 LC**

The first generation after the Emperor's death was defined by civil war,
contested succession, and the breakdown of centralized legitimacy.
Administrative provinces hardened into military zones. Local governors,
claimants, and ecclesiastical authorities attempted to preserve order
through emergency measures that increasingly became permanent. The
Church's claim to govern from Marcici until lawful continuity could be
restored convinced many provincial elites that the old imperial center
could no longer distinguish guardianship from domination.

**The Wars of Settlement — ~900 to ~950 LC**

As no claimant succeeded in reunifying the Empire, the wars shifted from
succession struggles into settlement struggles. Borders hardened,
successor states emerged, and local regimes began treating temporary
command structures as the basis of lasting sovereignty. The Church's
institutional authority survived, but its attempt to act as the Empire's
de facto governing body left a deep political stain; from this point
forward, questions of ecclesiastical legitimacy and post-Imperial rule
became nearly inseparable.

**The False Stabilization — ~950 to ~1000 LC**

The largest wars gradually burned out. Trade rerouted rather than
vanished. Some successor states developed durable dynasties, regional
identities, and working administrative systems of their own. This period
created the impression that the post-Veltric world had largely settled
into a new normal, even if imperial restoration remained a live ideal in
certain courts and religious circles.

**The Deepening Fracture — ~1000 to 1026 LC**

By the early eleventh century LC, that apparent stabilization had begun
to fail. Political orders weakened again, religious conflicts sharpened,
and commercial routes grew more contested. The present age is therefore
understood not as the immediate aftermath of collapse, but as a later
and deeper phase of the same unraveling.

**Chapter 35: The Religious Landscape of Eastern Nereth**

The religious history of eastern Nereth parallels its political history.
Five major traditions are present in the region, representing different
peoples, eras, and theological inheritances.

**Old Veltric Beliefs** — The most ancient tradition, a folk animist
practice predating the Republic. Never fully displaced by later
developments, always practiced openly in the Veltric heartland even during
the Empire's height. Currently experiencing a renaissance as Imperial
authority that had elevated later traditions has collapsed.

**Veltric Beliefs** — The organized polytheist tradition of the Republican
era, derived from the older animist folk beliefs. The state religion of
the Republic, still practiced widely throughout the Veltric cultural
sphere. Also experiencing renewed popularity since the Imperial
collapse. Expansionism score of 10 — the highest of any organized
religion on Nereth — reflects its current resurgence.

**The Rocciaganel Church** — The organized monotheist tradition that
became the official state religion of the Veltric Empire over time,
derived from Monzian Beliefs. The Church’s monotheism distinguishes it
sharply from both the polytheist Old Veltric Beliefs and Veltric Beliefs
traditions that are resurging in the successor states — a theological
fault line that sharpens the political conflict. The Church’s
institutional framework survived the Empire’s political collapse, but
its authority is eroding as the older polytheist traditions resurge. The
Church’s association with Imperial power now works against it in
communities that welcomed the Empire’s end. Formally, the Church remains
ordered around a single primatial office — often rendered in modern
scholarship as the Pontif or supreme cleric. In the Imperial era, both
Emperor and Pontif ruled from Marcici, in the Polbium heartland. After
the Emperor's death in 876 LC, the Church in Marcici declared itself the
de facto governing authority of the Empire until lawful succession could
be restored. Many provinces regarded this as usurpation, and that
reaction did much to turn succession crisis into lasting fracture.

**Altavism** — The dominant organized polytheist faith of the Alaian
cultural world, with approximately 10.4 million believers. Though
deriving from the same Monzian folk tradition as the Rocciaganel Church,
Altavism evolved as organized polytheism rather than monotheism —
preserving the full polytheist structure where the Church collapsed it
into a single ancestral divine figure. Altavism is primarily practiced
by Alaian-culture communities in southern Nereth, with secondary
presence in Olmagan and Monzian communities. The presence of ten
Altavist settlements within the Polbium Theocracy — the Rocciaganel
Church’s own heartland — is one of the most significant sources of
internal religious tension in the region. The Almaquegam Church, a
state-religion offshoot of Altavism confined to the state of Tazania,
represents the tradition’s only formally institutionalized state variant.

**Sinzism** — The organized polytheist faith of the Sinzian cultural
world, evolved gradually from older Sinzian Druids folk practice over an
unspecified but lengthy pre-republican period. It is culture-bounded
rather than missionary: Sinzian identity and Sinzist religion remain
fused, and the faith belongs to the Sinzian people rather than to the
Republic of Sinz as a state. Sinzism is therefore the religion of the
Sinzian cultural core only, not a republic-wide or official state
religion across all of Sinz’s composite population. Its older
land-and-cycle root is associated with Alburg, while Dachsbad is the
deity most associated with organized public Sinzism — especially oath,
law, exchange, and communal order — without requiring a universally
accepted supreme-god theology. Sinzism keeps a shared sacred year across
Sinzian communities, but rural communities place their strongest
emphasis on seasonal and harvest observance while urban communities give
greater weight to oaths, contracts, market order, and civic
reconciliation. Institutionally it is non-hierarchical: authority is
local, customary, and reputational, exercised through learned
custodians of sacred places and communal rites rather than through a
centralized church or binding synod. This gives Sinzism a persistent
tension with the oligarchic, commercially competitive civic culture of
the republic, even though the faith remains one of the defining markers
of Sinzian identity.

**The Polbium Theocracy**

Among the Veltric successor states, the Polbium Theocracy occupies a
unique position. It is the largest successor state by area and claims to
be the religious inheritor of the Rocciaganel Church — the keeper of its
institutional authority and doctrinal legitimacy. Because Marcici lies
within Polbium and had served as the joint seat of both Emperor and
Pontif, the Theocracy presents itself not as a novelty but as the last
lawful continuation of the old imperial core. The primatial office of
the Church therefore sits inside a state that claims both sacred and
post-Imperial legitimacy, making questions of ecclesiastical recognition
and political allegiance nearly inseparable. This claim gives the
Theocracy a form of soft power extending beyond its territorial borders:
it can delegitimize rival rulers, invoke ecclesiastical authority across
all former Imperial territories where the Church has adherents, and
mobilize religious sentiment across political boundaries.

However, the Polbium Theocracy is fighting a difficult rearguard action.
The religious renaissance of the older Veltric traditions is strongest in
the very heartland territories where the Church should be most secure.
The Theocracy's power is real but contested — an institution whose
authority was built on Imperial backing now forced to stand on its own
without that backing, while the traditions it displaced are visibly
regaining ground.

**Chapter 36: Likia & The Valtheran Naval Conflict**

**Likia's Rise — 0 to ~300 LC**

Likia was founded at 0 LC on the northeastern coast of Kasmora,
separated from the agricultural interior by the northern mountain range.
Turning to the sea out of necessity, Likia gradually expanded its
control across the central island chain of Oceanus Centralis,
establishing ports and naval bases that gave it command of the principal
maritime routes connecting all three inhabited continents. By the early
centuries LC, Likia had become the dominant maritime power of the known
world, its calendar the international standard.

**The Kingdom of Xin Guo & The Maritime Rivalry — ~300 to ~750 LC**

As Valthera's Tengcian naval states developed their maritime
capabilities and pushed outward into Oceanus Centralis, conflict with
Likia became inevitable. The dominant Valtheran naval power in this
struggle was the Kingdom of Xin Guo — a theocratic naval state whose
religious identity was inseparable from its maritime ambition. The
Kingdom challenged Likia for dominance of the Central Ocean in a rivalry
that lasted approximately four centuries. It is not a single
uninterrupted war, but a long strategic contest that began with
Valtheran expansion into Oceanus Centralis around 200–300 LC and was
thereafter marked by intermittent naval campaigns, trade embargoes,
proxy conflicts over island territories, piracy and privateering, and
shifting periods of uneasy truce. At several points the rivalry
threatened Likia's dominance, but Likia's strategic position astride the
central strait ultimately proved decisive.

**The Settlement & The See of Xin Guo — ~750 to ~800 LC**

Around 750-800 LC, after the final major war of this long rivalry, Likia
decisively defeated the Kingdom of Xin Guo. The peace settlement imposed
specific terms designed to permanently end Xin Guo's naval threat: the
Kingdom was dissolved and reconstituted as the See of Xin Guo — a
religiously governed state explicitly prohibited from maintaining a
navy. The transformation was not voluntary. It was a deliberate act of
political dismemberment, stripping Xin Guo not just of its fleet but of
the institutional right to ever have one again.

The See of Xin Guo that exists at 1026 LC is the rump of this once-great
power — tiny, legally prohibited from naval activity, governed by
religious authorities whose state exists as a reminder of defeat. The
See channels its frustrated institutional ambition into aggressive
proselytization of the Xinchang Deities faith — the one form of
expansion the truce cannot prohibit. Its expansionism score of 49.1, the
highest of any state in the world, reflects this frustrated ambition
rather than any military or territorial capacity. The Xinchang Deities
faith itself has modest expansionism — the See is using the faith as a
political instrument in ways that the broader community of the faith's
adherents may not entirely welcome.

**The Cold Peace — ~800 LC to Present**

Following the settlement with Xin Guo, Likia consolidated control of the
central strait and granted trade access to Valtheran states under Likian
terms. The cold peace that followed has held for approximately two
centuries — no open hostilities, but persistent tension. The other
Valtheran powers drew clear conclusions from what happened to Xin Guo.
Challenging Likia directly was not a viable strategy. Economic
integration under Likian terms, however uncomfortable, was the pragmatic
choice.

**Chapter 37: The Present Day — 1026 LC**

**A World in Transition**

The world of 1026 LC is defined by a power transition that no one fully
understands. The Veltric Empire provided an organizing principle for
nearly half the world's population for a thousand years. Its collapse
150 years ago did not simply fragment eastern Nereth — it disrupted the
trade networks connecting all three continents, elevated previously
peripheral states, and left an enormous demographic and institutional
vacuum that no single successor has filled.

Yet the present is not the immediate aftermath of collapse. The first
post-Imperial wars have long since burned out, and a rough successor
order has had time to emerge. What makes 1026 LC volatile is that this
second order is now beginning to fail in its turn. The current world is
therefore understood as entering a deeper phase of the Age of Fracture
rather than merely enduring the Empire's initial fall.

> *GM Note: The present world is weaker in apex spiritual power than the
> ancient world that fought the Thousand-Year War. Truly top-tier
> clerics are vanishingly rare. An uncompromised Lawful Good cleric of
> 18th level may not currently exist; if one does, there is likely only
> one. A simultaneous full awakening of multiple Great Old Ones would
> therefore exceed the response capacity of mortal institutions alone and
> likely force intervention from dragons, powerful outsiders, and other
> ancient defenders of ordered existence.*

**Nereth**

Nereth is the most demographically significant continent with
approximately 46 million people — nearly half the world's total
population of 98 million — and 470 urban settlements, more than Valthera
and Kasmora combined. This urban infrastructure is the legacy of the
Veltric Empire, still functioning even though the political structure
that built it is gone. Yet Nereth is politically fragmented at exactly
the moment its advantages should matter most.

Western Nereth is rising. The old southern coastal chain eastward
never fully recovered after the Veltric collapse, and Nereth’s hot
desert interior makes long-distance continental movement depend heavily
on the western habitable fringe and the mountain approaches. The new
overland and river trade route — Likian goods landing at Lienia’s ports
and moving east toward the Dwarven passes — has therefore elevated
previously peripheral kingdoms into commercial significance. The
Republic of Sinz and the Kingdom of Lienia are the dominant western
powers, powerful in different ways: Lienia through population, urban
density, articulated coastline, and its direct Likian commercial
relationship; Sinz through corridor control, customs pressure, and its
position on the landward approach to the Khundian and Dwarven pass
systems. Sinz is no longer best understood as a meaningfully maritime
state. Its one surviving port is vestigial; its real significance is as
the harder inland broker within a western transit system that
long-distance movement cannot easily bypass. Western kingdoms share deep
cultural ties and manage their frequent disputes without existential
conflict.

The old western kingdom that once joined the core territories of later
Sinz and Fresen did not survive the age of post-Veltric realignment.
Weakened by repeated rivalry with Lienia, mounting fracture in its own
Alaian south, and the wider instability of the Wars of Settlement, that
monarchy dissolved in 934 LC. The republic that replaced it was not a
purely Sinzian civic creation imposed on a passive south: Alaian
cooperation or acquiescence was part of the price of making the new
order stick, even though the resulting institutions remained decisively
patrician and Sinzian-led. Its larger inland body reorganized as the
Republic of Sinz, while the ruling house retained only its western
coastal personal demesne, which endured as the Grand Duchy of Fresen.
North of Lienia, the older Grand Duchy of Bramia remained a distinct
Lienian coastal-transfer state whose importance rested on a separate
river-mouth and lake-chain trade system rather than on dynastic
connection to the Lienian crown.

Within this western order, Lienia is understood as a broad maritime
monarchy rather than a single-port state. Bolcescast, at the estuarial
throat of the kingdom's capital basin, functions as the principal
customs and administrative hinge of the realm. Yet Lienia's real
strength lies in its long articulated coastline, secondary ports, and
broad inland body. Its political life is therefore shaped not only by
trade with Likia but by the problem of integrating several different
regional faces of the kingdom into one durable crown order.

Eastern Nereth is unsettled. The successor states of the Veltric Empire
are old enough to possess their own dynasties, institutions, and local
patriotisms, yet young enough to remain haunted by Imperial precedent.
Religious tensions between the Polbium Theocracy's Rocciaganel Church
and the resurging older Veltric traditions layer on top of territorial
disputes and the disruption of commercial networks. The coastal southern
route for Likian goods has not been fully reconstituted, leaving eastern
states dependent on the overland route through the mountains. The result
is not simple chaos but an uneasy, aging post-Imperial order whose
underlying legitimacy is beginning to fray again.

**Valthera**

Valthera remains fragmented, with no dominant power. The cold peace with
Likia has held for two centuries, and Valtheran states trade through
Likian-controlled waters under Likian terms. The Republic of Han Guo is
notable among Valtheran powers — a Tengcian but deeply integrated
Tengcian–Cheoni republic with a Cheoni capital, a southern heartland,
northwestern resource extraction outposts, and a formal religious duty
to protect Quz Guo, the sovereign sacred state centered on Shang,
birthplace of Taizhouist philosophy and religion. Guan Guo controls the
best lower-river outlet on a commercially important western bay, while
Longlin Guo dominates much of the upstream middle course and remains
acutely sensitive to downstream dependence. Cheonguk is the largest
Cheoni kingdom in the west: coastal, self-assured, and politically
stable despite ruling older Dedeokan heartlands alongside more recently
annexed Tongj territories. The Hayanguk Theocracy remains the principal
inland buffer state of the western frontier, governing through the Tongj
Apostates faith — a Cheoni reinterpretation of Dwarven religious
tradition blended with elements of the Witnesses of Dedeok. The See of
Xin Guo persists as a rump state nursing its grievances and channeling
them into religious proselytization.

**Kasmora**

Kasmora is the world's quietly emerging dominant. Its states are the
largest by population — Kordemeli (~6.8M), Dukan (~6.6M), Sala (~6.4M),
Haria (~5.3M) — sitting on the world's most agriculturally productive
continent, several with significant expansionism scores. The Veltric
collapse has not destabilized Kasmora. If anything, the disruption of
the traditional southern trade route has redirected some commercial
traffic while leaving Kasmoran agricultural output untouched. The
world's attention is focused on the drama of the Veltric aftermath.
Kasmora's demographic and economic trajectory is largely unexamined by
those distracted by that drama.

**The Trade Situation**

The disruption of Likian trade with eastern Nereth is one of the
defining commercial facts of the present day. The old route — Likian
ships to Alaian southwestern ports, goods chaining east through
Imperial merchant channels — collapsed with the Empire. Likia now trades
directly with western Nereth through Lienia's ports. Goods move overland
and by river eastward toward the Dwarven passes and through to the
eastern successor states — slower, more expensive, and more dangerous
for merchants, but functional. The Dwarven pass-lords of Nereth are
growing wealthy as the new intermediaries. Eastern successor states with
good mountain access have a commercial advantage over those that
depended on the coastal route. The old southern coastal route has not
disappeared entirely — some coastal successor states are attempting to
reconstitute commercial shipping — but it remains unreliable.

**Chapter 39: Origins & Culture**

**The Likian People**

Likian culture is a hybrid civilization — the product of centuries of
intermingling between Flandric seafarers from Nereth and the
Kordemelian coastal peoples of northern Kasmora. The culture that emerged from this Flandric-Kordemelian blend is genuinely Likian: distinct,
original, and not reducible to either parent tradition.

The founding Flandric seafarers pushed out into Oceanus Centralis as
raiders and transitioned quickly to traders, discovering the central
island chain and recognizing its extraordinary strategic value.
Sustained contact and intermingling with Kordemelian Kasmoran coastal
peoples gradually transformed them. The language shifted. The customs
blended. By the time Likia was formally declared an independent state at
0 LC, the transformation was substantially complete. The founding was a
peaceful acknowledgment of what had already become reality through
cultural drift — no wars, no dramatic break with the Flandric cousins left
behind in Nereth. Commerce was its foundation from the beginning.

**Religion**

Likia’s religious landscape reflects its Flandric origins and its role
as a cosmopolitan maritime crossroads. Two faiths are present within
Likian territory.

**Skrosenism** — The ancestral faith of the Flandric founders, retained
through a thousand years of cultural transformation. Dominant across all
Likian-culture settlements — the island chain, the Kasmoran coastal
territory, and the frontier outposts alike. Skrosenism is the world’s
largest organized religion at approximately 16.5 million believers, with
an expansionism of 9.2 — the highest of any faith. Its presence in Likia
was never imported through trade; it was always there, carried by the
founders. This creates a religious thread connecting Likia to the Flandric
states of western Nereth — Flaland, Laups, and the highland kingdoms — a
shared ancestral faith that transcends the commercial relationship.

**Xinchang Deities** — Tengcian-derived organized polytheism, present in
the two Tengcian community settlements (Shaozhoua and Wenzhongdu).
Arrived through post-cold-peace Valtheran commercial integration.
Approximately 6.3 million believers world-wide.

**Chapter 40: Geography & Settlements**

**The Central Island Chain**

The strategic heart of Likia. The islands sit directly across the
principal sea routes connecting all three inhabited continents. Sea
lanes radiate outward from the capital in multiple directions. The
islands are volcanic in origin and may still be tectonically active.

**Margaros** — Capital of the Principality of Likia. Population ~11,400.
Located on the largest island of the central chain, on elevated ground
surrounded by ocean. Has citadel, walls, port, and plaza. The political
and military headquarters of a naval empire. Effectively impregnable to
land-based assault. Follows Skrosenism. Province: Naupheleia.

**Nympha** — Island chain settlement. Population ~5,500. Follows
Skrosenism.

**Ekion** — Island chain settlement. Population ~2,500. Province: New
Naupheleia — a colonial foundation named after the home province of the
capital. Follows Skrosenism.

**Pseipodopo & Byblaleia** — Small island settlements on the Byblaleia
Islands. Populations ~912 and ~845 respectively. Both follow Skrosenism.
Remote island outposts at the margins of Likian territorial control.

**The Kasmoran Coastal Territory**

The original founding zone on the northeastern coast of Kasmora,
separated from the agricultural interior by the northern mountain range.
The Kordemelian Khaganate presses directly against Likia’s western
border — the city of Likia itself sits very close to the frontier. The
Dukanese Theocracy borders to the south. An eastern peninsula points
toward the island chain, serving as the natural embarkation point for
crossing to Margaros.

**The City of Likia** — Population ~10,900. Port, walls, plaza. No
citadel. The original founding settlement and commercial heart of Likia
— the city the state is named after, but not the capital. Follows
Skrosenism. Province: Rhamnos.

**Secenaidea** — Population ~10,100. Port, walls, plaza. Sits on the
north coast of the Kasmoran landmass facing the strait toward Valthera —
the most exposed Likian settlement to any renewed Valtheran naval
pressure, which explains its walls. Follows Skrosenism. Province:
Secenaidea.

**Menepios** — Population ~8,800. Significant inland town. Follows
Skrosenism. Province hub with three settlements in its county.

**Sypetos** — Population ~7,200. Inland, near a freshwater lake.
Agricultural and supply base. Follows Skrosenism.

**Stanagne** — Population ~3,400. Port with walls. Likely a secondary
naval station. Follows Skrosenism.

**Degemipo** — Population ~2,500. Has a citadel despite no walls — a
frontier defensive post. Province: New Kaponia, named after another home
province, confirming colonial settlement pattern. Follows Skrosenism.

**Shaozhoua & Wenzhongdu** — Populations ~1,975 and ~824. The two
Tengcian community settlements within Likian territory. Follow
Xinchang Deities. Located in the elevated Wenzhongdu Territory. Their
presence reflects the post-cold-peace commercial integration with
Valtheran Tengcian states — a Mercantilist faction achievement.

**Chapter 41: Government & Politics**

**Constitutional Structure**

Likia is formally a Principality — a democracy in constitutional form
and an oligarchy in functional reality. Universal citizenship and voting
rights exist. A formal assembly deliberates and passes legislation. At
the local level democratic participation is genuine and meaningful. At
the national level the agenda is shaped by guild interests before it
reaches the floor, and outcomes are rarely surprising to those who
understand how the system works.

After the Valtheran naval war, the heads of the three founding families
negotiated a constitutional settlement with the rising guild class. The
founding families retained nominal political leadership through the
hereditary patron position — the patron nominates guildmasters to their
council from the national pool of guild leadership, holds tiebreaker
authority on council votes, but otherwise does not vote. This
arrangement is codified in law. Over time, through the natural
gravitational pull of shared interests and ideology, certain guilds
began consistently aligning with certain houses, producing the three
recognizable political factions of the present day.

The three houses are families, not formal political institutions. House
membership does not require political loyalty — only the hereditary
patron is legally bound to the house’s factional role. Descendants of
any founding house may belong to any faction. The patron position is
hereditary. The patron nominates but does not vote, holds tiebreakers,
and exercises real influence through strategic use of nomination power
and personal relationships with guildmasters. A skilled patron can be
highly influential despite constitutional constraints. A weak or
disinterested patron becomes genuinely ceremonial.

**The Three Houses & Factions**

**House Pelagion — The Democrats (15% of assembly)**

Hawkish, culturally nationalist, focused on reasserting Likian authority
over Likian-culture peoples now living under Kordemelian rule in the
former imperial territories to the west. Aligned primarily with the
combat military guilds — naval officers, marines, the fighting arms
whose careers depend on Likia projecting military force. Frame their
ambitions as liberation and cultural restoration. The minority party.
Most Likians have no stomach for war as it disrupts trade. The
Likian-culture peoples they wish to liberate have been under Kordemelian
rule long enough that significant religious assimilation has occurred —
weakening the cultural argument for intervention. Their proximity to the
lost territories — visible from Likian soil — keeps their argument alive
despite their minority status.

**House Eurytos — The Constitutionalists (47% of assembly)**

Administrative, regulatory, bureaucratic. Control the institutional
machinery of Likia’s governance. Aligned with administrative and
regulatory guilds. Frame their entrenchment as stability and respect for
Likia’s founding constitutional order — positioning any challenge to
their power as an attack on the constitution itself. Dominant but not
commanding. Need Mercantilist cooperation or abstention to pass
significant legislation. The Mercantilists’ kingmaker position gives the
smaller faction disproportionate influence. None of the three factions
is genuinely egalitarian, but all frame their positions in publicly
palatable terms.

**House Dromides — The Mercantilists (38% of assembly)**

Expansionist, commercially outward-looking, focused on new trade
frontiers. Aligned with support and logistics military guilds —
shipwrights, navigators, provisioners, port administrators. Frame their
ambitions as opportunity for all Likians. Responsible for architecting
the Lienian commercial relationship and enabling the Tengcian community
settlements in Likian territory. The Veltric collapse strengthened them
— new trade relationships with western Nereth and Valtheran
Tengcian states are Mercantilist achievements delivering visible
prosperity. The kingmakers of Likian politics at 1026 LC.

**The Guild System**

Guilds regulate trade, set standards, train professionals, and arbitrate
disputes throughout Likia — genuine civic institutions that most Likians
interact with as functional rather than political bodies. A Likian
sailor has real rights and protections through their guild. The combat
guilds align primarily with the Democrats; the support and logistics
guilds align primarily with the Mercantilists or Constitutionalists.
This split within the military guild community means the Democrats do
not fully control their own natural constituency — the people who build
and maintain the fleet hedge their bets between factions, giving the
Mercantilists genuine military credibility despite not being the hawkish
party.

**Chapter 42: Key Relationships**

**Kingdom of Lienia (Nereth)** — Likia’s closest commercial partner. The
Lienian western Nerethan kingdom whose ports lie directly across
Oceanus Centralis from the island chain — the closest Nerethan state to
the island chain, the primary landing point for Likian goods entering
Nereth. A direct commercial relationship that predates the Veltric
collapse and has become Likia’s most important Nerethan relationship
since the disruption of the southern coastal trade route. Lienia is also
one of the most internally composite kingdoms in western Nereth. Its
public identity is Lienian-led and maritime, but the realm contains a
smaller Westric rural belt and a substantial Sinzian southwestern
bloc with meaningful towns, ports, and commercial weight. This has made
Lienia both broader and less culturally uniform than its clean royal
image suggests. Urban Sinzian sentiment has periodically looked with
sympathy toward Sinz, while rural populations have tended to remain more
local and pragmatic. The crown has nevertheless retained the region, in
part because its southern and southwestern coastal systems are too
valuable to surrender lightly.

**Kordemelian Khaganate (Kasmora)** — Immediate western neighbor. The
world’s most populous state at ~6.8 million people, pressing directly
against Likia’s western border — the city of Likia itself sits close to
the frontier. Managed coexistence by mutual incapacity: Kordemeli cannot
project naval power against the island capital; Likia cannot retake
continental territories from a state of that size. The Likian-culture
peoples within Kordemelian territory have substantially assimilated
religiously during their time under Khaganate rule. The relationship is
stable by necessity rather than warmth.

**Valthera — The Cold Peace** — The cold peace established at ~750-800
LC has held for approximately two centuries. Valtheran states trade
through Likian-controlled waters under Likian terms. The See of Xin Guo
— the Tengcian rump state created as a condition of the peace
settlement, legally prohibited from maintaining a navy — persists on
Valthera’s coast nursing its grievances through Xinchang Deities
proselytization. The other Valtheran naval powers drew clear conclusions
from the Kingdom of Xin Guo’s fate and do not challenge Likia’s maritime
dominance directly.

**Flandric States of Western Nereth** — Flaland, Laups, and the other
Flandric highland kingdoms of western Nereth share Skrosenism with Likia —
the ancestral faith of Likia’s founders, still practiced by the Flandric
peoples who stayed behind in Nereth. This shared religious heritage
provides a cultural bond beyond the commercial relationship, a thread of
common identity connecting the world’s dominant maritime power to its
Nerethan ancestral homeland.

**Part IX: Kasmora — Political Landscape**

Kasmora is the most agriculturally productive continent in Eutheria, but
its geography has always resisted imperial consolidation. A mountain
range separates the northern coastal zone from the vast savannah
interior. Salan peoples range the open savannah as nomadic
pastoralists; Kordemelian, Rawran, Harian, and Dukanese peoples
have settled along rivers and at coastal margins. The continent has
never produced a single dominant empire — its history is one of
persistent fragmentation among river kingdoms, nomadic confederacies,
and coastal trading states.

At 1026 LC, Kasmora contains seven major states and the Principality of
Likia on its northern coast. The states vary enormously in culture,
government, religion, and geopolitical orientation. What follows
documents each in turn, with particular attention to the political
dynamics most relevant to campaign play.

**Chapter 44: The Dukanese Theocracy**

The Dukanese Theocracy is ruled by the Hierophant — a religious office
that absorbed the kingship approximately 200 years ago (around 826 LC)
when the then-king converted to Delistanism and dissolved the
distinction between political and religious authority. The Hierophant
governs as both head of state and supreme religious leader, with no
meaningful separation between the two roles.

Dukan's culture is Dukanese, but its state religion is Delistanism
— a faith that evolved from ancient Dukanese polytheist folk tradition
among Harian peoples. The Dukanese Hierophant did not adopt a
foreign faith in any simple sense: Delistanism presents itself as the
fulfillment and organized expression of the older Dukanese tradition,
which it absorbed and superseded. This is the theological justification
the Hierophant uses to suppress the surviving Dukanese Deities folk
religion within Dukan's borders.

**Internal Tensions**

Two simultaneous resistance movements challenge the Theocracy's
authority:

**Dukanese revivalists** — Ethnic Dukanese who call for
restoration of the pre-Theocracy folk tradition. They reject the
Hierophant's claim that Delistanism fulfills and replaces the old
practice, arguing instead that a Harian-developed religion — however
theologically related — is not the legitimate heir of Dukanese ancestral
worship. The revivalists have no armed wing and operate through cultural
preservation, underground ritual, and political pressure on sympathetic
nobles.

**Likian Skrosenists** — Communities of Likian-culture peoples acquired
by Dukan approximately 150 years ago following a short, pyrrhic war with
Likia. These communities are Skrosenist — followers of the world's
largest Flandric-origin organized religion — and have resisted Delistanist
imposition. The most visible holdout is Kalginon (Likian culture,
Skrosenist, population ~8,982), a significant settlement sitting within
Dukan's borders that refuses conversion. The Dukanese state lacks the
resources and political will to force the issue, and the armistice with
Likia makes overt persecution diplomatically dangerous.

**The Likian War & Its Aftermath**

Approximately 150 years ago (around 876 LC), Dukan and Likia fought a
short war. Dukan achieved a pyrrhic victory — it acquired Likian-culture
territories including Kalginon, but the armistice barely holds and the
acquisition has proven more a liability than an asset. Dukan is
simultaneously managing two internal resistance movements and a
barely-stable border with Likia, which explains its moderate
expansionism figure despite its theocratic character: the Theocracy is
overextended, not pacifist.

The Democratic faction within Likia's government considers the
liberation of co-religionist Skrosenist communities under Dukanese
persecution their highest priority. This keeps diplomatic pressure on
the armistice from the Likian side even when Dukan itself has no
appetite for renewed conflict.

**Seza Colony and the Last Practitioners**

The Dukanese Deities folk religion — the ancestral tradition that
Delistanism claims to have fulfilled — has been suppressed so thoroughly
within Dukan that only 814 believers remain in the entire world. All of
them live in Seza Colony, a small coastal area and island chain in
southeastern Haria — territory that has never been Dukanese and whose
ruler, the Kingdom of Haria, does not enforce religious conformity.

The people of Seza Colony do not think of themselves as guardians of a
lost faith. They are simply practicing what their grandparents
practiced. Harians regard them as quaint and somewhat backwards; the
outside world is almost entirely unaware of their existence. The Dukanese
revivalists inside Dukan know Seza Colony exists, and it functions as
their most important symbolic resource — proof that the old tradition
survived somewhere, that it was not truly extinguished, only driven out.

> *GM Note: Seza Colony is a natural destination for Dukanese revivalist
> NPCs seeking living contact with the old tradition, or for players
> investigating religious suppression in Dukan. The people there have no
> awareness of their symbolic significance to the revivalist movement —
> which creates immediate dramatic tension the moment contact is made.*

**Chapter 45: Delistanism**

Delistanism is an organized polytheist faith with approximately 11.8
million believers, making it one of the largest religions in the world.
It is the state religion of the Dukanese Theocracy (enforced) and the
organic faith of the Kingdom of Haria (adopted through cultural contact,
not imposition). It also has significant presence in parts of Kordemeli
and scattered communities across Kasmora.

Its theological DNA is Dukanese — Delistanism evolved from and claims to
fulfill the ancient Dukanese polytheist folk tradition. But that
evolution happened among Harian peoples, not among Dukanese
ones. Delistanism is therefore a distinctly Harian organized faith with
Dukanese theological ancestry: the Harian peoples took the Dukanese
religious inheritance, formalized it, and built an organized theological
tradition from it. Dukanese peoples who encounter Delistanism find
much that is familiar in its theology — the same divine figures, the
same cosmological assumptions — but expressed through Harian ritual
practice, Harian institutional structures, and Harian cultural idioms.

The Dukanese Hierophant's claim that Delistanism is the fulfillment of
the Dukanese tradition is not simply self-serving propaganda — it has
genuine theological merit that makes the argument difficult to refute on
doctrinal grounds. The Dukanese revivalists' counter-argument is not
theological but cultural: even if Delistanism accurately represents the
theological content of the old faith, it is not the same practice, and
practice is inseparable from identity.

> *GM Note: Delistanism's dual role — as an enforced state religion in
> Dukan and an organic community faith in Haria — gives clerics of the
> same religion very different social characters depending on where they
> come from. A Delistanist cleric from Haria is a community religious
> leader with no coercive authority. A Delistanist cleric from Dukan's
> institutional church is an agent of a theocratic state. Both draw
> power from the same alignment forces; their social contexts could not
> be more different.*

**Chapter 46: The Kordemelian Khaganate**

The Kordemelian Khaganate is the largest state on Kasmora's northern
coast, with a population of approximately 6.83 million spread across 65
settlements and an enormous territorial extent. Its culture is
Kordemelian, its government a Khaganate, and its capital is Dogu — a
Kordemelian-culture, Ayedist city of approximately 20,391 people, the
largest single settlement in Kasmora.

Kordemeli is religiously divided in a way that reflects its territorial
history. The Khaganate contains two distinct religious zones: an Ayedist
majority in its Kordemelian and Salan-culture settlements, and a Skrosenist
population concentrated in its Likian-culture communities — former
Likian territories acquired in earlier expansions. Of 65 total
settlements, approximately 23 are Ayedist and 42 are Skrosenist. The
Skrosenist plurality is a direct consequence of Kordemeli's absorption
of Likian-culture populations and its shared northern border with Likian
territory.

**Dogu — The Capital**

Dogu is the Kordemelian capital — a Kordemelian-culture, Ayedist city of
approximately 20,391 people and the administrative heart of the
Khaganate. As the largest settlement in Kasmora, Dogu anchors the
Khaganate’s political and commercial identity. Its Kordemelian
character reflects the dominant cultural strand of Kordemelian
governance, even as the Khaganate’s settlement count is majority
Likian-culture.

**Cultural Composition**

Kordemeli contains 39 Likian-culture settlements, 20 Kordemelian-culture
settlements, and 6 Salan-culture settlements. The Likian majority in
settlement count does not translate to political dominance — Kordemelian
governance is Kordemelian in character, and the Likian communities are
subjects rather than co-rulers. The 6 Salan-culture settlements within
Kordemeli reflect the long open-border relationship with the Kingdom of
Sala to the south, mirrored by 8 Kordemelian-culture settlements within
Sala.

**Relationship with Likia**

Kordemeli and Likia share managed coexistence. Kordemeli controls the
Kasmoran mainland coast nearest to Likia's island chain, and the
Khaganate's proximity to the city of Likia itself is a persistent
strategic concern for Likian planners. Both states have reasons to
maintain peace — Kordemeli benefits from Likian maritime trade access,
and Likia cannot afford a land war while managing its island-chain
commitments — but the relationship is not warm. The Likian-culture
communities within Kordemeli provide a persistent point of political
friction, as Likia's Democratic faction regards them as co-religionists
deserving liberation.

**Ayedism and the Kordemelian Pilgrimage Route**

Kordemeli adopted Ayedism from Salan peoples — it is a convert
culture, without ancestral connection to Ayedism's holy sites. The
faith's center of gravity, Tuwaid in the Kingdom of Sala's remote Jora
Territory, lies entirely within Sala's borders. Kordemelian pilgrims
seeking to reach Tuwaid must enter Sala from the north and traverse the
entire kingdom to reach the southwestern Jora Territory. Sala controls
this access entirely and can restrict or open the pilgrimage route
according to its political interests.

> *GM Note: The pilgrim route from Kordemeli through Sala to Tuwaid is a
> natural adventure corridor: a months-long journey through a culturally
> distinct kingdom, ending at a sacred site whose religious significance
> is felt by both Salan and Kordemelian communities. Sala's
> occasional restrictions on pilgrimage access are a ready-made source
> of diplomatic tension.*

**Chapter 47: Ayedism**

Ayedism is an organized polytheist faith with approximately 11.95
million believers — the second largest religion in Eutheria after
Skrosenism. It is fundamentally a Salan faith, originating among
Salan nomadic peoples and expressing through sacred geography
rather than built temple infrastructure. Ayedism has produced no temple infrastructure; the faith's physical expressions are natural sites, pilgrimage
routes, and the landscape itself.

The center of Ayedism's sacred world is Tuwaid, a small settlement of
approximately 3,214 people in the Jora Territory of the Kingdom of Sala.
Jora Territory is vast — approximately 749,000 square miles — and
sparsely settled, with a total population of roughly 922,000. Tuwaid is
not a city; it is a pilgrimage destination whose significance is
entirely religious. The journey to reach it, through open savannah and
across Sala's interior, is itself part of the religious character of the
faith.

Kordemeli's adoption of Ayedism is historically significant: a
Kordemelian steppe culture adopted a fundamentally Salan faith, with
no ancestral connection to its sacred geography. This creates an ongoing
pilgrimage dependency — Kordemelian Ayedists must travel through foreign
territory to reach their holiest sites, with Sala as gatekeeper.

Sala's capital Jalim follows Ayedism. This means the kingdom's political
center aligns religiously with the pilgrimage economy and the faith most
associated with Sala's interior population. Sala's commercial life is
still shaped by maritime trade, but the capital belongs to the same
broad Ayedist religious world as the kingdom it governs.

**Chapter 48: The Kingdom of Sala**

The Kingdom of Sala is the most expansionist state in Kasmora, with an
expansionism index of 42.9 — higher than any other Kasmoran state and
among the highest in the world. It is Salan in culture, governed
as a kingdom, and its capital Jalim (population ~12,546) sits on or near
the coast and functions as a major commercial hub with heavy Valtheran
maritime trade presence.

Sala controls access to Tuwaid, Ayedism's holiest site, which gives it
enormous soft power over both the Salan Ayedist population of
its own interior and the convert Kordemelian Ayedists to the north. This
access is normally open — Sala has strong economic incentives to allow
pilgrimage — but restriction is a diplomatic tool available when needed.

**Cultural Composition**

Sala contains 51 Salan-culture settlements and 8 Kordemelian-culture
settlements. The 8 Kordemelian-culture settlements within Sala (of which 4
are Ayedist and 4 are Delistanist) reflect the same long open-border
relationship with Kordemeli that produces 6 Salan-culture settlements
within the Khaganate. This cultural interpenetration is the signature of
a centuries-old stable border relationship, not recent conquest.

The largest settlement in Sala other than the capital is Ener
(Kordemelian-culture, Delistanist, population ~9,777) — a Kordemelian
Delistanist community of significant size. There is also a small
Delistanist settlement called Yesen (Kordemelian, population ~1,050). The
presence of Delistanist Kordemelian communities within an Ayedist Salan
kingdom illustrates the degree to which cultural and religious identity
do not map cleanly onto political borders in this region.

The port of Salahrah follows Ayedism like the rest of Sala's core
religious landscape. Valtheran maritime trade may still be present in
Sala's ports, but it does not establish a separate confessional enclave
within the kingdom. Jalim likewise belongs to Sala's Ayedist religious
order rather than standing apart from it.

**Relationship with Kordemeli**

The Kingdom of Sala and the Kordemelian Khaganate share a complex
relationship defined by the Ayedism pilgrimage economy, their long open
border, and their mutual interpenetration of cultural communities. They
are not enemies — the 8 Kordemelian-culture settlements within Sala and 6
Salan-culture settlements within Kordemeli represent generations of
peaceful coexistence and cross-border settlement. But Sala's control of
Tuwaid access gives it structural leverage over Kordemeli that the
Khaganate cannot easily escape.

Sala's high expansionism (42.9) suggests it is actively pursuing
territorial or commercial expansion. Given the open northern border with
Kordemeli and the pilgrimage economy flowing through its territory,
Sala's most likely vectors of expansion are commercial and maritime
rather than military — controlling more of the pilgrimage
infrastructure, extending trade networks, and leveraging its coastal
position to increase maritime commerce.

> *GM Note: The Sala-Kordemeli relationship is one of the most complex
> bilateral dynamics in Kasmora. Sala holds the sacred geography;
> Kordemeli holds the larger military force and northern border access.
> Neither can easily dominate the other, and both benefit from
> stability. The relationship is the kind of managed interdependence
> that produces rich political intrigue when strained — a drought in
> Jora Territory, a disputed succession in Sala, or a militant religious
> faction within Kordemeli could all destabilize arrangements that have
> held for generations.*

**Chapter 49: The Kingdom of Hawa**

The Kingdom of Hawa is the second Salan state in Kasmora, with a
population of approximately 3.34 million spread across 37 settlements.
Its capital is Ashaybar — at 22,651 people the largest capital in
Kasmora, larger even than Dogu. Ashaybar is a port city with citadel and
walls, heavily fortified and maritime in orientation. Hawa is
overwhelmingly Ayedist and culturally homogeneous, with only four
Rawran settlements in its eastern interior near the border
with Har.

Hawa is fundamentally a maritime trading kingdom. It has seven port
settlements — more than any other Kasmoran state relative to its size —
and its expansionism (34.4) is maritime and commercial in orientation
rather than territorial. Hawa does not maintain the kind of heavy land
military that would threaten its neighbors; its energy and investment
are directed toward the sea.

Hawa's trade route to Likia runs south and east around the bottom of the
continent, passing through Harian coastal waters before reaching Likia's
eastern approach. This gives the Kingdom of Haria structural leverage
over Hawa's commercial lifeline — Hawa's access to international trade
depends on terms negotiated with a culturally and religiously distinct
neighbor.

**Relationship with Sala**

The Kingdom of Sala lies directly to Hawa's north, occupying the entire
western coast above Hawa's territory. The two states share a long
interior land border and are culturally and religiously identical — both
Salan Ayedist kingdoms with no cross-border settlement and no
structural friction. Every settlement on both sides of their shared
border is Ayedist. The relationship is quiet and uncomplicated: two
kingdoms that have sorted themselves out geographically, found their own
maritime orientations, and have nothing to fight about. Sala faces north
toward Likia and the pilgrimage economy; Hawa faces south and east
around the continent. Their port networks do not compete.


**Relationship with Har**

Har is Hawa's most significant land neighbor, sharing a long eastern
frontier. The relationship is cordial and non-threatening. Har's
valuable resources lie deep in the interior — far enough from the border
that they present no temptation for a land grab — and Har's low
expansionism (5.7) reflects a kingdom with no territorial ambitions.
Hawa's maritime focus means it maintains no large land army. Two states
with fundamentally different orientations sharing a long quiet frontier.

The border zone shows genuine cultural blending: four
Rawran settlements sit inside Hawa's eastern interior, and
several Salan Rawran Society settlements sit directly adjacent to Har's
largest city. The shared Rawran Society faith in the border zone
lubricates this contact, producing the kind of community-level
integration that characterizes neighbors who have coexisted peacefully
for generations.

**Relationship with Haria**

Hawa's relationship with the Kingdom of Haria is transactional and
bounded. Hawa's southern trade route passes through Harian coastal
waters, making Harian ports the natural waypoints for Hawan shipping
bound for Likia. Haria profits from this traffic. But the border between
the two states is a hard cultural line — zero Salan settlements in
Haria, zero Harian settlements in Hawa. These are two states that share
waters but not communities: different cultures, different religions
(Ayedism versus Delistanism), no theological overlap to lubricate
integration.

Haria's expansionism (29.2) means it is not a passive actor in this
arrangement. A growing maritime state that sits astride Hawa's only
route to Likia has structural leverage. The terms of Hawan access to
Harian waters are a matter of ongoing negotiation rather than settled
right.

> *GM Note: Hawa is the most port-dense state in Kasmora and its capital
> Ashaybar the largest city on the continent. Despite this, it rarely
> appears in Kasmoran political narratives because it has no enemies and
> no pressing grievances. That stability makes it an excellent base of
> operations for players — a wealthy, well-organized maritime kingdom
> with good infrastructure and no active conflicts. The Haria
> relationship is the natural source of tension if needed: a negotiation
> gone wrong, a Harian port tax dispute, or a Harian expansion that
> threatens the southern route could pull Hawa into conflict without
> requiring it to have been aggressive.*

**Chapter 50: The Kingdom of Har and the Commonwealth of Sipharan**

The Kingdom of Har and the Commonwealth of Sipharan are the two
Rawran states of Kasmora, both predominantly Rawran Society
in religion, geographically adjacent, and sharing the same cultural
heritage. They are nevertheless fundamentally different political
entities — Har a conventional feudal monarchy, Sipharan a federation of
self-governing republics and cantons — and this political difference is
the defining fact of their relationship.

**The Kingdom of Har**

Har is a prosperous, self-contained Rawran kingdom with a
population of approximately 2.62 million and 23 settlements organized
into 14 feudal provinces — eleven Counties, one Earldom, and minor
frontier territories. Its capital is Shardawat (population ~10,225), one
of four substantial port cities on Har's southern coast facing the gulf.

Har's low expansionism (5.7) reflects a kingdom that is comfortable with
what it has. The territory contains significant mineral or material
wealth in its interior — the specific resource is not yet established,
but the evidence is consistent: large port cities disproportionate to
its population, no military ambition, a self-contained cultural and
religious character, and a history of stable borders. Har exports its
own goods through its gulf ports, joining the southern trade route to
Likia that Hawa's ships also travel. It is a producer and exporter
rather than a transit economy.

Har's four port cities are notable for their size relative to the
kingdom's total population. Shardawat, Bor-Tida, E-Buzine, and Shalam
are all substantial communities, all Rawran Society, all facing the
gulf. They reflect a maritime export economy built around Har's inland
wealth rather than around trade intermediation.

Har borders Hawa to the west (cordial, stable — see Chapter 48),
Sipharan to the south (see below), Dukan to the northeast (a single
Delistanist settlement at Ukulmara marks the border contact), and the
open gulf to the south. The Dukanese border is the only point of
religious friction — one Delistanist community at the northeastern
margin — but Har's inward focus means this contact produces no
significant tension.

**The Commonwealth of Sipharan**

The Commonwealth of Sipharan is the most politically distinctive state
in Kasmora. With a population of approximately 851,000 across 10
settlements and 8 provinces, it is the smallest Kasmoran state by most
measures — yet it maintains complete independence from the much larger
feudal kingdom that surrounds it on three sides. The reason is
structural: Sipharan is not a smaller version of Har. It is a
fundamentally different kind of political entity.

Sipharan's eight provinces include three Republics (Baria, Shekh,
Urupha), two Cantons (Aksbonia, Eshlaktum), two Territories, and one
Land. The Republics and Cantons are self-governing communities with
their own political institutions. The Commonwealth is the federal
structure that binds them — not a monarchy with subjects, but an
agreement among autonomous communities to coordinate on matters of
shared concern. Har's feudal nobility has nothing to offer Sipharan's
republican communities that they do not already have, and everything to
take away.

Sipharan is 100% Rawran in culture and 100% Rawran Society
in religion — the most culturally and religiously homogeneous state in
all of Kasmora, possibly in the world. Not one settlement deviates. This
uniformity is not coincidental. The Rawran Society's fellowship-based,
initiatory structure — spreading through community belonging rather than
missionary outreach — is the institutional glue that holds the
Commonwealth together. Shared religious fellowship provides the trust
and social infrastructure that makes self-governance among autonomous
communities viable. The Rawran Society is not merely Sipharan's
religion; it is the foundation of its political culture.

Sipharan sits at the bottom of the gulf, geographically enclosed — Har
above it on three sides, the gulf waters to the south. It has zero
ports. The gulf provides natural protection rather than maritime
opportunity; Sipharan has turned inward rather than seaward. Its capital
Harzad (population ~10,748) has a citadel and walls, and Ur-Shar has
walls — notable fortification investment for a small, peaceful state.
The walls reflect not active conflict but the prudent caution of a
self-governing enclave surrounded by a larger feudal power.

Sipharan's moderate expansionism (10.9) suggests gradual organic growth
rather than aggressive expansion — new communities forming at the
margins, new cantons or territories being incorporated into the
Commonwealth structure as population fills the available land.

> *GM Note: Sipharan is the most unusual political entity in Kasmora.
> Its republican structure, fellowship religion, and geographic
> enclosure within Har create natural adventure hooks: a Sipharanese
> Republic seeking outside help against Harran pressure, a Rawran
> Society fellowship whose initiatory secrets have political
> consequences, or the question of what Sipharan does if Har's attitude
> toward its independence ever changes. The contrast between Har's
> comfortable feudalism and Sipharan's principled self-governance is a
> ready-made political and philosophical tension.*

**The Har-Sipharan Relationship**

Har and Sipharan share the same culture, the same religion, and a long
border — yet maintain distinct political identities. The relationship is
probably stable but not without underlying tension. Har has no immediate
incentive to absorb Sipharan by force: the Rawran Society communities on
both sides of the border share fellowship bonds that would make conquest
politically costly, and Har's comfortable prosperity gives it no
pressing need for Sipharan's territory. Sipharan's walls suggest
awareness that this calculation could change.

The closest Har-Sipharan settlement pairs are at distance 1.4 —
essentially adjacent. Communities on both sides of the border almost
certainly participate in shared Rawran Society fellowship networks that
transcend the political boundary. This creates informal bonds of mutual
familiarity and obligation that complicate any purely political analysis
of the relationship. Har and Sipharan are neighbors who share a faith
and a culture; the question of why they are two states rather than one
is fundamentally political and historical rather than cultural.

> *GM Note: The founding of the Commonwealth of Sipharan — when, why,
> and how a federation of republican communities established itself
> inside what became Harran territory — is an open historical question.
> It may have predated the Kingdom of Har entirely, making Sipharan the
> older political entity and Har the expansionist that grew up around
> it. Or it may have emerged as a deliberate act of political
> self-differentiation by communities that chose not to accept feudal
> authority. Either origin story has significant implications for the
> current relationship.*

**Part X: Valthera — Western States**

**Chapter 52: The Republic of Han Guo**

Political Character

Han Guo is a Tengcian republic that, in effect, is a deeply integrated
Tengcian–Cheoni union. Its official forms, court language, institutional
culture, and civil tradition are Tengcian, which is why the state is
conventionally counted as Tengcian in broad demographic reckoning. On the
ground, however, the republic is more cohesive than the settlement
pattern alone first suggests. The Tengcian and Cheoni populations of the
Han Guo region form overlapping language-family communities in a mixed
frontier society where substantial bilingualism is likely, especially in
urban and governing life.

Capital & Founding Compact

The capital, Insan, is Cheoni. This is not an anomaly. The evidence
suggests that Insan became the capital through a founding political
compromise: Han Guo governs through a Tengcian institutional order from a
Cheoni city. This implies that the Cheoni side of the union was
foundational rather than marginal.

Origins & Development

Han Guo likely took coherent republican form in the post–Xin Guo-war
order. In the aftermath of the Likian victory and the reorganization of
the Strait region, Tengcian and Cheoni communities in this part of
Valthera had strong incentives to combine their resources. Economic
pressure, competition with larger neighboring powers, and shared
Taizhouist religious life all encouraged durable political union. Han
Guo is therefore understood not as a conquest state, but as a negotiated
republican compact.

Geographic Reach

Han Guo's northwestern holdings are not a second heartland. The biome
and political maps show that they lie on the far western edge of the
habitable fringe beneath the glacier. They appear to be remote
extraction outposts — small, sea-linked, and economically useful —
rather than part of a broad northern territorial corridor.

Han Guo's Tengcian character is expressed not through a Tengcian capital,
but through its institutions, its religious life, and its duty to
protect the sacred Taizhouist center at Shang.

**Chapter 53: Quz Guo & Shang**

Sacred Status

Quz Guo is a small sovereign Tengcian Taizhouist state centered on Shang,
the birthplace of Taizhouist philosophy and religion. It predates Han
Guo and is understood as one of the older city-states of the region
rather than as a later creation of the republic.

Its continued separate existence is explained by sacred status rather
than by raw power. Quz Guo is formally sovereign and governs itself
internally, but its security and broader geopolitical shelter depend on
Han Guo. It is therefore better compared to a sacred polity whose
independence is preserved because absorption into ordinary secular
administration would diminish its religious standing.

Relationship to Han Guo

Han Guo's protection of Quz Guo is not merely a matter of convenience or
treaty mechanics. It is a religious duty built into Han Guo's
self-understanding. The republic safeguards Shang and preserves Quz
Guo's autonomy in much the same way that a larger religious civilization
might protect its holiest center without reducing it to an ordinary
province.

The resulting structure is dual but coherent: Insan is the political
capital of the republic, while Shang is the sacred center of the wider
Taizhouist world.

**Chapter 54: The Hayanguk Theocracy**

Hayanguk is a substantial inland Cheoni theocracy in western Valthera.
It is, first and foremost, a buffer state: geographically between
Cheoni, Tengcian, and Dwarven spheres, and religiously between mainstream
Cheoni faith and a frontier synthesis that its neighbors regard with
suspicion.

Its defining religion is Tongj Apostatism, a Cheoni reinterpretation of
Dwarven religious tradition blended with elements of the Witnesses of
Dedeok. This makes Hayanguk not merely a Cheoni state with an unusual
local cult, but a polity whose entire public character has been shaped
by frontier religious synthesis. Tongj Apostatism is considered an
apostasy by the Dedeokans and a heresy by the Dwarves, though the
Dwarves are generally less alarmed by it and tend to regard Tongists as
peculiar rather than intolerable. The sharper religious hostility comes
from the Dedeokan side.

Geographic Position

Hayanguk occupies an inland position in the south-central western part
of Valthera. It does not possess open maritime access and has no ports.
It is therefore not a naval state and is not defined by the maritime
logic that shapes parts of Tengcian Valthera.

Its position places it between Cheonguk to the west and northwest, Guan
Guo to the north and east, and Han Guo to the south. It also fronts on
the rougher upland belt associated with the Dwarven corridor. Hayanguk
is therefore a hinge state where Cheoni lowland society, Tengcian
neighbors, and the upland Dwarven frontier all meet.

Political Character

Hayanguk is a theocracy, but its most important political trait is not
simple clerical rule. It is a state that thinks of itself as
beleaguered. Its political culture is defensive, guarded, and deeply
concerned with preserving the internal dominance of its religious order.

It does not regarded as missionary or outwardly ambitious. Its worldview
is closer to that of a state that believes it has carved out a
precarious space for its own survival and now must prevent outside
pressures from corrupting or dissolving it. It wants to be left alone,
but does not assume it will be.

Demographic & Social Character

Hayanguk is large enough to matter. It has a population of roughly 2.53
million, making it a real regional power rather than a tiny frontier
remnant. At the same time, it is overwhelmingly rural, with only a
modest urban system and no dominant commercial metropolis. This suggests
a stable agrarian society rather than a heavily urbanized
religious-commercial state.

Its settlements are culturally entirely Cheoni, which sets it apart from
the more visibly mixed settlement pattern of Han Guo. Religiously,
however, it is not uniform in a simple sense. Tongj Apostatism is
dominant, but other faiths are present within the state, which
reinforces the idea that Hayanguk exists in a frontier zone under
constant religious pressure rather than inside a sealed sacred core.

**Economy**

Hayanguk appears economically solid, self-sufficient, and inwardly
oriented. Its economy is understood as primarily agrarian, supported by
a heavily rural population. The maps also suggest a strongly forested
environment and a significant river system descending from the uplands
into the settled zone. Taken together, this points toward an economy
based on agriculture, forestry, river-linked internal transport, and
trade with the Dwarven uplands.

Hayanguk is a secondary transit zone from east to west, but not a major
long-distance commercial corridor. Most surrounding states have access
to maritime outlets or other more efficient routes, so Hayanguk is
unlikely to dominate continental trade. It is better understood as a
state that can sustain itself and profit from local frontier exchange
without becoming a great mercantile crossroads. Timber is likely one of
its most important resource bases.

Diplomatic Posture

Hayanguk maintains relations with outsiders in a manner best described
as polite but reserved. It is not isolationist in the absolute sense. It
must deal with merchants, neighboring states, and Dwarven trade. But it
treats outside contact as something to be managed and contained, not
welcomed. Outsiders are expected to conduct their business under
supervision and then leave.

Fortification & Military Character

Hayanguk is more fortified than average. About one-third of its
settlements have walls, citadels, or both, which places it above the
overall norm and above many neighboring states. Its military character
is therefore understood as primarily protective. Hayanguk does not look
like a conquering theocracy. It looks like a state that invests in
fortification, local security, and the protection of key internal nodes
because it expects pressure from outside.

Its importance in western Valthera lies less in maritime or commercial
power than in what it prevents. It absorbs pressure where Cheoni,
Tengcian, and Dwarven spheres meet and helps keep the western frontier
politically, culturally, and religiously plural.

**Chapter 55: The Kingdom of Cheonguk**

Cheonguk is the largest Cheoni kingdom in western Valthera and should be
understood as the mainstream Cheoni anchor state of the region. It is
large, culturally uniform, coastal, and politically self-assured. Where
Hayanguk represents the defensive frontier and Han Guo represents a
mixed republican union, Cheonguk represents conventional Cheoni kingship
at major-power scale.

Geographic Position

Cheonguk dominates much of the western coast of Valthera. It has broad
maritime frontage, multiple coastal inlets, and several ports, making it
far more outward-facing than inland Hayanguk. At the same time, it is
not a naval republic in the Han Guo mold. Its political character
remains that of a kingdom rooted in land, dynasty, and territorial order
rather than in maritime commerce alone.

Cheonguk sits west and northwest of Hayanguk and helps define the
western Cheoni core. It is geographically removed from the mixed
Tengcian–Cheoni transition zone that shapes Han Guo. This makes it the
clearest example of a large Cheoni state that is neither a frontier
theocracy nor a confessional hybrid.

Political Character

Cheonguk is understood as a confident, status-conscious kingdom that
presents itself as a respectable guardian of order. It is strong enough
to behave pragmatically rather than nervously. Its diplomatic posture is
formal and controlled, accustomed to routine dealings with outsiders
without becoming especially warm.

The current capital is Eoyangwan.

Religion & Internal Settlement

Cheonguk is culturally unified but religiously divided. Its older core
is best seen as Dedeokan, while its Tongj regions are later
acquisitions. The most coherent explanation is that Cheonguk expanded
into Tongj territory within the last century, during a period of general
lawlessness in the inland frontier before Hayanguk existed as a coherent
state. Those frontier acquisitions remained in Cheonguk's hands at the
end of the expansion, while the remaining Tongj regions later
consolidated into Hayanguk.

This gives Cheonguk a clear religious settlement: the older core of the
kingdom is Dedeokan; the annexed frontier territories are Tongj; Tongj
worship is protected within those designated regions; Tongj inhabitants
enjoy full rights as subjects of the kingdom; and the crown limits the
spread of Tongjism into the older Dedeokan heartland.

Cheonguk therefore does not treat its Tongj territories as occupied or
provisional. It regards them as fully and permanently part of the
kingdom. Its policy is not persecution, but territorial toleration under
royal control.

Relationship to Hayanguk

Cheonguk did not seize these lands from a fully formed Hayanguk. It
expanded during an earlier period of disorder in what later became
Hayanguk's sphere. As a result, the two states have an asymmetrical
relationship. Hayanguk would prefer those Tongj lands to lie within its
own religious sphere, but it lacks the political will to challenge
Cheonguk's control. Cheonguk, by contrast, treats them as settled
possessions and does not organize its policy around Hayanguk's religious
sensitivities.

Economy & Maritime Orientation

Cheonguk's scale, coastline, and population make it one of the major
Cheoni powers of Valthera. It is understood as a balanced kingdom:
agrarian at base, coastal in access, and politically integrated enough
to remain stable despite internal confessional division. The sea is an
important instrument of exchange and contact, but not the sole basis of
its identity.

Cheonguk is therefore the largest and most conventional Cheoni kingdom
in western Valthera: a first-rank regional power, more confident in its
own legitimacy than either Han Guo or Hayanguk.

**Chapter 56: The Duchy of Hamcheon**

Hamcheon is a small Cheoni Dedeokan coastal duchy on the southwestern
edge of Cheonguk. It is best seen as a minor but durable polity whose
survival depends less on power than on defensibility, cohesion, and
political modesty.

Geographic Position

Hamcheon occupies a narrow coastal wedge between Cheonguk and the sea.
It has only one meaningful land neighbor — Cheonguk — and therefore
exists almost entirely within Cheonguk's strategic shadow. Its terrain
is not merely flat coast: the duchy includes defensible upland ground
behind the shoreline, giving it more internal resilience than its small
size might suggest.

Cultural & Religious Character

Hamcheon is strikingly homogeneous. Its known settlements are all Cheoni
in culture and all follow the Witnesses of Dedeok. Unlike Han Guo or
Cheonguk, it does not appear to be managing a major cultural or
confessional divide. This makes Hamcheon a useful contrast state in
western Valthera: small, simple, cohesive, and local.

Fortification & Political Identity

Hamcheon is unusually heavily fortified for a state of its size. All of
its known settlements possess walls, citadels, or both. This should not
be treated as incidental. Fortification appears to be part of the
duchy's identity.

Hamcheon is understood as a polity oriented toward holding,
withstanding, and surviving rather than expanding. Its ruling class
likely thinks in terms of continuity, security, and local endurance
rather than grand ambition.

**Economy**

Hamcheon's economy is probably modest and regional. With a small
population and only one port, it is not a major commercial or naval
actor. The most likely economic base is a combination of local
agriculture, fishing, forestry, small-scale coastal trade, and harbor
exchange functions. It may matter as a local coastal gate, but not as a
continental trade hub.

Relationship to Cheonguk

Hamcheon is formally independent but heavily constrained by Cheonguk's
dominance of the surrounding region. It is not large enough to challenge
Cheonguk and likely survives because annexing it would cause more
disruption than benefit. Its room to maneuver is narrow, and its
security depends on remaining orderly, predictable, and unthreatening.

Hamcheon is therefore understood as a small, old, respectable coastal
duchy rather than a dynamic frontier state or rising commercial power.
Its importance lies in the survival of a cohesive local polity under the
shadow of larger kingdoms.

**Chapter 57: The Kingdom of Guan Guo**

Guan Guo is a cohesive Tengcian kingdom in western Valthera whose
importance comes less from spectacle than from position. It is best
understood as a river-gate toll kingdom: a state whose capital, economy,
and political tone are shaped by control of a major inland-to-sea water
system.

Geographic Position

Guan Guo occupies a central position in western Valthera between several
important neighboring powers. It borders Longlin Guo to the north, Tengc
to the east, Kaihui to the south and southeast, Han Guo to the
southwest, and Hayanguk to the west and southwest. This places it at the
meeting point of several regional systems without making it a frontier
state in the Hayanguk sense.

The kingdom is organized around a very long and significant river that
runs through much of its territory before reaching the sea near the
capital, Xuzhoubin. This river leaves Guan Guo for part of its course
and then returns, which makes Guan Guo's control partial rather than
absolute. Even so, the kingdom appears to hold some of the most valuable
stretches of the system: the lower and reentry sections, the river
mouth, and the easiest outlet to the coast.

The Bay & The Outlet

Guan Guo's strategic value is sharpened by the bay into which its river
system empties. This is not just ordinary coastline. It is a sheltered
inner basin where Longlin's inland river world, Guan Guo's lower-river
customs zone, and Tengc's eastern shore all meet before traffic reaches
the open sea.

The geography of the bay helps explain Guan Guo's importance. The
Longlin and Tengc shores appear comparatively steep and elevated, while
Guan Guo's western and southwestern shoreline is lower and flatter. This
likely explains why Guan Guo, rather than its neighbors, possesses the
best natural port conditions in the basin. Xuzhoubin sits exactly where
one would expect a customs capital to sit: at the river mouth on the
most usable low shore.

Economic Character

Guan Guo is, first and foremost, a toll kingdom. It likely profits from
customs and tolls on river traffic, bonded storage and warehousing,
river policing and escorted movement, transshipment between inland and
coastal routes, and control of the coastal outlet at Xuzhoubin.

The kingdom is not merely living off passing trade. The maps also
suggest that Guan Guo possesses a substantial grassland interior, giving
it a strong productive base of its own. It is therefore not just a
middleman state, but a kingdom with real internal economic strength
based on agriculture and pastoral production as well as river commerce.

Water Orientation

Guan Guo should not be imagined as a blue-water maritime power. Its
strength is better understood as riverine and littoral. It uses water as
an instrument of internal integration and controlled transit rather than
as the basis of broad seaborne dominance. Its narrow coastal frontage is
therefore not a weakness. It concentrates authority over the outlet
rather than dispersing it across a large shoreline.

Cultural & Religious Character

Guan Guo is one of the more internally cohesive states in western
Valthera. It is culturally Tengcian at both the state and settlement
level, with only a trace of Dwarven presence in one settlement.
Religiously, its settlements are uniformly associated with the
Kaibeihuan Cult.

This uniformity distinguishes Guan Guo from neighboring states such as
Han Guo and Cheonguk, both of which manage more visible internal
divisions. Guan Guo appears to be a kingdom whose ruling order does not
spend much energy containing confessional fracture. That likely
contributes to its reputation for stability and administrative
seriousness.

Political Tone

Taken together, the sources suggest that Guan Guo is an orderly,
transactional, and quietly strategic kingdom. Its power comes from
keeping movement predictable, taxable, and difficult to bypass. It is
one of the quieter but more consequential states of western Valthera.

**Chapter 58: Longlin Guo**

Longlin Guo is a proud northern Tengcian kingdom whose territory is large
but whose meaningful life is concentrated in the southern belt. It is
not a fully integrated state spread evenly across its whole map area.
Much of its far north lies under icefield influence or in marginal,
hard-to-settle country. Its actual political and economic core lies
farther south, where its settlements, capital, and the river system
converge.

Geography & Settlement

Longlin controls a large share of the middle course of the major river
system that eventually empties through Guan Guo's bay. Its capital,
Andenan, sits on the large lake just north of that bay and is also its
only port. The placement of its settlements makes it clear that Longlin
is not organized around the frozen north, but around the southern
river-facing zone where movement and settlement are viable.

This gives Longlin real leverage. It is not simply an upstream
hinterland. It holds much of the commercially meaningful middle river
and therefore influences access, cargo flow, and movement before goods
ever reach Guan Guo's lower customs zone.

Communications & Constraint

At the same time, Longlin is hamstrung by its own communications
geography. The maps suggest that there is no clear major road linking
the capital to the northern settlements without traversing Guan Guo's
territory. Its bay shoreline also appears less naturally suited to major
port development than Guan Guo's lower western shore. As a result,
Longlin controls much of the river but not the best maritime exit.

This creates a structural frustration: Longlin matters a great deal, but
geography denies it a clean downstream advantage. It therefore has
reason to be acutely sensitive to access, outlet options, and the
profits captured by downstream states.

Resource Frontier

Longlin's northern and upland reaches are understood as a resource
frontier. Difficult overland movement through the ice-affected north
makes the river corridor especially valuable. Settlements like Xingzhou,
the first major settlement on the river in the more usable southern
zone, are well placed to function as toll, logging, and transfer points
where northern goods begin to enter organized downstream trade.

This gives Longlin an economic role distinct from Guan Guo's. Longlin
helps gather, organize, and move northern resources into the river
system before Guan Guo monetizes them farther downriver.

Relationship to Guan Guo & The Bay

Longlin and Guan Guo almost certainly operate under some kind of
standing river arrangement. Their interests are too entangled for
anything else to work. Longlin controls much of the middle course; Guan
Guo controls the easier lower outlet and the best natural customs point
at the bay.

Longlin likely sees Guan Guo as both necessary and overprivileged. Guan
Guo profits from a downstream position Longlin cannot easily replace,
while Longlin supplies traffic and access that Guan Guo cannot generate
by itself. Tengc provides a secondary alternative through Siang, but
that shoreline appears less naturally favorable than Guan Guo's. Longlin
therefore has options, but none that free it completely from dependence
on downstream partners.

Political Tone

Longlin's diplomatic and political tone is understood as proud,
upstream, and resistant to dependence. It is capable of practical
arrangements, but not relaxed ones. Its high expansionism and broad but
thinly settled territory suggest a monarchy comfortable with frontier
assertion and with claiming wide spaces even when it cannot densely
integrate them.

Longlin's real strength lies in the southern river-facing belt rather
than the ice-bound north. It is a kingdom whose leverage is genuine, but
whose geography ensures that leverage is always contested.

**Chapter 59: The Tengc Empire**

Tengc is a large Tengcian state on the northeastern seaboard of eastern
Valthera facing the western waters of Oceanus Centralis. It calls itself
an empire, but its title reflects antiquity, prestige, and seniority
more than administrative complexity or material abundance. Tengc
considers itself the oldest of the modern Valtheran states, and this
claim is plausible in-world because its political order preserves a much
older clan-based structure rather than the denser later bureaucratic
forms seen elsewhere.

Geography & Settlement

Tengc's map is much larger than its effective core. Its southern and
southeastern coastal belt contains the great majority of its meaningful
life: the principal settlements, ports, roads, and the most useful land.
Farther north and northeast the empire expands into cold frontier
country, forest margins, broken coasts, island chains, and wide thinly
settled spaces. Much of this terrain is claimed more easily than it is
deeply governed.

This geography helps explain the state's peculiar character. Tengc is
not understood as a classic agrarian empire or as a wealthy compact
kingdom. It is a northern coastal confederation whose settled core
supports a much larger prestige geography of ice, islands, fisheries,
and marginal frontier territory.

**Economy**

Fishing is the dominant industry of Tengc. The empire's economy should
not be imagined as flowing primarily from its far northern frozen lands.
Those outer regions likely yield only narrow, difficult, and often
seasonal frontier products: marine catches, marine byproducts, furs, and
other specialized resources gathered at high cost.

The real material base of Tengc lies farther south. Its forested belt
likely supplies timber, pitch, tar, resin, fuel, and other products
needed for boats, repairs, workshops, and coastal life. Its more usable
southern and southeastern zones provide the settlements, processing
sites, and population base needed to support the wider imperial map. In
practice, the productive south sustains a much larger and poorer
northern claim zone.

Political Structure

Tengc bears imperial dignity but governs as a confederation. It is not a
dense centralized bureaucracy. Family clans choose their regional
chiefs, and those chiefs in turn elect an emperor for a fixed term of
ten years. The emperor is therefore less an absolute monarch than a
senior overlord above regional clan powers.

This structure fits the realities of distance and sparse population. The
center governs lightly. The emperor is most accessible in the settled
southern core and increasingly remote in the hinterlands, but this does
not necessarily produce crisis because distant regions are not expected
to be governed intrusively in their daily affairs.

Religion

Religion in Tengc is considered personal rather than public. Overt
displays of piety are culturally disfavored and may be regarded as
vulgar or performative. The religious life of the empire therefore seems
to reside more in households, families, and clans than in public civic
institutions. This subdued religious profile is reinforced by the
absence of temple-centered urban culture in Tengc's settlement pattern.

Maritime Character

Tengc's maritime capability is real, but narrow. Its strength lies in
coastal patrol, fishery protection, convoying, resupply, and difficult
cold-water seamanship rather than in first-rate blue-water power. It is
well adapted to broken coasts, marginal waters, and the practical needs
of a fishery realm. A useful shorthand is that Tengc possesses an
empire-of-fishermen navy rather than a grand battle fleet.

Foreign Relations & Tone

Tengc deals with its limited neighbors with practical efficiency rather
than elaborate diplomacy. It does not maintain a broad embassy network,
but it does keep representatives in the capitals of Longlin Guo and Guan
Guo in order to manage trade and access around the northern gulf region.
Its general political tone is restrained, unsentimental, and practical.

Taken together, the Tengc Empire is understood not as a rich or
expansive empire in the conventional sense, but as an old, sparse,
fishery-centered northern confederation whose authority is strongest
along its settled southern coast and weakest where its imperial map
extends into forest, ice, islands, and empty distance.

**Chapter 60: The Republic of Kaihui**

The Republic of Kaihui is a Tengcian state in eastern Valthera, occupying
a broad stretch of the southeastern seaboard and facing the western
waters of Oceanus Centralis. In formal law it is a republic; in
political reality it remains the heir to an older pastoral kingdom whose
governing habits were never fully broken. Kaihui is a state of rolling
plains, fertile valleys, horse-breeding districts, and long but
underdeveloped coasts. It is neither a true maritime power nor a purely
inland realm, but a coastal-plains state whose wealth rests primarily on
food production, livestock, and horse culture rather than on ports or
fleets.

**Historical Development**

Before the Likian–Xin War, the state existed as the Kingdom of Kaihui.
Its economy was deeply tied to the maritime order dominated by Xin Guo.
Kaihui was not itself a major naval power. It did not need to be. Its
plains and valleys produced grain, livestock, and timber in abundance,
while Xin's ports and ships handled much of the large-scale maritime
movement that connected Kaihui to the wider world.

When war came, Kaihui's interests favored preservation of that existing
order. Its participation was therefore mostly logistical rather than
ideological or naval. Grain, livestock, and timber from Kaihui helped
fuel Xin Guo's war effort. In the peace that followed, Likia judged its
settlement with Kaihui lenient: the state was left intact, but its
monarchy was abolished and replaced with republican institutions imposed
by treaty. Kaihui's elites have never made peace with that arrangement.
To them, the republic remains a foreign constitutional shell laid over a
proud and tradition-heavy people.

Geographic Position

Kaihui lies south of Guan Guo, north of Kan Guo, east of Han Guo, and
west and southwest of the Guan–Longlin–Tengc bay system. Its own coast
forms part of the southeastern seaboard of eastern Valthera rather than
the inner northern bay world shaped by Guan's river-gate position. This
placement matters. Kaihui has more coastline than Guan Guo, yet it has
never translated that shoreline into comparable maritime leverage.

The maps suggest several stretches of usable shore and coastal
approaches that could have supported more substantial harbor
development. Yet those harbors were never built at scale. Before the
war, Kaihui did not need them. Xin Guo's maritime system handled the
larger work of shipping and naval protection, while Kaihui only needed
enough local infrastructure to move goods outward.

The Port Problem & Trade

After the war this dependence became a liability. Kaihui still trades
through former Xin-linked ports where necessary, but those routes are
now heavily tariffed and politically unfavorable. As a result, Kaihui
prefers whenever possible to move trade through Guan Guo's ports or
through exchange networks tied to Kan Guo. The republic is therefore a
state with real coastline but incomplete control over its own maritime
destiny. It is coastal without being maritime-dominant.

Its interior is the more decisive geographic fact. The biome and height
patterns indicate a broad country of rolling plains, open pastoral land,
and fertile valleys. Kaihui is not understood as a port republic with a
supporting hinterland. It is a plains state with access to the sea.

**Economy**

Kaihui is one of the major food-producing states of eastern Valthera.
Its plains and valleys make it regionally indispensable in a way that
exceeds its population or formal political weight. Grain, livestock, and
related goods from Kaihui help sustain not only the republic itself but
also meaningful portions of the surrounding region. Too many nearby
powers depend on Kaihui's output to casually destabilize it. At the same
time, that same productivity makes Kaihui an obvious prize for any
ambitious state seeking to dominate the region, especially the more
powerful Kan Guo to the south.

The interior matters more to Kaihui than the coast. Agriculture,
pastoralism, horse breeding, and the movement of inland produce are the
true foundation of the state. Maritime trade remains important, but the
sea allows Kaihui to sell and acquire what it needs; the plains are what
make it worth trading with in the first place.

Horses and National Identity

No discussion of Kaihui is complete without the horse. Kaihui hosts the
largest population of Valtheran Thoroughbreds in the world, and these
animals are not merely a valuable breed or an export commodity. They are
a national inheritance and a pillar of identity. Kaihui's relationship
to horses is closer to that of a horse-people society than to an
ordinary agrarian state. Horses shape work, travel, status, law, and
memory.

Valtheran Thoroughbreds are prized across the continent for endurance,
refinement, intelligence, responsiveness, and prestige. Kaihui protects
them jealously. Strict laws govern their export, and resistance to
large-scale foreign sale of horses is one of the few issues that
commands near-universal agreement across the republic. Kaihui's people
understand that to arm neighbors with Kaihui horses in great numbers is
to strengthen those neighbors at Kaihui's own expense.

Horse milk, including fermented varieties, is a staple
in much of Kaihui, especially in the more pastoral districts. This alone
reveals how deeply horse culture runs through the state.

Society and Social Order

Kaihui remains socially conservative, communal, and deeply shaped by the
legacy of inland horse clans. A significant nomadic or semi-nomadic
sub-population persists, especially in the northern and less urbanized
plains. These communities are not marginal curiosities. They remain part
of the living fabric of the state and continue to influence its social
values and political expectations.

Kaihui's hierarchy is proud and old, though not always legible to more
urban outsiders. It values lineage, elders, local authority, and
deference to recognized leaders. The government itself is not especially
loved. Many rural Kaihui communities have little direct need to engage
deeply with the state. Their loyalty is more often to family, district,
plain, herd, and custom than to constitutional structures in Wenzhou.

Government

Kaihui is formally republican and functionally oligarchic. The public
face of the state is the elected assembly and its presiding officer, the
Prytanarch. This title is intentionally foreign in tone and reflects the
imposed constitutional language of the postwar order. The Prytanarch
sets the legislative agenda, regulates debate, and serves as the visible
embodiment of republican dignity. Yet the office lacks decisive
governing force. In common Kaihui speech, the holder is often mocked as
the Kongzui, the "empty mouth."

Real executive power lies in the dual presidency: the offices of the
Zuozheng and Youzheng. These two presidents may veto proposals from the
assembly and together form the true executive center of the republic.
Their terms last four years and are staggered every other year. The
Zuozheng and Youzheng, together with the Prytanarch and the assembly of
provincial governors, shape the field of candidates from which the
popular assembly votes. This preserves public participation while
ensuring that effective power remains filtered by the governing class.

For commoners and in public ritual, the republic is celebrated. For much
of the merchant class and aristocracy, it is still regarded as a foreign
system imposed on a proud people. In practice, Kaihui is a republic in
name and ceremony more than in sovereign function. The bureaucracy,
descended from the old royal machinery, remains the state's most durable
organ.

Religion

Kaihui's traditional religion is the worship of the Xinchang Deities,
but religion is generally treated as a personal matter rather than a
public civic identity. This sharply distinguishes Kaihui from Xin Guo,
where the same religious tradition became more visibly public and
obligatory. In Kaihui, faith is woven into household practice, family
custom, and local observance rather than monumental civic display.
Temples and other heavily anchored monuments are notably sparse. This
absence reflects not irreligion, but a culture in which belief is real
without being theatrical.

**Military Character**

Kaihui is defensive rather than aggressive. Its chief strategic
strengths are food production, interior depth, horse culture, and the
social resilience of its plains population. It can sustain itself, move
across its own open country, and field skilled riders and mounted
couriers. Its chief vulnerability lies in the visibility of its wealth.
A power such as Kan Guo has every reason to view Kaihui's grain and
plains as strategically desirable.

**Foreign Relations**

Kaihui sees itself as non-threatening, protectionist, and conservative
in foreign affairs. It prefers managed relations, guarded autonomy, and
the preservation of existing balances over adventurous diplomacy. Its
relationship with Likia is formally compliant and emotionally resentful.
The political class remembers the republic as an imposed order, yet
lacks both the unity and the appetite for open defiance.

With Guan Guo, Kaihui maintains important commercial ties because Guan's
better-developed port system makes it a preferred maritime partner. With
Kan Guo, relations are necessary but wary: southern trade matters, but
Kan's power makes it the republic's most obvious long-term threat. With
former Xin ports, Kaihui still deals where it must, though not on
favorable terms.

Political Tone

Kaihui thinks of itself as proud, communal, defensive, and old in its
habits. The republic itself is not the primary object of that pride.
Kaihui society is. The people of the plains, the horse-breeding
districts, the older hierarchies of respect and elder authority, and the
habit of local self-sufficiency matter more to ordinary Kaihui than the
constitutional language of the state.

For that reason, the Republic of Kaihui is understood as a state whose
official order belongs to the postwar age, but whose deeper identity
still belongs to the older world of the Kingdom of Kaihui: fertile
valleys, rolling grasslands, horse-breeding clans, practical
self-reliance, and a lasting suspicion of foreign systems imposed in the
name of peace.


**Chapter 61: The Kingdom of Kan Guo**

The Kingdom of Kan Guo is a Tengcian monarchy in eastern Valthera
occupying a substantial stretch of the southeastern seaboard and facing
the western waters of Oceanus Centralis. In contrast to several of its
more structurally unusual neighbors, Kan Guo is understood as a fairly
straightforward feudal kingdom: hereditary, landed, agrarian, and
coast-facing, with a stronger crown than most of its nobles would
prefer, but without the treaty-imposed republican shell of Kaihui, the
archaic confederative character of Tengc, or the specialized river-gate
identity of Guan Guo. This relative simplicity is part of its regional
importance. Kan Guo is a state whose power lies less in constitutional
novelty than in density, fertility, and the practical cohesion of a
normal working monarchy.

**Geography**

Kan Guo lies south of Kaihui and east of Han Guo on the southeastern
seaboard of eastern Valthera. It does not occupy the northern gulf and
river-gate position that defines Guan Guo, nor does it share Kaihui's
combination of long coast and underbuilt maritime infrastructure. Its
territory instead consists of a compact but populous coastal kingdom
whose eastern and southeastern shores open onto Oceanus Centralis.

The kingdom's terrain is more varied than a simple coastal plain. Much
of Kan Guo lies at low to moderate elevation, with productive lowlands,
river valleys, and green country spread across much of the state, but
the interior is interrupted by uplands and broken belts of higher
ground. This gives the kingdom both agricultural breadth and some
internal defensibility. It is not a horse-plains state on the Kaihui
model, nor a thin maritime strip; it is a fertile coastal monarchy with
a usable interior.

The settlement pattern reinforces this reading. Kan Guo supports an
unusually dense network of settlements for its size, and those
settlements are distributed across the interior and the coast rather
than concentrated in a single overwhelming capital metropolis. The royal
capital, Hejian, is a fortified port with a citadel, but it is not the
largest city in the kingdom. That distinction belongs to Xilinan, which
is better regarded as the principal commercial harbor. This division
between a royal capital and a larger mercantile port fits a monarchy
that seeks to retain control of maritime wealth without surrendering
political primacy to merchants alone.

**Historical Development**

Kan Guo appears to be one of the more ordinary enduring kingdoms of the
eastern Valtheran seaboard: old enough to contain inherited feudal
irregularities, but cohesive enough to have remained recognizably a
kingdom rather than collapsing into a looser confederation or being
forced into postwar constitutional reconstruction. Its present form
suggests a history of dynastic consolidation rather than radical
institutional rupture. Whatever local houses, marcher domains, or older
provincial lordships once existed across this coast, the kingdom
ultimately succeeded in binding most of them into a coherent feudal
order.

That consolidation was not perfectly complete. The enclosed Duchy of Fuz
Guo remains the clearest sign that the map of Kan Guo is the result of
historical layering rather than neat administrative design. The kingdom
therefore gives the impression of an old monarchy that absorbed most of
its lesser rivals and exceptions, but not every one of them.

**Political Order**

Kan Guo is treated as a conventional feudal monarchy. The king rules by
hereditary right and governs through a hierarchy of dukes, earls,
barons, and lesser landed lords whose privileges, obligations, and
rivalries give the kingdom much of its internal texture. Royal power is
real, but it is not absolute in the abstract. It depends on crown lands,
dynastic legitimacy, feudal obligation, and the kingdom's ability to
draw levy, tax, and service from a large settled population.

The density of settlement and the large number of named provinces make a
feudal reading especially plausible. Kan Guo has the look of a realm in
which local lordship is widespread, deeply rooted, and politically
normal. The crown is likely strongest in the core districts around
Hejian and the chief port complexes, while noble privilege survives more
visibly in the outlying interior and in older hereditary domains. This
does not make the kingdom unstable. On the contrary, Kan Guo's political
strength lies in the fact that these arrangements are familiar, legible,
and functional.

The kingdom's simplicity should not be mistaken for weakness. In a
regional field shaped by unusual institutions and postwar distortions, a
competent hereditary monarchy may be the most dangerous thing of all.

Fuz Guo

Within Kan Guo lies one conspicuous exception: the Duchy of Fuz Guo, a
small enclosed duchy whose continued distinctness preserves an older
layer of feudal history inside the kingdom. The cleanest interpretation
is not geometric but historical. Fuz Guo is understood as an old
privileged holdout, likely once a marcher or allied ducal house whose
chartered rights survived the later consolidation of Kan Guo around it.
As the kingdom expanded and rationalized, the duchy became enclosed by
later royal acquisitions, but its ancient status was never fully erased.

In constitutional terms, Fuz Guo is not a fully foreign state. Its duke
owes homage to the King of Kan Guo, and the duchy's existence is part of
the kingdom's internal feudal order rather than an external diplomatic
anomaly. Yet it remains unusually autonomous by comparison with ordinary
provincial lordships. Its own hereditary line, courts, and local
prestige distinguish it from the surrounding territories and signal that
Kan Guo, though broadly straightforward, is also old enough to contain
privileges the crown never found worth the trouble of destroying.

Fuz Guo's aristocracy is further set apart by its long-standing claim to
sorcerous blood. In earlier centuries the great houses of the duchy
appear to have produced genuinely formidable sorcerers with some
regularity, and that reputation contributed to the preservation of their
status. In the present age, however, the old blood has thinned. Powerful
sorcerers still arise on rare occasions, but much more commonly the line
produces only minor gifts, unstable talents, or no obvious magical
expression at all. The prestige of the blood remains stronger than its
average contemporary reality, but that prestige is still politically
useful. It helps explain why the duchy retains an aura of old dignity
out of proportion to its size.

More generally, sorcerous bloodlines in Eutheria appear to be
hereditary, but not reliably cultivable. They may be preserved, thinned,
or lost through lineage, but they do not breed true in any simple or
predictable fashion. Greatness may skip generations, vanish in one
branch, or reappear unexpectedly in another. For that reason, houses
such as those of Fuz Guo can guard the memory of mighty sorcerous
ancestry without being able to reproduce it at will.

**Economy**

Kan Guo's strength rests first on the land. Its productive lowlands,
river valleys, and green interior support a broad agrarian base capable
of sustaining a comparatively dense population. This is the foundation
of royal revenue, noble power, and military potential alike. The kingdom
is not merely a coast with a hinterland attached. It is an inhabited and
working country.

Its coast nonetheless matters greatly. Kan Guo possesses multiple usable
ports, more than Kaihui's current network, and is clearly better
positioned than some of its neighbors to convert coastal frontage into
practical advantage. Yet it still does not regarded as a pure maritime
hegemon. Sea access strengthens the kingdom, but does not define it
entirely. Kan Guo should therefore be understood as a land-rich monarchy
with real maritime competence rather than as a sea-state whose whole
fortune depends on trade alone.

This economic balance helps explain why Kan Guo is such a plausible
regional predator. It has enough agricultural depth to feed and sustain
itself, enough coastal infrastructure to project commercial and naval
presence, and enough internal cohesion to act when more constitutionally
awkward neighbors cannot.

Religion

Kan Guo's religious profile appears notably uniform. Its settlement
pattern is consistently associated with the Xinchang Deities, and there
is little sign of confessional fragmentation in the kingdom's current
urban distribution. At the same time, the kingdom does not seem to be
defined by monumental temple-centered urbanism. Religion is clearly
normal to public and private life, but not in a manner that requires a
forest of great civic shrines in every town.

This suggests a kingdom in which shared religion supports cohesion
without becoming the main drama of politics. Kan Guo does not appear to
spend much energy managing internal religious fracture. That alone is a
considerable advantage.

**Military Character**

Kan Guo is likely the most straightforwardly dangerous land power in
this immediate southeastern Valtheran cluster. Its military strength is
best imagined as that of a conventional feudal monarchy with real
agrarian backing, a usable coast, and enough towns and roads to mobilize
men and supplies with relative efficiency. It does not need to be
exotic. Levy forces, noble retinues, crown troops, coastal patrol
elements, and fortified local centers are enough to make it formidable.

Its maritime arm is likely credible rather than dominant: strong enough
for coastal defense, escort, and practical assertion of local sea-power,
but not the kind of force that would independently define the kingdom's
identity. The kingdom's real danger lies in the combination of food,
manpower, cohesion, and the ability to turn a normal feudal state to
aggressive purpose when circumstances permit.

**Foreign Relations**

Kan Guo's position gives it reason to watch Kaihui, Guan Guo, Han Guo,
and the southeastern littoral closely. Of these, Kaihui is perhaps the
most obvious object of strategic temptation. Kaihui's grain wealth,
horse-breeding plains, and constitutional awkwardness make it both
useful and vulnerable. The fact that Kaihui is important to the regional
food balance does not remove that temptation; it merely complicates the
costs of acting on it.

Toward Guan Guo, Kan Guo likely combines commercial calculation with
measured wariness. Guan's river-gate position is valuable, but Guan is
also structurally specialized in a way that Kan is not. Toward Han Guo,
the relationship is likely shaped by the broad question of eastern
Valtheran balance rather than by a single defining corridor. Fuz Guo,
though enclosed, remains an internal concern rather than an external
one: a pocket of old privilege the crown must tolerate, bargain with, or
occasionally humble, but not a separate foreign power.

Political Tone

Kan Guo is valuable in the regional canon precisely because it is not
overwrought. It should be treated as a relatively ordinary but capable
feudal monarchy: crown, nobles, ports, fields, taxes, levies, rivalries,
and dynastic continuity. In a setting where several neighboring states
have become defined by postwar compromise, archaic clan structures, or
specialized geographic roles, Kan Guo stands out by being direct. It is
a kingdom that works in the expected way.

For that reason, the Kingdom of Kan Guo is understood as a dense,
fertile, and coast-facing monarchy whose strength lies in functional
normality. It is old enough to contain one preserved magical duchy,
proud enough to trust hereditary rule, and cohesive enough to make that
inheritance matter. In the southeastern politics of eastern Valthera,
that may be more dangerous than novelty.

**Chapter 62: The See of Xin Guo**

The See of Xin Guo is a small Tengcian clerical state on the southeastern
seaboard of eastern Valthera, facing the western waters of Oceanus
Centralis. In territorial terms it is a remnant. In cultural and
institutional terms it is one of the most influential states in the
region. The See is the surviving rump of the old Kingdom of Xin Guo,
once the great Valtheran naval challenger to Likia. That kingdom was
destroyed in the long maritime struggle of the middle centuries of the
Likian Calendar and replaced, under the peace settlement, by a reduced
clerical state forbidden from restoring its navy. The current See is
therefore not simply a smaller Xin Guo. It is a polity built out of
defeat, grievance, religious authority, and disciplined adaptation.

**Geography**

The See lies south and southeast of Kaihui and east of Kan Guo, amid the
broken coastal channels and islands of eastern Valthera's southeastern
seaboard. It does not command a major river gate or a dominant bay in
the manner of Guan Guo, nor does it possess the broad agrarian interior
of Kaihui or Kan Guo. Its core territory consists of a relatively small
main island and a handful of outlying insular possessions and attached
enclaves. The maps make the state's present condition plain: it still
faces the sea on nearly every side, but it no longer possesses the depth
or breadth needed to turn that position into maritime supremacy.

The terrain is mostly low and workable, with modest uplands rather than
great interior barriers. Its coast is deeply indented and naturally
maritime, but that maritime character is now defensive and connective
rather than imperial. The See is not a sea-power in the old sense, but a
sea-born remnant whose geography still fosters communication, mobility,
and outward awareness.

**Historical Development**

The old Kingdom of Xin Guo was once the dominant Valtheran naval rival
to Likia. Its fleets, ports, and maritime reach made it the principal
eastern counterweight to Likian dominance until the long war turned
decisively against it. In the settlement that followed, Likia did not
merely exact tribute or seize harbors. It abolished the Kingdom
entirely, forbade naval restoration, and required that a new order
replace it.

What Likia imposed was the end of the kingdom, not the mature internal
structure of the See. The clerical state that exists in 1026 LC was
built afterward by Xin Guo's surviving religious and administrative
elites. In the generations following defeat, they transformed a
humiliated remnant into a disciplined church-state whose ambitions could
no longer be pursued by fleet and conquest. The result was the See of
Xin Guo: a polity that preserved memory, doctrine, and institutional
continuity while redirecting the old outward impulse into religion,
education, diplomacy, and intrigue.

For that reason, the See is not a foreign-designed constitutional
puppet. It is better understood as Xin Guo's own answer to catastrophic
defeat: an internally reconstructed clerical remnant forced to accept
naval impotence while refusing to surrender influence.

**Political and Ecclesiastical Order**

The See is a true church-state rather than a monarchy draped in pious
language. Its public order is built around clergy, doctrinal offices,
educational institutions, and a disciplined administrative hierarchy.
The core of the state is not an aristocratic court but an ecclesiastical
apparatus: archives, schools, canonical offices, diplomatic houses,
clerical appointments, and territorial units defined in religious rather
than merely secular terms.

At the summit of this order stands the **Zhadrin**, the supreme hierarch
of the See. The office is not simply administrative. The Zhadrin is
expected, when possible, to be a genuinely miracle-working cleric of the
Xinchang faith — a figure of real sanctity and divine authorization
rather than merely a senior priest or functionary. Yet divine power
alone does not automatically confer the office. A true cleric is
necessary but not sufficient. The senior clergy must also recognize the
candidate as spiritually fit to bear the See.

This principle emerged after the creation of the See itself and reflects
the remnant state's own internal development. It exists to prevent the
mere possession of sacred power from automatically entitling an
unworthy, unstable, or morally compromised figure to rule. In times when
no true and spiritually fit cleric can be recognized, authority reverts
provisionally to the **Khashung**, the senior clerical council and
bureaucratic governing body of the state.

The Khashung anchors the continuity of the See. It supervises
administration, appointments, correspondence, foreign representation,
education, archives, and much of the practical machinery of governance.
This gives the See an unusual dual structure: at its height, it is led
by a genuinely holy ruler; in ordinary operation, it is sustained by one
of the most disciplined clerical bureaucracies in the known world.

**Clergy and Education**

Across much of eastern Valthera, clergy serve as the principal
educators. This gives the See influence far beyond its own borders. Its
schools, tutors, texts, and trained clergy shape literate culture across
many Xinchang communities, especially in courts, capitals, and towns
where written records, formal instruction, and polished religious
language matter.

This influence is never absolute, because what clergy may teach is
ordinarily regulated by the state under which they serve. Yet that
limitation has not prevented the See from building a remarkable network
of educational presence. Its strength lies not in forcing every foreign
population into obedience, but in shaping the people who become scribes,
officials, tutors, urban notables, and respectable interpreters of
religious life.

This is one of the See's great adaptive successes. Stripped of fleets,
it learned to project power through the formation of minds.

Diplomacy, Intrigue, and Soft Power

The priest-diplomats of the See are among the most distinguished in the
world. They are renowned for discipline, memory, discretion, and
linguistic polish, and they have helped make the See disproportionately
influential relative to its territory and population. Courts that would
never fear the See militarily often treat its envoys with exceptional
seriousness.

The same channels that sustain this diplomatic prestige also sustain an
extensive intelligence network. The See's intrigue does not take the
form of a separate rogue apparatus detached from ordinary ecclesiastical
life. Rather, it grows out of clerical correspondence, educational
houses, mission networks, port contacts, archives, confessional ties,
and long-practiced diplomatic habits. For that reason, the See's
knowledge of foreign courts and settlements is often deeper than
outsiders realize.

Its soft power is strongest in places where Xinchang communities already
exist and where literacy, schooling, and formal religious culture have
social prestige. It is weaker in societies whose religion remains mainly
household-centered, oral, or locally customary.

**Religion and Mission**

The state religion of the See is the cult of the **Xinchang Deities**,
but in Xin itself that faith has become more than inherited devotion. It
is a public, socially organizing, and institutionally supervised order.
The See does not treat religion as one private custom among many. It
treats the faith as the framework through which society should be
disciplined, educated, and bound together.

This distinguishes the See sharply from states such as Kaihui, where the
same religion survives primarily as household observance, family
tradition, and local custom rather than as a strongly socialized public
system. The See has long sought to transform such looser forms of
Xinchang practice into something more publicly organized and morally
binding. In Kaihui especially, it has tried to socialize the faith and
use education to deepen influence in a society otherwise resistant to
outside clerical reshaping.

Those efforts have met only partial success. In Kaihui's capital, the
See has won a degree of courtesy, elite imitation, and polite formal
deference. Across the wider republic, and especially in the plains, its
influence remains limited. Kaihui's older household-centered tradition,
its broad indifference to literacy outside elite use, and its stubbornly
local social order have blunted the See's ambition. The See receives lip
service there more often than obedience.

**Economy**

The See is not materially powerful in the ordinary territorial sense. It
is too small, too insular, and too restricted in maritime development to
be counted among the richer agrarian or mercantile states of eastern
Valthera. Its economy is modest at home and strong in connection.

Its resources likely derive from a combination of coastal farming,
fishing, clerical estates, port tolls, copying and educational services,
pilgrimage or devotional traffic, donations from foreign Xinchang
communities, and the institutional wealth generated by its schools,
archives, and diplomatic houses. The See's chief export is not grain or
timber but trained people, authoritative texts, religious legitimacy,
and access to transregional networks of information.

In that sense, the See remains poor in land and rich in reach.

**Military Character**

The See has no place among the naval powers of the age. That is the most
important negative fact about it. Its old kingdom was broken precisely
to prevent such a restoration, and its reduced territory gives it little
basis for conventional military ambition even if the legal restraints
were ignored.

Yet the See is not powerless. It retains fortified centers, defensible
ports, and enough administrative cohesion to protect itself locally. Its
real strength, however, is not military. It lies in diplomacy, doctrine,
education, confessional coordination, and intelligence. The See cannot
compel its larger neighbors by arms, but it can embarrass them,
influence their educated classes, coordinate opinion among the faithful,
and insert itself into crises well beyond its borders.

**Foreign Relations**

The See's relationship with Likia is shaped by memory, caution, and
cold-blooded realism. Likia destroyed the old kingdom and forced the end
of Xin Guo as a naval power, but it did not dictate the mature internal
structure of the successor state. The present church-state is Xin's own
postwar creation. For that reason, the See regards Likia not simply as a
past conqueror, but as the power that permanently denied it the most
natural form of its former greatness.

The See's relationship with Kaihui is one of frustrated opportunity. It
sees a Xinchang population living outside a disciplined public
confessional order and would like to draw Kaihui more firmly into its
moral and educational orbit. It has achieved only limited success, most
clearly in the capital and among educated circles.

With the broader Xinchang world, the See seeks something more ambitious:
to become the acknowledged arbiter of orthodoxy, the preferred source of
trained clergy, and the center to which educated believers and
Xinchang-speaking courts must look for standards, mediation, and refined
doctrinal authority. This ambition gives the See influence, but it also
makes other states wary. Many welcome See-trained clergy or priest-
diplomats while resisting any deeper submission to Xin's moral
supervision.

Ordinary Life and Political Tone

Ordinary life in the See is likely more disciplined, literate, and
publicly ritualized than in most neighboring states. Clergy are visible.
Schools matter. Records matter. Public observance matters. The state is
small enough that its institutions are felt closely, and its people live
under the shadow of a collective memory that never entirely forgets that
their homeland was once greater than it is now.

This gives the See a distinctive emotional character. It is proud, but
in a wounded way. It is devout, but not naively so. It is serious,
watchful, and permanently aware that it is the remnant of something that
once commanded fleets and now commands mainly minds.

For that reason, the See of Xin Guo is understood as a humiliated
maritime theocracy that reinvented itself as a clerical power of
education, diplomacy, and soft control. Likia destroyed Xin Guo's navy,
but not its reach. The See replaced the fleet with clergy, envoys, and
informants, and in doing so made itself small in territory but
formidable in influence.


**Chapter 63: The Marches of Kai Guo**

The Marches of Kai Guo are a small Tengcian littoral polity on eastern
Valthera's southeastern seaboard, positioned at a strategically
sensitive coastal hinge between Kaihui, Kan Guo, Yan, and the Xin-facing
offshore world. They are not understood as an island state, nor as an
ordinary hereditary marchland. Their significance lies in their control
of the sea outlet of a southern watershed shared by borderlands of both
Kaihui and Kan Guo. This watershed is distinct from the larger northern
river system more central to Kaihui's broader state geography. Kai Guo
therefore does not command Kaihui's inland movement in general, but it
does govern one important southern corridor where protected inland
waters meet the open sea.

The Marches are politically unusual because they are not ruled by a
monarch, duke, or popular assembly. Instead they are governed by a small
bureaucratic council under a long-standing treaty order designed to keep
the outlet neutral, navigable, and defensible. Their pride rests in this
arrangement. Kai Guo regards itself as proof that rival powers can
submit a shared necessity to law and procedure rather than conquest.
That pride, however, is inseparable from caution, for the Marches know
that their survival depends on remaining useful, balanced, and competent
enough that neither Kaihui nor Kan Guo is tempted to unmake the
settlement.

**Geography**

Kai Guo occupies a broken littoral zone of peninsulas, narrow channels,
coastal lobes, and sheltered approaches along the southeastern edge of
eastern Valthera. Its capital, Yanhuaxing, sits at the fortified outlet
harbor where the southern watershed opens toward Oceanus Centralis. The
Marches also hold a secondary maritime node at Jingzhou, giving them
more than one working point of access to the sea, though not enough
scale to constitute a major maritime power.

The maps make clear that Kai Guo's importance is positional rather than
territorial. It is small in area, modest in population, and lacks the
broad agrarian depth of Kaihui or Kan Guo. Yet its waters are narrow,
local, and difficult to ignore. Whoever manages them can tax, protect,
delay, inspect, or deny movement between the southern inland watershed
and the seaboard. That function gives the Marches leverage out of
proportion to their size.

The coast itself is low to moderately elevated, green, and workable in
places, but it does not support a great inland surplus. Kai Guo is thus
best seen as a threshold state rather than a territorial one: a place of
outlets, crossings, beacons, patrol routes, customs stations, and harbor
records.

**Historical Development**

The Marches of Kai Guo were not created at the end of the Likian-Xin
War. Their council-governed order predates the end of that conflict by
roughly seventy-five years. The arrangement appears to have developed
from the practical need to keep the southern outlet neutral in a region
where both Kaihui and Kan Guo had legitimate interests but neither could
safely be trusted with full unilateral control.

Kan Guo in particular had reason to support the formalization of the
Marches. A neutralized outlet prevented Kaihui from turning the southern
watershed into a wholly Kaihui-controlled strategic asset, while also
avoiding the instability that direct Kan annexation might have produced.
Over time, what may once have been a more local littoral march hardened
into a treaty-regulated administrative regime.

By the end of the Likian-Xin War, the essential structure was already in
place. Likia therefore had strong reason to leave the arrangement
intact. To disturb it too aggressively risked provoking Kan Guo into the
war at a moment when Kan's entry might have altered the balance of the
conflict. The war did not create Kai Guo; it confirmed the strategic
value of a system already designed to keep the southern outlet from
becoming the uncontested possession of either neighboring power.

**Political Order**

Kai Guo is governed by the Council of the Marches, a body of thirteen
councilors. This number is regarded as large enough to dilute the power
of any one councilor while remaining small enough for practical
government. The detailed composition of the council is administrative
rather than aristocratic in character. The Marches are therefore best
understood as a bureaucratic treaty polity, not a miniature feudal
court.

The council governs all ordinary internal affairs of the Marches. These
include local customs administration, toll collection within established
limits, policing, minor legal matters, pilotage, fishing regulation,
routine maintenance, harbor management, and the ordinary supervision of
traffic through the outlet.

At the opening of each twelve-month session, the council chooses a
Speaker. The Speaker is first nominated by lot from among the eligible
councilors and then confirmed by majority vote of the council. If a
nominee is rejected, another eligible name is drawn and the process is
repeated until one is confirmed. The office is intentionally procedural.
The Speaker presides over the current session, manages order and
records, and represents the council ceremonially, but holds no
independent executive authority.

Kaihui and Kan Guo each maintain a treaty-recognized representative on
the council with veto power over Reserved Matters. These representatives
are not ordinary councilors and do not govern the daily life of the
Marches. Their function is to prevent strategic drift. Reserved Matters
include questions such as fortifications, military ceilings, foreign
agreements, major toll or access changes, constitutional reforms,
strategic transfer of land or harbor rights, and other measures capable
of upsetting the balance at the outlet.

The veto procedure is exact. If both representatives veto a Reserved
Matter, the proposal fails absolutely. If only one representative vetoes
it, the matter is returned to review and may thereafter pass only by the
unanimous vote of the council. In law, one veto delays while two
destroy. In practice, no veto returned for review has ever been
overturned.

**Identity and Political Tone**

Kai Guo prides itself on being a paragon of diplomatic success. In its
own civic imagination, the Marches are not merely a buffer or
compromise, but evidence of what can occur when rival states accept law,
records, and shared stewardship in place of conquest. This gives the
Marches a quiet but real institutional self-respect. Their officials
tend to value calm, precision, procedure, and non-provocation, and
public life likely places unusual moral weight on competence.

That pride is always accompanied by trepidation. The people and officers
of Kai Guo know that they live under the attention of two larger powers
whose interests do not disappear. The Marches therefore cultivate a
political culture of exactitude. To keep the treaty functioning is not
merely good administration. It is the condition of continued existence.

**Economy**

Kai Guo's economy rests chiefly on fish and passage. The coastal and
riverine environment supports fishing as the primary economic activity,
along with fish processing, salting, drying, ropework, netmaking, boat
service trades, and related maritime labor. These industries provide the
Marches with their most ordinary and reliable material basis.

The council's treasury, however, depends heavily on the administration
of the outlet itself. Tolls, customs, transit fees, pilotage,
warehousing, and the maintenance of navigable approaches form the second
pillar of the Marches' economy. Kai Guo is therefore not a rich
producing state in the Kaihui or Kan Guo sense. It is a service-heavy
choke-point polity whose relevance comes from the combination of useful
waters and disciplined administration.

Local agriculture exists, but only modestly, and is best seen as
supportive rather than defining. The Marches live less by inland surplus
than by coastal labor and controlled movement.

**Military Character**

The Marches maintain no large field army and no true blue-water navy.
The council is instead responsible for internal security and legally
bound to maintain a minimum defensive capability adequate to preserve
the neutrality and operability of the outlet.

That defensive capability is centered on coastal and river patrol
forces. These entities police the channels, secure the narrows, watch
for smuggling or unauthorized military movement, and protect traffic
through the southern watershed route. Fortified crossings, harbor
defenses, beacons, small garrisons, and patrol craft are the practical
instruments of Kai Guo's security. In effect, the Marches function as a
treaty-bound coast-guard march rather than as a war state.

**Religion and Public Life**

Kai Guo appears culturally and religiously cohesive. Its settlements are
Tengcian and Xinchang, and nothing in the current sources suggests deep
internal confessional fracture. Yet the public tone of the Marches is
not that of a highly demonstrative religious polity. Their
administrative identity, treaty obligations, and procedural ethos likely
encourage a restrained public culture in which religion is ordinary and
inherited but not theatrically foregrounded in statecraft.

This does not make the Marches irreligious. It makes them careful. A
state that survives by balancing two neighbors and administering a
shared outlet has good reason to keep public life orderly, legible, and
difficult to weaponize.

**Foreign Relations**

Kai Guo's relations with Kaihui and Kan Guo are the central facts of its
external existence. Kaihui needs reliable access from the southern
watershed to the sea but cannot safely be allowed to dominate the
outlet. Kan Guo benefits from the same neutrality and has long treated
the balance there as strategically important. Both powers therefore
sustain and constrain the Marches at the same time.

Other neighbors must treat Kai Guo according to its position. Yan cannot
ignore a polity that sits across narrow waters and manages a sensitive
littoral threshold. The See of Xin Guo and the broader Xin-facing
maritime world likewise encounter Kai Guo less as a great power than as
a careful guardian of crossings, tolls, and local maritime order.

For that reason, the Marches of Kai Guo are understood as a small but
durable littoral march whose importance lies not in broad wealth or
military power, but in the disciplined management of a strategically
sensitive outlet that larger neighbors cannot safely allow one another
to possess outright.

**Chapter 64: The Duchy of Yan**

Overview

The Duchy of Yan is a small Tengcian Xinchang coastal state on eastern
Valthera's southeastern seaboard. It is a legitimate but minor duchy,
whose scale is modest enough that it has never ranked among the decisive
powers of the southeastern littoral. Its survival owes less to force of
arms than to caution, cohesion, and the habit of minding its own
business. Yan's ruling house emphasizes the dignity, continuity, and
antiquity of the duchy chiefly for domestic purposes. At home, its
ceremonial self-regard reinforces loyalty and preserves the memory of an
older and greater past. Abroad, the same house is notably restrained and
takes care not to provoke stronger neighbors.

**Historical Origin**

Yan is the remnant of an older kingdom now lost to time. The name and
full history of that earlier realm survive only dimly, but the ducal
house traces its legitimacy to the fact that, when the kingdom itself
vanished, this one coastal remnant endured. That persistence has become
a central element of Yan's internal self-understanding. The duchy thinks
of itself not as a new creation, but as the surviving heir of something
older than its present scale would suggest.

This older pedigree helps explain the difference between Yan's domestic
and foreign tone. Within the duchy, the court speaks and acts with the
formal dignity of a house preserving ancient station. Beyond its
borders, it behaves with deliberate caution. Yan has neither the wealth
nor the force to test its prestige against larger neighbors, and it has
survived in part because it has never made that mistake.

The Likian-Xin War passed around rather than through Yan. The duchy was
small, locally cohesive, and of too little strategic consequence to
justify major intervention by the great powers. It therefore survived
the conflict substantially intact, not because it shaped its outcome,
but because it remained too modest and too self-contained to become
worth unmaking.

**Geography**

Yan occupies a small stretch of the southeastern Valtheran coast facing
the western waters of Oceanus Centralis. Its territory has only limited
depth. The duchy's life is therefore organized around one fortified
harbor capital and a modest inland settlement spine rather than around a
broad interior. The current settlement pattern reflects this clearly.
Dengzhou is the capital, the sole true port, and the military anchor of
the state. Renhuang and Jianyang form the main inland support zone, and
the remaining settlements are minor by comparison.

Yan's geography makes it a shore duchy rather than a maritime power. It
profits from fishing, harbor life, and local coastal movement, but it is
not structured to dominate wider trade. Nor does it control the major
choke points of the region. It lies close enough to the broken littoral
world of Kai Guo, Kaihui, and the Xin-facing coast to be affected by it,
but not in a position to command it.

**Government and Political Tone**

Yan is a conventional hereditary duchy. Its ruler, court, and local
lords appear to operate in a broadly ordinary feudal manner without the
constitutional peculiarities seen in Kaihui, Kai Guo, or the See of Xin
Guo. This simplicity is one of the reasons the duchy remains legible and
stable. Yan's political institutions do not seek to be innovative. Their
purpose is continuity.

The court's ceremonial self-importance should not be misunderstood. Yan
does not present itself to the wider world as the equal of larger
neighbors, nor does it court humiliation by acting above its station.
Its rhetoric of dignity and old status is directed primarily toward its
own subjects and retainers. In this respect the duchy is proud at home
and cautious abroad.

**Economy**

Yan's economy is chiefly that of a fishing and harbor duchy. Fishing,
fish processing, small-scale coastal shipping, harbor service trades,
and modest inland agriculture provide its material basis. Dengzhou is
less a great mercantile city than a secure working port around which the
ducal state is anchored.

The duchy likely gains some secondary benefit from traffic moving
through the broader southeastern littoral, but it does not live by toll
mastery or strategic transit in the way Kai Guo does. Nor does it
possess the interior productivity of Kaihui or Kan Guo. Its economy
should therefore be imagined as modest, stable, and local in character.

**Military Character**

Yan's military strength is limited but appropriate to its needs. The
walls and citadel of Dengzhou indicate that the ducal house takes port
defense seriously and cannot afford to lose its one major harbor. The
duchy's forces are understood as a combination of ducal retainers,
harbor guards, coastal watchmen, and a few patrol craft. Yan is better
suited to holding its own coast than to projecting force beyond it.

**Religion and Public Life**

Yan is conventionally Xinchang in religion. Its public religious life
appears cohesive and ordinary rather than doctrinally intense or
politically experimental. In this respect Yan stands between Kaihui and
the See of Xin Guo. It is not notably private and household-centered in
the Kaihui manner, but neither is it eager to place itself under the
clerical ambitions of the See. Yan's religious culture is inherited,
local, and generally content to remain so.

**Foreign Relations**

Yan maintains cordial but not extensive relations with its neighbors. It
trades where useful, avoids entanglement where possible, and gives no
surrounding power strong reason to treat it as a problem. No major
neighbor has a particular quarrel with Yan, which is itself part of the
duchy's survival strategy.

For that reason, the Duchy of Yan is understood as a small but proper
coastal state: locally proud, regionally modest, and durable chiefly
because it has learned how to preserve its dignity without forcing
stronger neighbors to take notice.


**Part XI: Western Maritime Nereth — Lienia**

**Chapter 65: The Kingdom of Lienia**

The Kingdom of Lienia is the stable gateway monarchy of western Nereth:
a Lienian-led maritime kingdom whose strength lies in turning oceanic
trade into inland influence while ruling a broad and unevenly integrated
territorial body. It is one of the two dominant western powers of
Nereth, paired most often with the Republic of Sinz, but it occupies a
different role within the western order. Where Sinz is more overtly
aggressive, commercially hard-edged, and territorially assertive, Lienia
projects steadiness, continuity, and the authority of a durable crown
that has successfully bound several regional faces into a single realm.

Lienia's importance begins with geography. Bolcescast, the royal
capital, stands at the estuarial throat of the kingdom's capital basin,
where movement can be watched, taxed, and organized with unusual
efficiency. That estuary is the principal customs and administrative
hinge of the realm, but it does not by itself explain the kingdom's
power. Lienia is not merely a port-state attached to a capital harbor.
Its wider strength comes from a long articulated coastline, a chain of
secondary ports and coastal towns, and a broad inland body that feeds,
staffs, and connects the maritime-commercial system. It is therefore
understood as a maritime monarchy whose identity is coastal and
estuarial, but whose reality is deeply territorial.

The kingdom is culturally composite. Lienian culture provides the
crown's public face and dominates the main coastal-commercial core, but
Lienia is not a flat Lienian polity. A smaller Westric rural and
upland belt survives inland, judged by mainstream Lienian society as
sturdy, old-fashioned, and somewhat backward. More politically important
is the substantial Sinzian bloc of the south and southwest. This is not a
mere scattering of merchants. It is a real territorial complex of towns,
ports, and cross-border horizons facing Sinz. In urban and mercantile
circles that bloc has repeatedly generated autonomist or pro-Sinz
sympathies, though rural Sinzian populations tend to be more localist and
pragmatic. The resulting "Sinzian question" is the kingdom's most serious
internal cultural-political problem.

Religiously, Lienia is broadly Skrosenist in its Lienian and Westric
zones, a pattern that helps explain its ease of connection to the wider
northwestern maritime world without making it a Flandric state. Stronger
Sinzist patterns survive in the Sinzian south and southwest. Religion
therefore gives the kingdom regional texture and identity, but does not
produce the sort of legitimacy crisis seen in the post-Veltric east.

Skrosenism’s institutional life in Lienia is managed through the
**Hallmaster** — an ecumenical council based in Bolcescast that oversees
temple construction, appoints regional head-priests, and manages
significant clerical disputes. The council’s presiding chair is the
**High Render**, selected by the crown from among the Hallmaster’s
membership. Regional head-priests hold the title of **Aldrender** and
are appointed by the Hallmaster to significant regional centers.
Parish-level clergy are called **Renders**. The title designates a
religious office rather than a character class; not all Renders are
spellcasting clerics, and actual clerics at the parish level are
comparatively rare. The civil and religious authority chains are
structurally separate: the reeve has no role in religious appointment,
and the Aldrender has no authority over civil administration.

How Aldrender appointments translate into rural parish practice varies
considerably across the kingdom. In urban centers and major ports the
institutional structure is active and present. In remote inland regions
the process by which a parish Render comes to hold their position may
owe as much to community custom and local lord’s preference as to formal
Hallmaster sanction.

Politically, Lienia is a crown-centered but unevenly reaching monarchy.
It is neither a simplistic feudal patchwork nor a modern bureaucracy.
Its institutions are strongest where direct control yields clear
returns: Bolcescast, the principal ports, major roads, customs points,
and politically sensitive mixed districts. Much of the inland kingdom,
however, is governed largely through lesser gentry, settlement elites,
priests, reeves, stewards, and customary local order rather than through
dense royal presence. Lienia exemplifies the broader Eutherian pattern
of preindustrial sophistication: a state that is politically intelligent
and commercially integrated, but geographically uneven in reach.

Economically, Lienia has benefited more than most western states from
the post-Veltric trade realignment. Likian goods now land first in the
west and move inland toward the Dwarven passes. Bolcescast captures
customs and warehousing at the estuarial throat, while the kingdom's
secondary coasts, coastal shipping, fisheries, inland roads, and market
networks turn maritime arrival into broader regional advantage. Lienia
is therefore not simply a coastline that happens to own a capital. It is
a maritime-commercial crown built on a large inland support body.

The social tone of the kingdom follows the same geography. At the nodes,
Lienia is cosmopolitan: merchants, customs officers, ship-service
trades, chartered town elites, and noble households close to court
define public life. Away from the nodes, much of the realm is localist
and customary. Villagers know the crown as law, peace, dues, and distant
authority rather than as daily administration. This makes Lienia feel
politically sophisticated without feeling modern.

Its security follows a similar logic. Lienia should not be imagined as a
realm that saturates every district with patrols. Harbor and customs
security are strong where trade matters most; protection on the trunk
roads between key settlements is reliable enough to keep large disorder
unattractive; and strategic nodes are selectively fortified. Yet broad
stretches of the inland body and several secondary coasts remain more
thinly watched. Lienia's peace is therefore understood as corridor
peace: real and effective at high-value nodes, thinner in the seams.

In foreign affairs Lienia's most important relationship is with Likia,
whose maritime system it helps translate into western continental power.
Its second defining relationship is with Sinz, the kingdom's principal
western competitor. Lienia is commercially open, politically cautious,
and acutely attentive to the balance of western power. Its own
self-image is accordingly grand but not wholly false: civilized,
indispensable, and broader and steadier than its rivals, a kingdom that
prides itself on turning geography into order and prosperity.

**Chapter 66: The Regional Faces of Lienia**

Lienia is most usefully understood not as a uniform coastal kingdom, but
as a realm of several distinct and interlocking regional faces.

The first is the **Bolcescast estuary core**, the royal-commercial heart
of the kingdom. Here crown authority is most direct. Customs,
warehousing, diplomacy, maritime law, and high mercantile activity are
concentrated in and around the estuarial throat.

Beyond it lies the **northern and western secondary coast**, an older
Lienian maritime belt of lesser ports and coastal towns. This zone is
economically important but less intensely supervised than the capital
basin. Crown presence remains real, though often mediated by local
elites, town authorities, and selective corridor protection rather than
constant direct management.

The **Sinzian southern and southwestern littoral** forms the kingdom's
most politically sensitive internal region. Its Sinzian towns and ports
matter too much economically and strategically to surrender, yet their
urban elites are also the most likely to imagine alternatives to
Lienian-led crown rule. Rural populations in the same belt are usually
more pragmatic and less ideological, but the region as a whole remains a
standing internal problem for the monarchy.

A related but distinct face is the **southern border-facing coast**, a
working shore oriented toward lower-prestige cross-border traffic,
coastal exchange, and practical maritime economies rather than royal
display. Crown oversight here is lighter and more stability-oriented
than in the Bolcescast core.

Behind these coasts lies the **Lienian inland body**, the agricultural
and demographic backbone of the kingdom. This broad interior supports
the maritime system through food, transport, labor, and inland market
exchange. Much of Lienia's ordinary life is lived here rather than in
its harbors.

Within that interior sits the **Westric rural and upland belt**, a
smaller but meaningful cultural zone characterized by localism,
conservatism, and lighter engagement in wider state politics. The crown
is content to leave it relatively quiet so long as it remains
productive.

Finally, Lienia contains broad **interior low-control seams**: regions
that are fully within the kingdom yet distant from the nodes the crown
values most. In such places patrol density is lower, local authority
matters more, and problems often remain local unless they threaten
revenue, roads, or prestige. These seams are the most plausible setting
for recurring humanoid pressure, local feuds, neglected shrines, road
predation, and adventure-scale danger.

Taken together, these regional faces explain how Lienia can be both a
strong and sophisticated western monarchy and a kingdom whose lived
experience differs sharply from one district to another. It is strongest
at nodes, weaker in seams, and held together by the crown's success in
making those differences function within one order.

**Chapter 67: Dudbury**

Dudbury is a broad inland Lienian region of Lienia and the clearest
example of the kingdom's interior low-control seams. It is loyal,
recognizably civilized, and fully part of the kingdom in law, tax, and
symbolic order, but it does not belong to the capital's dense
commercial-bureaucratic world. Dudbury is therefore understood as a
region in which the kingdom is real but distant in daily practice.

The capital of the region is **Malton**, with **Olbore** serving as the
secondary inland or upland town. Malton should not be imagined as part
of the immediate capital basin of Bolcescast. It is a far-inland
regional center tied to the crown by roads, law, and hierarchy, yet
provincially removed from the assumptions of the estuarial-commercial
core. This relationship matters tonally. Dudbury is not outside the
kingdom, but it is far enough from royal priorities that local
institutions usually absorb local crises first.

Dudbury is very large, sparse, and only lightly urbanized. Its roads,
villages, and market links are real, but the region should not feel
dense, polished, or heavily patrolled. Most of the land between towns
belongs more to local society than to active state supervision. The
ordinary political assumption of the region is simple: if Dudbury has a
problem, Dudbury handles it. Counts, gentry, reeves, sheriffs, priests,
and village headmen matter far more to daily life than distant royal
officers. Royal intervention remains possible, but it should feel
exceptional rather than routine.

Dudbury is also a useful benchmark for the kingdom's threat ecology. Its
long integration into Lienia prevents the existence of large openly held
humanoid polities, yet its size, sparse settlement, and thin patrol
coverage leave room for persistent hostile pockets. Goblins are the most
characteristic recurring humanoid threat. They survive as bands, hidden
warrens, road-feeding raiders, and opportunistic local predators rather
than as a rival state. Kobolds are also plausible in more localized
pockets tied to earthworks, old workings, or stream-cut ground.
Hobgoblins and bugbears remain rare and usually indicate unusual
organization or escalating danger.

The concealment terrain that supports such pressures is varied rather
than singular. Dudbury's dangers most plausibly survive in upland folds,
broken hill-country, woodland pockets and secondary forests, ravines,
old tracks, disused route systems, abandoned shrines, ruins, burial
places, old workings, and the wide rural seams between villages, manors,
and official patrol routes. As a result, the region's trouble most often
takes the form of stock theft, attacks on isolated crofts and hamlets,
minor-road predation, poaching and extortion in thinly watched woods,
and disappearances at shrines, ruins, and remote farms. These are crises
serious enough to matter greatly to villagers, but too small to draw
fast royal attention.

Large separations between Dudbury's settlements should not be
misregarded as proof of empty land. The settlement layer records the
region's higher-order urban and market network, not a full census of
meaningful settlement. The land between Malton and Olbore is therefore
better imagined as a mesh of parish-service villages, lesser hamlets,
manor clusters, mill-sites, granges, isolated crofts, shepherd holdings,
woodcutters' dwellings, and other dependent places. The distance between
settlements can remain large while the distance between ordinary
inhabited sites is much smaller.

Dudbury's importance within the kingdom is therefore twofold. It is at
once a loyal inland support body for Lienia's maritime-commercial order
and a region where local heroes matter before the crown does. Among all
Lienian regions, it is one of the clearest places in which the kingdom's
strength and its limits can be seen together.

**Chapter 68: Thornwick**

Thornwick is a substantial parish-green village of roughly three hundred
and fifty people in eastern Dudbury. It is not a settlement and should
not be treated as a true town, market borough, or urban node. Instead it
is the head-village of its immediate district: the dominant local parish
and service settlement, large enough to gather worship, repair, storage,
gossip, and customary authority without becoming urban. No nearby
settlement rivals it in parish gravity or local centrality.

The village is best imagined as a settlement-on-a-green. Its broad
communal center gathers together the parish temple, a covered well, a
community building, an inn, a smithy, and stabling serving the wider
district, with a road-ribbon of cottages, sheds, and lesser households
extending away from the core. A mill sits at a distance to the
southwest. Thornwick should feel useful, muddy, lived-in, and socially
legible rather than fortified or picturesque.

Its function is to serve a surrounding ring of farms, crofts, shepherd
holdings, wood-edge households, and dependent hamlets. People come there
for worship, milling, smithing and repairs, ale and lodging, hiring,
road news, and disputes too large for a hamlet to settle alone. In that
sense Thornwick is the nearest place where the countryside becomes
organized.

**Authority and Governance**

Thornwick sits at the end of a four-level civil authority chain. The
Earl of Dudbury, **Aldric Vorne**, holds the broad region. Below him,
the Count of Malton, **Osbert Crale**, administers the regional capital
approximately one hundred miles to the west. The Baron of Harnwick,
**Godwin Harn**, holds the Thornwick district as a fief under Malton.
The Baron’s manor is located at **Alderfield**, several miles
west-northwest of the village. Baron Harn is rarely resident, preferring
the social scene at Malton; in his absence, his chief butler **Aldous
Wrenn** serves as the estate’s de facto administrator and the Baron’s
spokesperson throughout the district. Wrenn is the practical authority
for matters requiring the Baron’s name — competent, loyal, and
clear-eyed about the reality of his lord’s absenteeism.

The village reeve is **Edric Dole**, installed through political
obligation rather than merit. Count Crale appointed him as a favor to a
family connection; Dole holds the formal administrative position but
carries limited practical authority given the community’s awareness of
his origins. The village’s de facto marshal is **Garran Harn**, the
district smithy, a distant relation of the Baron whose family name
carries quiet social weight but whose genuine standing rests on competence
and reliability rather than blood connection. Garran’s workshop sits
adjacent to the village’s inn, the **Crooked Stag**, where he serves
informally as the establishment’s strong arm when occasion demands.

The civil and religious authority chains are structurally separate.
The parish Render answers to the Aldrender of Malton through the
Skrosenist institutional hierarchy, not to the reeve or the Baron.

**Religion**

Thornwick is a Skrosenist community whose primary devotional life
centers on **Halmor** (TN) — the full-cycle deity of nature, seasons,
birth, and death. The agricultural calendar organizes the parish year
around four seasonal festivals: Seedtide in early spring, Greenrest at
midsummer, Harvestclose in late autumn, and Darknight at midwinter.
Harvestclose is the most formally religious of the four; Darknight is
the most solemn, with the Render naming the year’s dead and commending
them to the Grey Shore.

The parish Render is **Dain**, a dwarven cleric of **Sigred** (NG) —
the deity of healing, mercy, and grief. Dain performs all parish rites
under the appropriate deity’s name, including Halmor’s seasonal and
funerary rites, while drawing his clerical power from Sigred. Having a
genuine spellcasting cleric as Render makes Thornwick better served than
many comparable rural parishes. The community addresses him as Father Dain by custom; his formal institutional title within the Skrosenist
hierarchy is Render.

The parish temple sits on the western edge of the green and maintains
shrine alcoves for multiple deities alongside its central Halmor
orientation. The Render keeps a secured collection of personal objects
entrusted to him by parishioners — the **Totem Entrust** — for use in
funerary rites when a body is unavailable. The totem serves as the
physical substitute for the body in Halmor’s custody transfer.

The district also maintains the memory of **Saint Cuthbert** — a hermit
woman, formerly local, mockingly called “saint” in life for her religious
zeal. She fell into the fissure at what is now called **St. Cuthbert’s
Well** and died there. Her ironic nickname fossilized into genuine local
tradition after her death, and the place bears her name.

**The District**

The district around Thornwick is old and settled, not wilderness-side.
Its immediate geography is an open cultivated core of fields, lanes,
pasture, and parish land, broken by low ridges, brooks, smaller woods,
and rougher margins. Stranger ground and the largest concealment zones
are concentrated toward the edges of the district rather than in the
village center. Other nearby named places are dependents rather than
peers: **Harrowfield** to the south-southeast, **Stonebrook** to the
southwest, **Oolding Crossing** at the river ford to the south,
**Alderfield** to the west-northwest, and **Stelton** to the south.

Thornwick’s danger profile follows Dudbury’s wider pattern of civilized
center and dangerous margin. The core around the village is worked and
recognizably ordered. Pressure comes from the edge-ring: larger woods,
broken ridges, abandoned workings, old burial or shrine landscapes,
forgotten paths, and under-patrolled seams between settled places.
**Duskmire Wood** is the district’s main concealment forest and the most
plausible center of recurring goblin pressure. **Old Quarry** functions
as an abandoned working and secondary danger-node. **Barrow Heath** is
older memorial ground whose unease lies as much in memory and taboo as
in ordinary utility.

Among these sites, none is more feared than **St. Cuthbert’s Well** — a
natural fissure opening into a wider cave system. Local tradition holds
that those caverns eventually reach the Underrealm. The surrounding
ground is held to be haunted, and even the goblins who occupy the
surrounding woods are said to avoid the Well and the ruined homestead
nearby. Cuthbert’s ruins stand as little more than a chimney stump and
a stone foundation.

Thornwick is therefore understood as an old parish-green head-village in
a settled Dudbury district: the only true local center in a mesh of
dependent hamlets and old named sites, secure enough at its core to feel
civilized, but ringed by woods, ruins, fissures, and forgotten ground
where danger persists.

**Chapter 69: Rombevenia**

Rombevenia remains a useful contrast to Dudbury within Lienia. It is a
small Lienian region centered on **Bermouth** and belongs to the greater
Bolcescast estuary world rather than to the kingdom's distant inland
body. It lies close enough to the capital basin that crown power, trade
movement, and royal assumptions are all real, yet it still stands
outside the densest administrative and commercial zone.

Bermouth is the region's practical market center and local organizing
node. Its roads and waterways tie Rombevenia into the wider estuarial
system, but the region remains secondary to Bolcescast itself. It is
therefore neither frontier nor polished core-country. Rather, it is a
place where integration is real but incomplete.

The characteristic problems of Rombevenia arise less from distance than
from under-prioritization. Locals expect the king's peace to matter more
than it actually does, and the region's troubles often take the form of
jurisdictional slippage, estuary and shoreline smuggling, road predation
below crisis scale, and local dangers that remain everybody's concern
but no one office's highest priority. Its creature pressure is
accordingly low-to-moderate overall, with stronger pockets in marginal
terrain.

Rombevenia is thus best regarded as an inner-estuary region of Lienia:
close enough to the kingdom's maritime core to belong fully to its
order, but secondary enough that shoreline, road, and jurisdictional
problems can still persist in the gaps beneath royal priority.


**Part XII: Western Maritime and Northern Littoral Nereth — Sinz, Fresen, Bramia, Garka, and Laups**

**Chapter 70: The Republic of Sinz**

The Republic of Sinz is the harder civic power of western Nereth: a
Sinzian-led oligarchic republic formed in 934 LC from the larger inland
body of the old western monarchy, whose strength lies in turning
territorial position, customs intelligence, and a composite internal
order into leverage over movement between Lienia’s maritime arrival
zone, the Dwarven mountain approaches, and the habitable western fringe
of the continent. It is one of the two dominant western powers of the
region, paired most often with the Kingdom of Lienia, but it occupies a
different role within the western order. Where Lienia presents itself as
a steadier crown translating maritime arrival into inland prosperity,
Sinz appears sharper, more legalistic, more commercially aggressive, and
more ready to turn geography into bargaining power.

**Geography**

Sinz’s importance begins with its shape and biome position. Its core sits
in grassland and savanna — grain and pastoral country rather than the
mixed temperate forest agriculture earlier summaries implied. Hot desert
presses against its northern edge; tropical seasonal forest begins to
the south; and the northeastern salient runs into temperate deciduous
upland near the approaches to the mountain system. The republic is
therefore not best imagined as a dense mercantile mesh spread evenly
across the map. It is a broad territorial body governed through selected
towns, trunk roads, provincial bargains, and strategically valuable
nodes, with large stretches of rural interior that remain lightly
urbanized and locally textured.

The mountain barrier separating Sinz from Alaia is known in Dwarven as
Khundrazil and in ordinary human usage as the Khund or, more formally,
the Khundian Mountains. The Dwarven passes that matter so much to the
western trade system run through this barrier. Sinz does not merely
participate in the new western order; its northeastern salient helps
shape how western goods and political influence can approach the
mountains at all. That position gives the republic customs leverage,
transit leverage, military screening power, and the ability to complicate
Lienia’s access to the landward side of the pass system.

This geography matters at continental scale, not only regional scale.
Nereth’s desert interior makes long-distance movement depend heavily on
the habitable western fringe and near-coastal approaches. Sinz is
therefore best understood as a corridor republic rather than a maritime
one. Its one operational port, **Ildeanerale**, survives on a thin
southern coastline in Alaian country, but that position is vestigial
rather than foundational. The republic’s material base rests on tolls,
customs, warehousing, inland exchange, road services, and broad agrarian
depth in the grassland core. Maritime wealth is not a supplement to
these systems. It is largely absent.

The timing of this matters. Following the monarchy’s dissolution in 934
LC, Lienia seized most of the Sinzian ports on Oceanus Centralis. The
subsequent decades saw repeated Sinzian attempts to recover them, also
largely unsuccessful. Post-Veltric trade change only deepened the loss:
merchant traffic favored the more northern ports Lienia now controlled,
further reducing the value of what little southern coast Sinz retained.
The result is a republic whose remaining coast exists, but no longer
defines its power.

The capital, **Fisch**, lies in **Wutenken Province** on the republic’s
main west-east trunk-road system. Fisch is not a frontier-post capital
and not a maritime showpiece. Its placement makes Sinz feel like a
republic that rules a country rather than one that merely watches a
border. From Fisch the state can coordinate provinces, monitor major
roads, gather route intelligence, oversee customs interests, and mediate
between the western rivalry front, the broad interior, and the southern
and southeastern belts. The city is best imagined as magistratic,
archival, legal, and logistical in character rather than ceremonially
royal.

**Political Order**

Politically, Sinz is an oligarchic territorial republic. The public
language of the state is civic and practical rather than dynastic or
sacral, but that should not be mistaken for egalitarianism. Real power
rests with councils, magistrates, patrician houses, major commercial
interests, and provincial or territorial officials. The republic
possesses genuine coercive capacity, yet it governs unevenly and
pragmatically. Authority is strongest in the capital province, on major
roads, at revenue nodes, across the northeastern salient, and in
districts where border or customs pressure matters most. Away from such
places, local society retains more practical autonomy.

The republic’s founding also matters to how it governs. The Alaian south
was not simply carried into the new order by force or administrative
inertia. The monarchy’s failure to respect its Alaian population made
southern cooperation or acquiescence part of the price of the 934 LC
settlement. Yet the institutions that emerged from that settlement
remained decisively Sinzian-led and patrician in tone. The republic’s
civic narrative acknowledges broader participation in its founding more
readily than its institutional structure actually reflects it. The
Alaian grievance is therefore not a vague regional complaint but a
founding grievance: the south helped make the republic possible and was
then given a smaller place in it than the moment seemed to promise.

**Culture and Religion**

Culturally, Sinz is Sinzian-led but not culturally flat. Four internal
components matter most. The first is the Sinzian core — the governing
heart of the republic and the zone in which Sinzian language, civic
style, and Sinzism are most fully aligned. The second is the Alaian
southern and southeastern belt, the republic’s defining internal
tension and the location of its only surviving coast. The third is the
rustic Westric northeastern salient, overwhelmingly rural and lacking
the urban nodes that would make its separateness politically acute. The
fourth is a scattering of small Flandric pockets that add texture but
do not drive the republic’s political character.

The Alaian south deserves separate emphasis because it is where Sinz’s
composite nature becomes hardest to ignore. The interior is functionally
semi-autonomous, governed through negotiated arrangements with local
Alaian authority structures rather than through dense direct rule. The
republic’s administrative presence is concentrated on the coast and
above all around Ildeanerale. The port city itself is Alaian-majority at
street level but Sinzian-transplant and Cruzado dominant in much of its
elite life, making it a city with an edge rather than a city on the
brink: chronic unresolved tension held inside an uneasy working
agreement. Guilds and labor bodies are a persistent pressure on
republican administration there, and wealthy pure-blood Alaians remain a
real force in the upper tier alongside the mixed-heritage Cruzado
stratum.

The term **Cruzado** is an Alaian-origin social designation rather than a
legal or biological caste. It refers to mixed Sinzian-Alaian figures
who possess above-average wealth, influence, or entitlement inside the
republic’s institutional and economic life. Mixed ancestry alone is not
enough; social position is required. The designation does not propagate
simply through family lines, and Alaian attitudes toward Cruzados range
from pride to suspicion to strategic hope. Many common Alaians regard
them as a possible vehicle for greater Alaian influence in Fisch. Most
Sinzians, by contrast, continue to regard them simply as Alaian.

Religiously, Sinz is not unified by a single state faith. Sinzism is the
religion of the Sinzian cultural core only, not the republic as a whole
and not its official state religion across all populations. The Alaian
south contains Altavist, Brinese Order, and Alaian Forefathers
communities; the Westric and Flandric zones are Skrosenist. This does
not produce the sort of overt legitimacy fracture visible in eastern
Nereth, but it does make Sinz’s religious map a real part of its
internal structure rather than mere background color.

Within the Sinzian core, Sinzism is best understood as an evolved
people’s religion rather than a state cult. It predates the republic by
a substantial margin and grew gradually out of older Sinzian Druids folk
practice without founder, reformation, or single institutional break.
Its older land-and-cycle root is associated with **Alburg**, while
**Dachsbad** is the deity most associated with organized public Sinzism
— especially oath, law, exchange, and communal order — without that
implying a universally accepted supreme-god theology. Sinzism remains
place-centered and non-hierarchical. Religious authority is local,
customary, and reputational, exercised through learned custodians of
groves, springs, shrines, meeting places, civic sanctuaries, and other
sacred sites rather than through a centralized church. Rural communities
center the shared sacred year on sowing, harvest, winter reckoning, and
land-bound continuity; urban communities observe that same cycle but
give greater public prominence to oath renewal, contracts, market order,
and civic reconciliation. This gives Sinzism a persistent tension with
the republic’s oligarchic, commercially competitive civic culture even
where the two remain publicly intertwined.

**Social Tone**

The social tone of Sinz follows from the same structure. At the major
nodes the republic feels hard, practical, and watchful: magistrates,
record-keepers, customs officials, petitioners, merchants, officers,
escorts, and warehouse interests all belong naturally to public life.
Away from those nodes, much of Sinz is still experienced through local
society more than through constant state presence. Ordinary people know
the republic as roads, dues, market law, tax claims, and occasional
official scrutiny rather than as a uniformly felt daily administration.

That texture changes by region. The Sinzian core feels most explicitly
republican in the civic and administrative sense. The Alaian interior
experiences the state more intermittently and more through negotiated
arrangement than through daily direct rule. The coast around Ildeanerale
feels sharper and more politically charged than either, because there
ruler and ruled are in continuous contact. The Westric salient is more
rustic and administratively awkward than politically rebellious. Sinz is
therefore not modern in its reach. It is simply intelligent about where
power pays.

**Security and Foreign Posture**

Sinz’s security and diplomacy are shaped by the same habits. It prefers
pressure over pageantry: fortified nodes, road leverage, customs
presence, corridor watches, and the readiness to turn economic position
into political advantage. Its rivalry with Lienia is one of the
defining facts of western Nereth, but it is not a relationship of
constant open war. The present fifteen-year peace is real, yet it sits
on top of a deeper strategic humiliation. The Sinzian populations now
inside southern Lienia are not expatriate merchant communities or
colonial leftovers. They are populations the border moved over during
the post-934 rearrangements. Sinz’s grievance against Lienia is therefore
territorial, economic, and moral at once.

This is also why Fresen matters so much. The **Grand Duchy of Fresen** is
not simply another western coastal state. It is the dynastic remnant of
what Sinz ceased to be: a coastal polity with genuine Oceanus Centralis
access, pre-republican legitimacy, and a ruling house continuous with
the older western order. That makes Fresen structurally alien to Sinz’s
civic self-image even where historical kinship remains real. Fresen
holds what Sinz lost, and that fact almost certainly produces more
resentment on the Sinzian side than public rhetoric admits. Yet Fresen
is too small to defend itself independently and must navigate between
Sinz and Lienia, which keeps the relationship complicated rather than
openly hostile.

To the south, **the Fon** is less a military threat than a
background political concern. The Fon is Alaian in culture, Brinese in
religious majority, lightly settled, and politically confederated rather
than densely centralized. Its importance for Sinz lies in the fact that
it shares culture and religion with the Alaian south inside the
republic. The Fon therefore functions as a cultural reference point and
source of imagined solidarity for Sinz’s Alaian populations, especially
near the ecological transition on the coast. Fisch monitors that
dynamic, but the relationship is primarily commercial and political
rather than military.
**Chapter 71: The Grand Duchy of Fresen**

The Grand Duchy of Fresen is the old dynastic coast of western Nereth: a
small Sinzian-led littoral state whose significance lies less in great-
power scale than in legitimacy, continuity, and durable coastal
usefulness. It occupies the coast immediately west of Sinz and survives
not as a random minor duchy, but as the remaining personal demesne of
the ruling house of the old western kingdom that dissolved in 934 LC.
When the larger inland body of that monarchy broke with the crown and
reorganized as the Republic of Sinz, the dynasty retained only this
western coastal core.

That origin explains Fresen's peculiar dignity. It is smaller than the
major western powers and no longer defines the western order, yet it is
older in tone than the republic beside it. Its ruling house preserves a
continuity of blood, ceremony, and lawful propriety that Sinz deliberately abandoned. Fresen therefore feels like a proper grand duchy rather than a failed kingdom: compact, cautious, and still treated with more respect than its raw scale alone would predict.

**Geography**

Geographically, Fresen is a modest but articulated coast with several
usable inlets, small islands, and a compact interior capable of
supporting a real territorial state. Its capital, **Guhauben**, should
be understood less as a giant entrepot than as a ducal control point for
the coast and its short internal routes. The duchy's economy is
littoral and moderate: fisheries, coastal exchange, harbor service,
local shipment, and ordinary agrarian support rather than first-rank
customs dominance.

**Culture and Political Tone**

Culturally, Fresen is Sinzian-led, though its southern littoral shows a
meaningful Alaian imprint. That southern face should not be inflated
into a state-defining crisis, but it helps explain why Fresen feels like
a warm western coast with more than one historical layer. Politically
and socially, the duchy is best imagined as dynastic, local, and proper:
more personal and conservative than Sinz, less broad and crown-heavy
than Lienia, and chiefly concerned with preserving orderly autonomy
between stronger neighbors.

For that reason, the Grand Duchy of Fresen is most usefully understood
as the lawful coastal remnant of an older western order: a state whose
survival rests on inherited legitimacy, manageable scale, and the fact
that preserving it often proves less disruptive than absorbing it.

**Chapter 72: The Grand Duchy of Bramia**

The Grand Duchy of Bramia is an ancient Lienian coastal-transfer state
north of Lienia. It should not be understood as a Lienian offshoot or as
another dynastic remnant of the Sinz-Fresen settlement. Bramia possesses
a more independent history. Traditionally Lienian in political identity,
it long functioned as a crossroads between the southern Lienian maritime
world and the Flandric north, a role made possible by its unusually useful
combination of coast, inland waters, and short overland connections.

**Geography and Economy**

The river-mouth and associated lake-chain opening into Bramian waters are
the structural heart of the duchy's economy. Before Lienia matured into
the dominant western sea-gate monarchy, Bramia was one of the principal
transfer states through which goods, people, and maritime habits moved
between the Central Ocean, the Flandric-facing coasts, and the upper inland
routes leading toward Sinz's northeastern foreland. Lienia's rise
reduced Bramia's relative prominence, but it did not erase the duchy's
importance, because the Bramian water system remained a natural
transshipment interface.

This makes Bramia something more specific than a minor fishing coast and
less than a maritime hegemon. It is a transfer duchy. Its economy rests
first on moving cargo between ocean shipping and river-and-lake
transport, then on relay trade, warehousing, harbor service, and the
ordinary agrarian support of its interior. It therefore complements the
other major western powers without replacing them: Lienia remains the
broader deepwater sea-gate monarchy, while Sinz still conditions the
upper and final inland approach.

**Culture and Religion**

Culturally, Bramia is traditionally Lienian, but long contact with the
Flandric world has left a real mark on its ports, seafaring customs, and
coastal society. That influence should be imagined less as a present-day
demographic split than as an old maritime layering visible in mercantile
habits, ship culture, law, and harbor life. Religiously the duchy is
broadly and conventionally Skrosenist, which further helps explain its
comfort as a bridge between Lienian and northern coastal worlds.

**Foreign Posture**

Its capital, **Holterden**, is best understood as the ducal control point
of the hinge system rather than merely the duchy's largest port. Bramia's
foreign posture is therefore cautious, orderly, and self-respecting. It
survives not by dominating larger neighbors, but by remaining a useful,
legible, and well-governed intermediary whose geography still gives it a
real place in the western order.


**Chapter 73: Garka**

Garka is the smallest of the northern coastal polities of western Nereth:
a tiny Flandric shore-state whose internal order remains wholly Flandric and
tribal even though foreigners style it the “Duchy of Garka.” It endures
less because neighbors think it ought to exist than because it is too
small, cohesive, fortified, and low-yield to be worth the trouble of
extinguishing. To outsiders it may appear quaint, rustic, or backward;
to Garkans, such judgments are irrelevant. They know what Garka is.

That internal understanding matters. Garka does not think of itself as a
duchy in native constitutional terms. In native terms it is a communal
tribal-sacral polity of the **Four Winds**, culturally Flandric and
religiously Skrosenist, and unusually cohesive by the standards of
western maritime Nereth. Garkans place great importance on the fact that
no Flandric people have ever been permanently ruled by non-Flandric peoples,
and in Garka especially this is treated not merely as history but as
evidence of divine favor.

**Geography**

Geographically, Garka occupies a small rugged forested littoral pocket
north of Bramia, centered on a sheltered fortified harbor and a very
limited coastal-interior body. It is locally useful and reasonably
defensible, but neither rich enough nor central enough to tempt
sustained major-power annexation. Its capital, **Raudar**, is a
fortified port and the duchy’s governing and military heart. The coast
and harbor matter more than any deep interior, for Garka is a pocket
polity of the sea-edge rather than a broad territorial state.

**Political Order**

Most Garkan social and political life is communal, local, and tribally
ordered rather than centrally administered. Every Garkan belongs by
birth to one of the **Four Tribes**, known collectively as the **Four
Winds**. Tribal elders are elected within their own communities and,
outside the **Þing of the Four Winds**, rarely function as a single
governing body. The Four Winds exist chiefly for intertribal
coordination when common action is required: war and common defense, the
settlement of disputes between tribes, and the management of shared
obligations such as levies, tithes, or other contributions owed for
matters that concern Garka as a whole.

The ruler is the **Goðbróðir**, or, when a woman holds the office, the
**Goðsystir**. The office is held for life, never nominated, never
campaigned for, and treated as a sacred burden rather than a prize. When
the office falls vacant, a six-month interregnum follows. After that the
elders gather in the Þing of the Four Winds and each casts a single lot.
The lots are tallied, and the chosen elder is named to the office
whether willing or not, since the result is understood as divine
indication and must be obeyed.

The office is accountable, but only through an extreme process. Any
elder may challenge the reigning Goðbróðir or Goðsystir to single combat
only after securing majority support in the council of elders of each of
the four tribes. Falling even one vote short in any tribe disqualifies
the challenge. An elder who attempts such a challenge without the
required support is judged disloyal and is expected to die by ritual
suicide. The challenged ruler may name a champion to fight in their
stead. This carries no stigma for an aged or infirm officeholder, but is
deeply taboo for a young and vigorous one and would likely provoke
further challenges.

The spouse of a Goðbróðir or Goðsystir holds no second office and no
co-rule by right. The only formal privilege of the marriage is elevation
to elder-status within the spouse’s own birth tribe. This makes widows
and widowers of sacred rulers fully legitimate future candidates for the
office. Historically, most Goðsystirs had first been the wives of former
Goðbróðir.

**Economy**

Garka is not a major trade state. Its economy rests on fishing and
coastal labor, timber and forest products, small-harbor traffic, limited
local dues, and modest subsistence support from its rough littoral
hinterland. In this it differs sharply from Bramia’s transfer-duchy role
to the south. Garka’s importance is strategic and local rather than
systemic: a small hard coast, cohesive enough to govern itself and too
troublesome in relation to its value for outsiders to bother uprooting.


**Religion and Founding Association**

Garka is universally acknowledged across the Skrosenist world as the
legendary birthplace of the faith — the land where Skrosen walked, where
the first temples were raised, and where the founding communion with the
gods occurred. This association is not contested by any mainstream
Skrosenist community. Even Flandric neighbors who regard Garka as rustic
and backward acknowledge its founding significance, however grudgingly.

The precise founding geography within Garka has been lost through
centuries of oral transmission. The tradition names the region; it does
not name a site. Some Garkan clergy believe the founding occurred at or
near Raudar. Others suspect the true location lies elsewhere in their
territory, buried under forest growth or eroded coastline. Neither
position can be proven. Most pilgrims and visiting clergy who come to
Garka for study or formation naturally assume they are walking where
Skrosen walked. Garkans do not aggressively correct this assumption,
partly because they believe it likely, and partly because the
association serves them.

What is not in doubt is that the oldest active Skrosenist temples in the
world stand in and around Raudar. These temples are institutionally
alive — not merely historically significant — and Garkan senior clergy
carry prestige across the Skrosenist world disproportionate to their
state’s political significance. The Akrosite tradition in Likia
maintains training connections with these temples and regards Garkan
clergy as the most authoritative voices on traditional practice.

Garka’s pride in its founding association is grounded rather than
defensive. A minority of Garkan Akrosites and devout literalists
maintain legends that Skrosen traveled beyond Flandric lands to Lienia
or even across the sea — these are treated as zealotry by mainstream
Skrosenist communities including most Garkan clergy.

**Chapter 74: The Kingdom of Laups**

Laups is a broad Flandric upland kingdom whose strength lies less in
cities or maritime wealth than in holding together a large, rough
northern country through a few strong royal nodes, a cohesive
Skrosenist culture, and a monarchy built for sparse settlement and long
endurance. It is one of the Flandric highland kingdoms of western Nereth
and belongs firmly to the broader Flandric and Skrosenist world. Laups is
coastal, but it is not a coast-first maritime kingdom. Its identity
comes more from holding country than from funneling trade.

**Geography**

Geographically, Laups occupies a large northwestern shoulder of western
Nereth, with a long exposed coast, a rough upland and broken interior,
harsher northern country, and a somewhat more workable
southern-southeastern belt. It is better read as a broad upland kingdom
with forests, rougher central and northern belts, and more usable
approaches in the south than as a pure mountain-wall state. The west and
north give Laups its territorial weight; the south and southeast give it
its political nerve-center.

Though not the kingdom’s largest town, **Aldved** serves as Laups’s
royal hinge-capital. It sits where sea access, inland waters, and the
kingdom’s most important external routes converge, making it a
royal-defense and contact center rather than merely a commercial
capital. This placement helps explain Laups’s monarchy. The crown rules
not from the biggest market, but from the strongest strategic hinge.
**Hvenesbak**, by contrast, is the larger commercial and demographic
node, but does not displace Aldved’s royal primacy.

**Economy**

The kingdom’s economy rests on linked but modest systems—upland
production, forestry and woodland resources, limited coastal traffic,
southern hinge exchange, and the seasonal use of remote northern crown
holdings—rather than on any single dominant commercial center. Laups has
coastal access without maritime centrality. Its low urbanization and
small port count argue against reading it as a great sea-gate state.
Instead it should be imagined as broad, rough, and materially
distributed: a kingdom of woods, upland resources, scattered nodes, and
a few critical southern approaches.

**Foreign Relations**

Laups’s border with **Flaland** is generally peaceful and socially
porous. Frontier districts interact across it more readily than with
distant capitals, and the Laups–Flaland line forms part of a living
shared Flandric and Skrosenist border society rather than a hard
civilizational frontier. Minor grievances are sometimes discharged
through bounded ceremonial battles rather than open war: dangerous but
recognized contests that settle local honor and practical disputes
without escalating into major interstate conflict.

The kingdom’s northern reach appears in two detached crown holdings,
**New Laups Colony** and **New Bostingia Colony**. These are not failed
settler provinces or abandoned civil districts. They are seasonal
northern crown holdings, maintained for temporary maritime, extractive,
or other periodic use without permanent settlement or standing civilian
population. They show that Laups possesses a thin northern maritime
horizon, but not a heavy settler-colonial tradition.

**Culture and Religion**

Culturally and religiously, Laups appears highly coherent. It is Flandric
and Skrosenist throughout its known core. The kingdom’s tensions
therefore arise less from internal ethnocultural fracture than from
distance, roughness, sparse settlement, and the challenge of ruling a
large hard country through a few royal nodes. Socially it feels austere,
proud, local outside the main centers, and more customary than
bureaucratic. To neighbors, Laups is solid, somewhat hard, and more
respected for endurance than for brilliance: a northern weight rather
than a glamorous power.

Skrosen is understood to have traveled widely through Flandric lands
during his life, and Laups holds scattered ambient traditions — place
names, local legends, stone foundations of unclear origin — that some
associate loosely with an ancient Flandric high-king. No living
tradition connects these to Skrosen specifically, and the Laupan
relationship to the founding mythology is that of proud practitioners
of the faith rather than custodians of its founding geography.

**Part XIII: Southern Alaian Nereth**

**Chapter 75: The Brinese Order**

The Brinese Order is the dominant organized hearth religion of much of
the Alaian world. It emerged when older Alaian ancestor veneration
gradually fused with the broader polytheist religious pattern that
spread widely in the centuries after the Great Withdrawal. The result
was not the abandonment of the forefathers, but their incorporation into
a wider sacred order governed by the gods.

The Brinese Order is conservative, domestic, and lineage-conscious in
tone. Its strongest concerns are the proper tending of the dead, the
continuity of the household, the moral obligations between generations,
the right ordering of burial and remembrance, and the preservation of
communal custom. It is broad enough to shape villages, tribes, market
settlements, and prestige cities alike, and should not be mistaken for a
narrow clerical or courtly cult.

In Brinese teaching, the ancestors are real and worthy of honor, but
they are always subordinate to the gods. Each honored forebear is
understood as the client of a particular deity, usually the god whose
values, remembered virtues, or characteristic temperament best fit that
ancestor’s life. In this way the older Alaian ancestor tradition was
absorbed into a wider polytheist sacred hierarchy without losing its
household-centered core.

Brinese moral life is reinforced by the belief that the dead must be
rightly tended after death. People therefore fear not merely shame in
life, but diminished honor among the remembered dead. A badly lived life
may leave descendants reluctant to name a forebear proudly, uncertain in
their commendation, or unwilling to elevate that person among the
honored ancestors. The religion’s moral force is therefore
conservative, familial, and reputational rather than abstract.

The Brinese Order distinguishes between household ancestors and great
ancestors. Household ancestors are tended within the nuclear family and
immediate lineage and form the ordinary core of domestic religious life.
Great ancestors are older clan, tribal, regional, and route-community
forebears whose memory has expanded beyond one household into public
veneration. In some regions they approach demi-god-like status in
practice, though never in doctrine: they remain exalted dead beneath the
gods, not divine beings in their own right.

Great-ancestor shrines are expected to possess a relic or important
personal possession attributed to the ancestor, commonly a fingerbone,
tooth, or significant object from life. Such shrines are legitimate not
because some central body certifies them, but because the local
community recognizes both shrine and relic as worthy of public
reverence. No central authority systematically polices authenticity.
Some relics are doubtless covert imposters, but communal recognition
matters more than provable certainty.

For this reason the Brinese Order is both broad and loose. It offers a
shared religious framework across much of the Alaian world without
requiring hard central supervision. It can sustain household religion,
local shrine traditions, and major public ancestor cults at the same
time, making it especially well suited to culturally cohesive but
politically thin Alaian polities.

**Chapter 76: The Fon**

The Fon is a lightly settled Alaian confederated land-sphere whose
political unity is identified with the land itself rather than with
dense central administration. It should not be understood as an empire.
The Fonese treat the polity and the geographical space of Fon as
effectively the same thing: the confederation is the political
expression of that land, not an administrative layer imposed upon it
from above.

Its supreme traditional ruler is the Sotenar of the Fon. The Sotenar is
the leader of the tribe whose territory encompasses Sotenadabue, the
principal common city of the confederation. The office is attached to
that position by custom rather than election. It carries some religious
dignity, but its practical character is chiefly commercial and
managerial rather than strongly monarchic or sacral.

The Sotenar’s authority is thin. He does not rule the whole Fon as a
densely present crown. His common powers concern the matters that the
confederation actually shares: inter-tribal trade, tolls and levies, the
management of the main roads, and the limited common coordination
required when local disturbances threaten to spill across tribal
boundaries. Most daily governance remains in the hands of local rulers,
chiefs, kings, and other sub-polity authorities.

This structure fits the geography of the Fon. Tribal and sub-polity
territories are geographically rooted and do not shift without serious
friction. When boundaries do move, the result is usually not neat
administrative redistribution but limited engagements, reprisals, feuds,
and negotiated settlements. The Fon is therefore difficult to centralize
not because its people lack political structure, but because its
structure is territorial, customary, and locally entrenched.

Large-scale warfare is rare in the Fon. Local conflicts become serious
chiefly when they grow beyond the capacity of one district to contain
them or begin to bleed into adjacent tribes and regions. Neighboring
states generally avoid attempts to dominate the Fon because its dense
tropical geography and sparse population make conquest and occupation
impractical. The land is difficult, the returns are poor, and the local
political order is too rooted to be cheaply displaced.

Military obligation in the Fon is organized through tribal quota rather
than direct centralized levy. Every male is expected to give one year of
service at age sixteen, after which he remains liable for emergency
call-up until age forty. In times of crisis, the Sotenar sends heralds
to the constituent tribes and demands quotas of men according to the
needs of the situation. The tribes then determine internally how to
fulfill that obligation. Tradition recognizes a ceiling of seventy
percent of eligible men, though no call-up has ever approached that
level in practice.

Economically, the Fon is largely self-sufficient in essentials. Local
tribes grow and harvest most of their own food and produce most ordinary
goods within their own territories. The Sotenar’s income comes chiefly
from the limited common economy of the confederation: trade tolls and
levies, especially at the capital, and the charges associated with the
main road system that gives the office much of its practical importance.
Beyond that corridor, economic life is primarily local.

Religiously, the Fon belongs to the Alaian world and is majority Brinese
in public religious character. The Brinese Order provides a broad shared
framework of household continuity, ancestor reverence, burial order, and
local shrine life without requiring hard central supervision. This gives
the Fon a form of cultural and religious cohesion stronger than its
political centralization. The result is a polity that is loose in
administration, sticky in territorial identity, and stronger in custom
than in bureaucracy.

To neighboring powers, the Fon is less a military threat than a
difficult and persistent fact. Its chief importance to the Republic of
Sinz lies in culture and affinity: it stands as a southern Alaian
reference point for the Alaian populations of the republic’s own south,
especially where questions of identity, religion, and local autonomy
remain politically alive.
