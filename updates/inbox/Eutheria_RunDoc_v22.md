## Section I. World Orientation

The continental orientation used for current canon is as follows: Nereth occupies the northeast; Golsta occupies the southeast and is the smallest continent; Kasmora lies to the southwest and center-left; and Valthera lies to the northwest, above Kasmora. This orientation governs future geographic discussion unless superseded by a later canon ruling.

## Section II. Golsta

Golsta is canonically distinct from the other major continents. It is not treated as a conventional civilized landmass. According to current canon, Golsta possesses no significant humanoid population and should not be interpreted through settlement or state-generation artifacts where those artifacts conflict with encyclopedia authority.

Golsta is instead understood primarily as an inhospitable draconic wilderness. It functions in world structure less as a peer civilizational continent than as a frontier, ecological extreme, and non-humanoid geopolitical space.

## Section III. The Rawran Society

The Rawran Society is inseparably bound to the historical development of the Commonwealth of Sipharan. Current working canon holds that Sipharan and Rawranism evolved together, with the commonwealth’s recognizable civic identity crystallizing alongside the maturation of Rawran fellowship institutions.

In social function, Rawranism is best understood as a fellowship-centered religion of ordered community. Its strongest thematic affinities are moral cultivation, disciplined conduct, internal administration, civic trust, educational formation, and social harmony rather than conquest, missionary expansion, or heroic glory-seeking.

Rawranism should be read less as a universalizing conversion faith and more as a civilization-shaping tradition. A useful real-world analogy is Confucianism at the level of social function, though Rawranism remains more explicitly religious, initiatory, and fellowship-based in structure. It should be interpreted not as absolute pacifism, but as a tradition of ordered restraint that favors defense, mediation, and internal administration over expansion.

### Current interpretive principles

| Principle | Current Canon Reading |
| --- | --- |
| Core social ethic | Ordered fellowship, moral cultivation, restraint, and trustworthy administration. |
| Institutional tendency | Schools, archives, councils, adjudication, disciplined local communities, and initiatory belonging. |
| Use of force | Not absolute pacifism, but restrained force directed toward preservation of order rather than expansion. |
| Political adaptability | Compatible with both Sipharan republicanism and Haran monarchy because it prioritizes right order over a single mandatory constitutional form. |

The religion is naturally compatible with high literacy, record-keeping, legal scholarship, and the moral formation of civic and administrative elites.

Its low expansionism is understood as a product of doctrine and institutional culture rather than historical accident.

Rawranism emerged from earlier Mesopotamian animist traditions rather than appearing as an externally introduced faith.

## Section IV. Commonwealth of Sipharan

The Commonwealth of Sipharan is presently understood as a neutral, fellowship-shaped republic whose prestige derives primarily from culture, education, jurisprudence, and institutional trust rather than conquest or great-power commerce. Its civil identity cannot be separated from the Rawran Society, which acts as the moral and social framework of the commonwealth.

Sipharan is best interpreted as a cultural and educational hub within southern Kasmora. Its relevance on the wider stage lies in the excellence of its schools, archives, public disputation, legal scholarship, and religious-intellectual institutions. Its neutrality is therefore not merely a diplomatic convenience, but an outgrowth of Rawran civic-religious values expressed through the commonwealth’s political form.

By current working canon, Sipharan’s significance rests in normative authority: it teaches, records, arbitrates, and legitimizes. It is a place other states may rely on without wishing a rival to control it, and its role within Kasmora is best understood through neutrality, institutional trust, and cultural prestige rather than military leverage.

Sipharan’s neutrality is analogous to a trusted neutral commonwealth whose independence is useful to neighboring powers.

Its economy and prestige are tied heavily to education, arbitration, archives, scholarship, and other high-trust institutional services.

Sipharan and Rawranism should be treated as mutually formative rather than merely co-located.

## Section V. Kingdom of Har

Har is interpreted as a later adopter of Rawranism rather than its cradle. The kingdom represents a monarchical and feudal expression of the same broader religious civilization that in Sipharan takes republican form. It should not be treated as alien to Sipharan, but as a related state that embodies the same tradition under different constitutional assumptions.

Current working canon holds that Rawranism emerged from earlier Mesopotamian animist traditions and therefore felt culturally familiar to the population of Har. The faith’s appeal lay in its capacity to reinforce social cohesion, administrative reliability, ethical discipline, and political stability.

Har’s adoption of Rawranism is linked to royal patronage. A future king of Har was educated in Sipharan, encountered Rawran institutions there, and after ascending the throne declared Rawranism the official religion of the realm. The transition should be understood less as civilizational rupture than as the formalization, regularization, and eventual predominance of a faith already rooted in the same sacred world as older Haran animism.

In broad strokes, Har should be understood as a materially prosperous and conservative kingdom whose reputation rests less on conquest than on dependable craftsmanship, stable kingship, and the production of durable goods. If Sipharan represents the scholarly and civic prestige of Rawran civilization, Har represents its settled, monarchic, and artisanal expression.

In the southern Kasmoran sphere, Har functions as the monarchical counterpart to Sipharan: culturally related and Rawran, but constitutionally distinct.

Har’s inland wealth is tied to abundant natural resources, exported through its gulf ports and transformed into highly regarded finished goods.

It is widely known for exceptional manufactured goods, especially tools, metalwork, and fine ceramics.

Har is a prosperous, inward-looking Mesopotamian monarchy whose strength lies in stability, order, and material productivity.

## Section VI. Rawran-Haran Development

The following chronology reflects current working canon and remains provisional pending future encyclopedia revision:

Earlier Sipharan communities predate the rise of organized Rawranism.

Rawran reform currents emerge later from local Mesopotamian animism within the Sipharan sphere.

The recognizable Commonwealth of Sipharan crystallizes as Rawran fellowship institutions mature and become socially authoritative.

Sipharan rises into prominence as a regional center of education, culture, arbitration, and learned administration only after this institutional consolidation.

A future king of Har is educated in Sipharan, becomes conversant in Rawran belief and practice, and later declares Rawranism the official religion of Har.

Har’s population adopts Rawranism rapidly because it grows from the same Mesopotamian animist roots already familiar to the realm, though older practices are gradually absorbed and reinterpreted rather than instantly erased.

## Section VII. Haria

Haria is currently understood as the principal Berber kingdom of Kasmora and the main political center of Berber civilization in the inhabited world. It is a large, culturally cohesive state whose identity should be grounded first in Berber civilizational centrality rather than reduced merely to trade or maritime advantage.

Its dominant religion is Delistanism, but in Harian society that faith is expressed in a non-theocratic form. Haria therefore stands in useful contrast to Dukan: both are Delistanist, yet Harian religious life is understood as socially rooted and community-led rather than fused to coercive state rule.

Geographically and politically, Haria occupies a long and strategically significant southeastern coastline in Kasmora. Its position gives it leverage over important southern sea routes, including the waters through which neighboring Hawa must pass to reach Likia. Haria should therefore be treated as a maritime actor with real regional influence, but one whose maritime importance grows out of its status as a major Berber kingdom rather than replacing it.

In broad strokes, Haria should be understood as a culturally central, Delistanist, outward-facing coastal kingdom: cohesive, self-confident, and more strategically assertive than Har, while remaining distinct from theocratic Dukan.

## Section VIII. Kingdom of Hawa

Hawa is currently understood as Kasmora’s premier Arabic maritime trading kingdom: a cohesive, outward-facing southern state whose strength lies in ports, shipping, and commercial organization rather than territorial conquest.

Culturally and religiously, Hawa belongs firmly to the Arabic Ayedist sphere and stands in especially close civilizational continuity with Sala. Its internal profile appears relatively cohesive, with little evidence of deep cultural fragmentation when compared with more mixed frontier states.

Geographically, Hawa occupies a long southern coastline and is naturally oriented toward maritime exchange. Its capital, Ashaybar, is a major fortified port and the largest capital in Kasmora. The wider kingdom remains mostly rural, but a disproportionate share of its political and economic significance is concentrated in coastal infrastructure, harbor cities, and maritime administration.

Hawa’s expansionist posture should be understood primarily as commercial and seaborne rather than territorial. It is ambitious in trade and outward in orientation, but not organized as a heavy land-conquest state.

At the same time, Hawa is not strategically unconstrained. Its southern access to Likia depends on waters influenced or controlled by Haria, making maritime negotiation an enduring feature of its foreign relations. Hawa should therefore be understood as prosperous, seafaring, and commercially capable, but also structurally dependent on secure regional sea access beyond its own coast.

## Section IX. Dukanese Theocracy

The Dukanese Theocracy is currently understood as a large Iranian-analog theocratic state in eastern Kasmora, ruled by the Hierophant, whose office fused kingship and clerical authority after the crown’s conversion to Delistanism. Dukan presents itself as the true paragon of Delistanist faith, and its religious institutions serve as organs of state authority rather than merely as community leadership.

In broad strokes, Dukan should be treated as the clearest malign power among the major Kasmoran states: rigid, oppressive, and convinced of its own spiritual supremacy. It is one of the continent’s largest powers on paper, yet its authority is burdened by internal resistance, difficult borderlands, and the strategic consequences of ideological overreach.

The Likian-Dukan war should currently be understood as having lasted from 874 to 876 LC, during the same broader era of disruption that followed the collapse of the Veltric Empire in eastern Nereth. The Theocracy justified the war as an effort to liberate an Iranian population then living within Likian borders, but this rationale quickly lost credibility when Dukanese forces continued into clearly Likian-majority territory. The more important motive was economic and strategic: Dukan sought relief from Likian taxation on goods passing through Likian territory into Dukan. The Dukanese victory brought territorial gains, but at catastrophic cost.

In the aftermath of the war, Dukan emerged territorially enlarged but strategically weakened. Large portions of its remaining military strength were consumed by occupation duty in the conquered lands, where Likian resistance has continued to grow. Likia has since maintained a naval blockade on Dukanese ports, while Haria restricts trade into Dukan largely to foodstuffs and polices entry points carefully, in part because Harian Iranians fear the influence of the Theocracy more than any other neighboring population.

Dukan and Haria should be understood as uneasy religious siblings. They share Delistanism but practice it in radically different ways: Haria as a socially embedded faith without clerical rule, Dukan as a coercive theocratic state. Dukan quietly assumes that Haria ought eventually to be absorbed into the Theocracy, while Haria has no interest in such submission. The significant Iranian minority within Haria provides Dukan with its most plausible future pretext for intervention under the banner of liberation.

Dukan’s defining contradiction is religious and political. Delistanism descends from the older Iranian Deities tradition, but its organized form developed in the Berber south before returning north. The Dukanese Theocracy claims to embody the faith in its highest and purest form, yet that claim is contested both by Iranian revivalists within Dukan and by the mere existence of non-theocratic Delistanist Haria.

## Section X. Kordemeli

In broad strokes, Kordemeli should be understood as a settled northern khaganate: large, durable, strategically important, and governed through pragmatic imperial hierarchy rather than cultural unity. It is one of Kasmora’s major land powers and is widely respected across the continent, even by states that do not especially like it.

Its relationship with Sala is warm and reciprocal. Kordemeli relies heavily on Salan grain to help feed its large population and on Salan roads to facilitate Ayedist pilgrimage, which, though not mandatory, is highly encouraged. The two states are therefore bound not merely by tolerance, but by active mutual benefit in food supply, movement, and sacred geography.

Kordemeli’s relationship with Likia should be understood as one of long-term strategic tension without open war. The Likian subject populations within the Khaganate remain a grievance in Likian politics, and only the Democratic faction openly calls for their reintegration. Other Likian factions do not openly espouse reconquest, recognizing both the economic distraction and the practical impossibility of waging an offensive war against one of Kasmora’s largest land powers. Likia can defend its existing territory, but not readily launch a war of recovery.

Current working canon holds that Kordemeli acquired much of its western Likian territory during the Likian–Xin Guo war, when Likia’s eastern military commitments left its western provinces politically neglected and increasingly unstable. Local militias offered only token resistance, recognizing that no serious reinforcement would come. Rather than continue a futile struggle, the western Likian cities made terms with Kordemeli and preserved their civic life under imperial supremacy.

Kordemeli governs subject populations pragmatically rather than uniformly. Communities that submit and maintain order are often left their laws, customs, religion, and local institutions. In the Likian territories, certain port cities retain full municipal autonomy, while the ports themselves remain under direct Kordemelian control through garrisons, customs authorities, and naval administration. This arrangement preserves local urban identity while guaranteeing uninterrupted Kordemelian access to the harbors and straits.

Power in Kordemeli flows through personal loyalty to the Khagan. Clans are the principal social and political building blocks of the ruling elite, but office, command, and regional authority depend on imperial favor rather than independent hereditary right. Kordemeli should therefore be interpreted as a settled imperial state whose hierarchy is personal, martial, and administrative at once.

Kordemeli is politically Turkish in character, but culturally and religiously composite. It rules over a large Likian subject population and incorporates Ayedist and Skrosenist communities within the same imperial framework. Its strategic importance lies not simply in size, but in its control of the northern Kasmoran mainland approaches and its position near the strait zone between Kasmora and Valthera. Its role in regional politics is therefore that of a respected and powerful northern land state whose weight no neighbor casually challenges.

## Section XI. Sala

In broad strokes, Sala should be understood as one of Kasmora’s most influential soft-power states: an Arabic Ayedist kingdom whose authority rests on sacred geography, pilgrimage infrastructure, agricultural abundance, and commercial ambition. It governs access to Tuwaid through the Jora Territory and converts that religious centrality into durable diplomatic and economic leverage.

Sala’s claim to be Kasmora’s breadbasket is grounded in fact. The state data show one of the continent’s largest overwhelmingly rural populations, and the biome map supports the existence of broad productive lands capable of sustaining major staple agriculture at scale. Sala should therefore be understood not merely as a pilgrimage kingdom, but as one of Kasmora’s great food-producing states.

Its primary export is grain. Secondary exports likely include other staple crops, livestock products, fodder, preserved foods, and other provisions tied both to long-distance trade and to the movement of pilgrims through the sacred heartland. Sala’s agricultural surplus also supports substantial northward trade into Valthera and helps sustain Kordemeli, whose warm relations with Sala are rooted in practical dependence as much as religious fellowship.

Politically, Sala appears to transform sacred legitimacy into influence without becoming a coercive theocracy. Its expansionism is best understood as commercial and maritime rather than straightforwardly military, and its strength lies in access, provisioning, roads, markets, and the management of a sacred landscape upon which foreigners also depend.

Its relationship with Hawa remains one of close civilizational parallel: both are Arabic and Ayedist kingdoms with little structural friction, though Hawa faces outward toward southern sea trade while Sala faces northward and inward toward pilgrimage, sacred geography, and northern maritime exchange.

Correction note for future encyclopedia revision: references identifying Jalim and Salahrah as following a non-Ayedist religion are outdated and should be revised to reflect current canon. Both are Ayedist, which better accords with Sala’s role as the kingdom controlling access to Tuwaid and the core sacred landscape of Ayedism.

## Section XII. Kasmora

Kasmora should be understood as the world’s most agriculturally productive continent and one of its most quietly consequential. Vast interior plains, reliable river systems, and deep alluvial soils give it a demographic and economic weight out of proportion to the attention it receives from outside powers. The continent’s geography, however, resists empire: a northern mountain range separates the coast from the interior, rivers drain outward toward multiple littoral zones, and no single state has ever been able to impose durable rule over the whole landmass. As a result, Kasmora’s history is one of persistent fragmentation among kingdoms, khaganates, commonwealths, theocracies, and maritime states rather than continental unification.

Settlement across Kasmora is overwhelmingly rural, with large populations anchored in agricultural belts, river valleys, and productive coastal regions. Even its major states remain far more agrarian than urban. This gives the continent unusual resilience. Kasmora’s strength lies less in singular giant cities than in the cumulative weight of millions of cultivators, caravan routes, pilgrimage roads, and port towns feeding one another through a dense web of regional exchange.

The continent’s internal economy rests on interdependence. Sala stands as the breadbasket of Kasmora, exporting grain and other staple goods at scale. Hawa is the clearest maritime trading kingdom of the south. Har contributes exceptional manufactured goods, especially tools, metalwork, and fine ceramics. Sipharan’s influence is institutional rather than material, rooted in learning, cultural prestige, and the Rawran Society. Haria combines Berber civilizational centrality with maritime leverage over southern coastal routes. Kordemeli dominates the northern mainland approach zones and commands enormous territorial weight. Dukan remains powerful but strained, its theocratic ambitions burdened by internal resistance and costly overreach. Taken together, these states form a continental system in which no one power rules all, but each depends in some way on the others.

Religion in Kasmora is politically inseparable from geography. Ayedism gives Sala soft power through control of Tuwaid and the pilgrimage routes that lead to it. Delistanism exists in two distinct civilizational forms: organic and socially embedded in Haria, coercive and statist in Dukan. Rawranism shapes the political cultures of Har and Sipharan, binding one to stable monarchy and the other to learned republican fellowship. Skrosenism enters Kasmora principally through Likian populations and through the long shadow of Likia’s maritime and cultural influence on the northern coast. These faiths do not map neatly onto states or peoples; instead, they create many of the continent’s most important tensions, dependencies, and claims to legitimacy.

Politically, Kasmora is defined by coexistence without unity. Its states are often deeply different in culture, religion, and constitutional form, yet most are durable because each occupies a recognizable niche. Sipharan is neutral and institutional. Har is stable and inward-looking. Haria is culturally central and regionally assertive. Hawa is maritime and commercially ambitious. Sala turns sacred geography into leverage. Kordemeli is a respected northern land power. Dukan is the most oppressive state on the continent and the clearest source of ideological menace. Likia, though not one of the seven major mainland states, remains indispensable to the northern maritime straits and therefore to continental trade.

For outsiders, Kasmora is easy to underestimate because it lacks a single hegemon and often appears politically fragmented. That is misleading. Its fragmentation is not weakness so much as equilibrium. Kasmora feeds surrounding regions, anchors major religious traditions, and contains several of the largest and most consequential states in the world. While Nereth remains preoccupied with the aftermath of the Veltric collapse and Valthera remains divided, Kasmora’s demographic and economic weight continues to grow. In this sense, it is the world’s quietly emerging center of gravity.

## Section XIV. Valthera

Valthera is the northwestern continent of Eutheria, a land defined by cold northern extremes, lake-rich interiors, rugged uplands, and politically divided lowland cores. It is one of the world’s great civilizational regions, home to the continent’s Chinese-analog and Korean-analog peoples, as well as a major Dwarven presence in the interior mountain systems. Though wealthy in places and highly developed by preindustrial standards, Valthera has never been united under a lasting continental empire. Its geography favors regional powers, and its political history has produced a durable pattern of competing states rather than hegemony.

### Physical Character

Valthera is a continent of environmental contrast. Its far north is dominated by glacial and subarctic landscapes, while the south and southeast give way to more temperate forests, plains, wetlands, and agricultural basins. More than any other continent, Valthera is shaped by inland water. Vast lakes, branching river systems, and broken coastlines form the structural backbone of trade, settlement, and state formation.

The central and interior regions are marked by highlands and mountain systems that divide the continent into distinct subregions. These uplands do not fully isolate the major settled zones, but they do hinder overland consolidation and create natural corridors, choke points, and defensible enclaves. As a result, Valthera is best understood not as a single continuous political space, but as a chain of connected regional worlds linked by water and interrupted by stone.

### Settlement and Economy

Settlement in Valthera is concentrated in the more productive southern lowlands, lake margins, river valleys, and coastal belts. The harsh northern zones support far less dense habitation, while the central uplands appear to favor fortified settlements, mining communities, strongholds, and pass-oriented centers rather than broad agrarian states.

Because of this geography, Valtheran commerce is unusually dependent on waterways. Lakes and rivers likely serve not merely as supplements to overland trade, but as the primary arteries of exchange across much of the continent. This helps explain why many of the major Chinese-analog states of Valthera are maritime or naval in character. Political influence in Valthera is not measured only in land held, but in harbors controlled, river mouths taxed, lake routes secured, and corridors denied to rivals.

### Peoples and Civilizational Structure

Valthera is defined by three major cultural blocs.

The first and most extensive is the Chinese-analog sphere, which dominates much of the south, east, and northeast. This bloc appears to produce many of the continent’s strongest naval and commercial states and seems to be the principal driver of Valthera’s maritime and lacustrine political culture.

The second is the Korean-analog sphere, concentrated primarily in the west. These states form a distinct regional system of their own and appear to be somewhat less maritime in orientation than their eastern counterparts, though still fully integrated into the broader political life of the continent.

The third is the Dwarven sphere of the interior mountains. Valtheran Dwarves occupy the highland systems between the major human blocs and are likely of outsized importance relative to their territorial footprint. They are not merely a peripheral people. Their location places them along the natural barriers and transit routes that separate west from east and highland from lowland. This makes them strategically, economically, and religiously significant.

Taken together, these three blocs give Valthera the character of a contact continent. Its defining pattern is not isolation between peoples, but structured interaction across boundaries shaped by terrain.

### Religion and Cultural Contact

One of the most important implications of Valthera’s cultural geography is the intensity of contact between Korean-analog and Dwarven populations in the western and central uplands. This contact has already had major religious consequences, most notably in the emergence of the Tongj Apostates tradition associated with Hayanguk.

This suggests that Valthera is not a continent of neatly bounded faith zones. Instead, it likely contains several regions where religion, culture, and political identity have developed through prolonged frontier interaction. Mountain borders in Valthera are probably not dead edges. They are zones of exchange, reinterpretation, tension, and selective synthesis.

### Political Pattern

Valthera is politically fragmented, but not politically weak. It contains numerous substantial states, several of them powerful by continental standards, yet no single hegemon has succeeded in uniting the whole continent. This does not appear to be the result of stagnation or lack of ambition. Rather, it reflects the structure of the continent itself.

Valthera’s terrain encourages the rise of regional powers anchored in different environmental systems: western basins, southern coasts, southeastern plains, lake networks, mountain corridors, and island-linked maritime zones. These states can become strong within their own spheres, but the continent as a whole resists consolidation. Mountains complicate inland campaigns, waterways create alternative power centers, and the distribution of peoples prevents easy civilizational absorption.

The result is a durable balance-of-power environment. Valthera is a continent of rival states, not failed unification.

### The Likian Constraint

Valthera’s internal politics cannot be understood entirely on their own terms. The southern maritime order imposed by Likia places a real ceiling on Valtheran naval expansion beyond the continent. The defeat of Xin Guo and the establishment of the long cold peace reshaped the strategic horizon of Valtheran states. Since then, the major powers of the continent appear to have accepted trade within a Likian-dominated maritime framework rather than openly contesting southern sea control.

This does not mean Likia rules Valthera. It does mean that Valthera’s most outward-facing powers operate under a structural external limit. A Valtheran state may dominate a coast, a sea lane, a lake system, or a colonial frontier, but true southern maritime supremacy is constrained by the Likian order.

This likely pushes Valtheran competition inward and sideways rather than outward. Rivalry therefore expresses itself through trade dominance, border competition, proxy influence, colonial ventures, and control of internal water systems rather than through open bids for oceanic mastery.

### Historical Character

Valthera possesses a deep and consequential past, including major events of the ancient world, but its present political condition should not be reduced to ancient history alone. The continent’s current state is better explained by enduring structural conditions than by any single old catastrophe. Geography, ethnocultural distribution, internal competition, and the external maritime balance with Likia are more immediately useful explanations for the Valthera of 1026 LC than distant events, however famous they remain in memory.

Its history matters, but its structure matters more.

### Overall Character

Valthera is a continent of cold horizons, interior waters, divided highlands, ambitious states, and layered civilizational contact. It is wealthier and more organized than a simple frontier, yet too geographically broken and politically plural to collapse into empire. Its peoples are powerful enough to matter on the world stage, but constrained enough to remain in competition with one another. It is a continent where trade, terrain, and regional identity matter more than abstract territorial scale.

In practical terms, Valthera is best understood as a politically fragmented but highly developed continental system: maritime in the east and south, culturally layered in the west, strategically knotted in the center, and never fully subject to a single will.

## Section XV. Han Guo

Han Guo should be understood as a Chinese-classed republic that is, in practice, a deeply integrated Chinese–Korean union. Its official forms, court language, institutional culture, and civil tradition are Chinese, which is why the state is classed as Chinese in the aggregate data. On the ground, however, the republic is more cohesive than the culture map alone first suggests. The Chinese and Korean populations of the Han Guo region are best understood as overlapping language-family communities in a mixed frontier society where substantial bilingualism is likely, especially in urban and governing life.

The capital, Insan, is Korean. This should not be treated as an anomaly. The best explanation is that Insan became the capital through a founding political compromise: Han Guo governs through a Chinese institutional order from a Korean city. This implies that the Korean side of the union was foundational rather than marginal.

Han Guo likely took coherent republican form in the post–Xin Guo-war order. In the aftermath of the Likian victory and the reorganization of the Strait region, Chinese and Korean communities in this part of Valthera had strong incentives to combine their resources. Economic pressure, competition with larger neighboring powers, and shared Taizhouist religious life all encouraged durable political union. Han Guo is therefore best understood not as a conquest state, but as a negotiated republican compact.

Han Guo's northwestern holdings are not a second heartland. The biome and political maps show that they sit on the far western edge of the habitable fringe beneath the glacier. They should be understood as remote extraction outposts—small, sea-linked, and economically useful—rather than as part of a broad northern territorial corridor.

Han Guo's legitimacy is reinforced by its relationship to Quz Guo. Its Chinese character is expressed not through a Chinese capital, but through its institutions, its religious life, and its duty to protect the sacred Taizhouist center at Shang.

## Section XVI. Quz Guo

Quz Guo is a small sovereign Chinese Taizhouist state centered on Shang, the birthplace of Taizhouist philosophy and religion. It predates Han Guo and should be understood as one of the older city-states of the region rather than as a later creation of the republic.

Its continued separate existence is explained by sacred status rather than by raw power. Quz Guo is formally sovereign and governs itself internally, but its security and broader geopolitical shelter depend on Han Guo. The right comparison is not a normal minor coastal duchy, but a sacred polity whose independence is preserved because absorption into ordinary secular administration would diminish its religious standing.

Han Guo's protection of Quz Guo is not merely a matter of convenience or treaty mechanics. It is a religious duty built into Han Guo's self-understanding. Han Guo safeguards Shang and preserves Quz Guo's autonomy in much the same way that a larger religious civilization protects its holiest center without reducing it to an ordinary province.

The resulting structure is dual but coherent: Insan is the political capital of the republic, while Shang is the sacred center of the wider Taizhouist world.

## Section XVII. Clarification on the Likian–Xin Guo Conflict

The Likian–Xin Guo conflict should not be understood as a single uninterrupted 450-year war. The cleaner and more credible reading is a centuries-long maritime rivalry.

Valtheran expansion into Oceanus Centralis began around 200–300 LC. From roughly 300 LC to 700/750 LC, Likia and Xin Guo were engaged in a prolonged strategic contest marked by intermittent naval wars, embargoes, proxy struggles, piracy and privateering, and competition over islands and trade routes. This rivalry culminated in a final major war in the eighth century LC, ending with Likian victory and the reconstitution of Xin Guo as the See of Xin Guo around 750–800 LC.

Earlier RunDoc references to the "Likian–Xin Guo war" should be read in this clarified sense unless otherwise specified.
