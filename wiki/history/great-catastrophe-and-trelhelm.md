# Great Catastrophe and the Battle of Trelhelm

The Great Catastrophe (**9034 BLC**) marks a civilizational turning point in Eutherian history. It combines two simultaneous processes: the decisive end of the [Thousand-Year War](thousand-year-war.md) and the beginning of the long glacial retreat known as the Great Withdrawal.

## The Thousand-Year War and the Deep Folk crisis

Late ice-age pressures in deep subterranean regions destabilized Drow and Hryngar systems and contributed to mass military confrontation with surface civilizations. In alliance with major orc war bands, Deep Folk coalitions launched sustained campaigns against surface polities.

The conflict lasted roughly a millennium, devastated large regions, and repeatedly pushed surface civilizations toward collapse. Public historiography agrees on the reality of the deep crisis but not on its ultimate cause.

## General TarKath and Trelhelm (9034 BLC)

In the war's late stage, the orc warlord TarKath unified major Dark Folk forces. The decisive engagement occurred in the southern mountains of [Valthera](../geography/valthera.md): the Battle of Trelhelm.

TarKath's defeat broke coalition momentum. Drow and Hryngar forces withdrew into deeper regions, while surviving orc groups fragmented and repositioned to frontier zones.

## The Great Withdrawal

Around the same historical moment, major northern glacial systems began retreating poleward. Over following centuries:

- sea levels rose
- river systems reconfigured
- forest belts expanded northward
- newly habitable territories opened

Postwar societies therefore rebuilt while planetary geography itself was actively changing.

## Dispersal and recovery patterns

Post-Trelhelm reconstruction differed by people and region:

- Elven polities concentrated in long-duration forest refugia
- Dwarven groups resecured mountain complexes
- Human populations expanded rapidly across newly productive landscapes
- Drow and Hryngar withdrew below, while orc populations remained partly near-surface

These divergent recovery trajectories still shape continental settlement and strategic geography.
