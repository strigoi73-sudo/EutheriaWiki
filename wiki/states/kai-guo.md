# The Marches of Kai Guo

The Marches of Kai Guo are a small treaty-bound littoral march on eastern Valthera's southeastern seaboard. Their importance comes less from sheer size than from control of a southern sea outlet that matters to both [Kaihui](kaihui.md) and [Kan Guo](kan-guo.md).

![Political map crop of the Marches of Kai Guo](../assets/maps/kai-guo-map.png)

## Strategic role

Kai Guo is not best understood as an island state or as a major independent sea power. It controls the outlet of a southern watershed distinct from Kaihui's larger northern river system, which gives it leverage over one important corridor without making it master of all regional inland movement.

That narrow but real importance explains why stronger neighbors tolerate and preserve it. The Marches matter because they keep a strategically sensitive outlet from falling wholly under either Kaihui or Kan Guo.

## Political order

Kai Guo is governed by the **Council of the Marches**, a thirteen-member body that oversees ordinary internal affairs, customs administration, harbor management, policing, maintenance, and treaty obligations.

Its **Speaker** serves as a presiding and ceremonial officer rather than an independent executive. The office is chosen for a twelve-month session by lot from among eligible councilors and then confirmed by majority vote of the council.

## Reserved Matters and neutrality

Kaihui and Kan Guo each maintain a treaty-recognized representative on the council with veto power over **Reserved Matters**. These do not cover normal local legislation, but they do apply to fortifications, foreign agreements, strategic land transfers, military ceilings, major toll changes, and other decisions that could upset the regional balance.

If both representatives veto such a measure, it fails outright. If only one does, the matter returns to review and can pass only by unanimous council approval. In practice, this makes Kai Guo a carefully managed neutral outlet rather than an ordinary sovereign march.

## Economy and civic culture

Kai Guo lives by fish and passage. Fishing, fish processing, ropework, netmaking, pilotage, warehousing, customs, and transit fees define the state's economic life more than agriculture does.

Its civic culture values procedure, exact records, calm administration, and non-provocation. The Marches present themselves as proof that rival states can solve a strategic problem through law and treaty instead of conquest.

## Security

The Marches maintain patrol and internal-security forces sufficient to preserve neutrality and keep the outlet operational. Their posture is closer to that of a treaty coast-guard state than to that of a field-army kingdom or a blue-water naval power.
