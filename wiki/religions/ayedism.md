# Ayedism

Ayedism is an organized polytheist tradition centered in Kasmora, with approximately 11.95 million believers and strong association to Arabic-analog religious culture. It is structured around sacred geography rather than temple-centered monumental institutions.

## Religious structure

Ayedist practice is strongly place-oriented. Pilgrimage routes, sacred landscapes, and ritual travel are as important as fixed clerical institutions.

Canon sources describe no major Ayedist temple network; the faith's physical religious life is concentrated in routes, waystations, and natural sacred sites.

Its holiest known site is [Tuwaid](../places/tuwaid.md) in [Jora Territory](../places/jora-territory.md), under the sovereignty of [Sala](../states/sala.md).

Tuwaid is a small pilgrimage burg (about 3,214 inhabitants), while Jora Territory is geographically vast and sparsely settled (about 749,000 square miles and roughly 922,000 inhabitants), making overland travel itself part of Ayedist religious experience.

## Geographic distribution

Ayedism is dominant or highly significant in:

- [Sala](../states/sala.md)
- [Hawa](../states/hawa.md)
- parts of [Kordemeli](../states/kordemeli.md)

Kordemelian adoption is historically notable because it makes a major non-Arabic imperial polity dependent on sacred sites located in another kingdom.

## Political implications

Because Tuwaid lies within Sala, Sala controls core pilgrimage access logistics for external Ayedist populations. This grants Sala recurring soft-power leverage in regional diplomacy, especially in relations with Kordemeli.

This dynamic is most visible in north-south movement between [Kordemeli](../states/kordemeli.md) and Sala: Kordemelian Ayedists depend on access through a foreign kingdom to reach their holiest site.

Ayedism therefore operates simultaneously as:

- a religious tradition,
- a trans-state mobility system,
- and a strategic factor in interstate negotiation.
