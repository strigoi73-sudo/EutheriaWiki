# Souls and the Cycle of Existence

Every mortal creature possesses a soul originating in the Pleroma. Mortal life in the Material Plane is the arena in which that soul develops alignment, memory, character, and a more definite form of selfhood.

This means mortal existence is not an incidental episode between greater states. It is the decisive zone of formation within the cosmic order.

## Death and transition

After death, souls pass into the Astral Plane. From there, their future is determined not by arbitrary sorting but by the condition they have acquired through life.

Three primary outcomes are described.

## Reincarnation

Most souls eventually return to mortal life. Their alignment remains mixed, unstable, or incomplete, and so they continue through the cycle of mortal becoming.

Reincarnation is therefore the ordinary pattern, not a rare exception. The Material Plane is the place where unfinished souls continue their formation.

## Transfiguration

Some souls become so perfectly aligned with an Outer Plane that they are permanently drawn into it, becoming native outsiders.

This is not simply reward or punishment in a moralistic sense. It is a metaphysical consummation: the soul has become so fully identified with a non-neutral alignment force that it can no longer remain a mixed mortal being.

## Return to the Pleroma

A very small number of souls achieve perfect harmony and return to the Pleroma, leaving the cycle entirely.

This is the rarest destiny because it implies not merely strong alignment, but a form of completion compatible with primordial unity rather than with the differentiated moral absolutism of the Outer Planes.

## Religious and existential significance

The cycle of souls explains why mortal life matters, why alignment has consequence beyond conduct, and why death in Eutheria is neither annihilation nor a simple one-time judgment.

It also helps explain the importance of ethics, ritual, memory, and metaphysical discipline in many traditions. What a person becomes in life is not shed at death. It determines the shape of what follows.
