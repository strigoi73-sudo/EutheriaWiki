# Han Guo

Han Guo is a Chinese-classed republic in Valthera that is, in practice, a deeply integrated Chinese-Korean union. Its official legal culture, court language, and state institutions are predominantly Chinese, but its social fabric is mixed and cohesive, with substantial Chinese-Korean overlap and likely widespread bilingualism in urban and administrative life.

## Historical development

Current canon places Han Guo's coherent republican consolidation in the post-Xin Guo order. After the final phase of the Likian-Xin Guo conflict and the reorganization of the Strait region, Chinese and Korean communities in this part of Valthera had strong incentives to combine political resources under a shared constitutional framework.

Han Guo is therefore best interpreted as a negotiated civic compact rather than a conquest state. Its durability appears to come from institutional integration across communities that might otherwise have remained separate regional polities.

## Political character

Han Guo's public identity is Chinese in aggregate classification, but this should be read as an institutional profile rather than an ethnically exclusive one. The republic's governing forms are Chinese-forward; its everyday social composition is mixed.

That distinction helps explain why Han Guo can appear culturally complex without being politically incoherent. It is a union state whose legitimacy depends on preserving shared republican process across multiple language-family communities.

## Insan and founding compromise

The capital, **Insan**, is Korean. In canon interpretation, this is not an anomaly but a foundational compromise: a Chinese-ordered republic governing from a Korean city. The location signals that the Korean component of Han Guo was constitutive, not peripheral.

This duality is central to Han Guo's identity. Institutional form and civic geography do not fully overlap, and the state treats that mismatch as a source of legitimacy rather than as a defect.

## Territory and economy

Han Guo's northwestern holdings are not treated as a second demographic core. They are better understood as remote, sea-linked extraction outposts at the habitable fringe beneath glacial margins.

This makes Han Guo a layered territorial system: a dense political core tied to mixed frontier society, plus peripheral resource zones integrated through maritime and administrative links.

## Religion and legitimacy

Han Guo's legitimacy is reinforced by religious duty. It protects [Quz Guo](quz-guo.md) and safeguards Shang, a sacred Taizhouist center. This role helps anchor Han Guo's Chinese civil identity without requiring a Chinese capital city.

For broader religious context, see [Taizhouism](../religions/taizhouism.md).

## Current limitations

Several encyclopedia-level details remain open, including:

- precise demographic ratios by region
- administrative relationship between core republican institutions and remote outposts
- military organization and maritime force posture
