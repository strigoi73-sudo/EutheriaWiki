## Clarification on the Likian–Xin Guo Conflict

The Likian–Xin Guo conflict should not be understood as a single uninterrupted 450-year war. The more coherent reading is a centuries-long maritime rivalry.

Valtheran expansion into Oceanus Centralis began around 200–300 LC. From roughly 300 LC to 700/750 LC, Likia and Xin Guo were engaged in a prolonged strategic contest marked by intermittent naval wars, embargoes, proxy struggles, piracy and privateering, and competition over islands and trade routes. This rivalry culminated in a final major war in the eighth century LC, ending with Likian victory and the reconstitution of Xin Guo as the See of Xin Guo around 750–800 LC.

Earlier references to the “Likian–Xin Guo war” should be read in this clarified sense unless otherwise specified.

## Han Guo

Han Guo is a Chinese-classed republic that is, in practice, a deeply integrated Chinese–Korean union. Its official forms, court language, institutional culture, and civil tradition are Chinese, which is why the state is classed as Chinese in the aggregate data. On the ground, however, the republic is more cohesive than the culture map alone first suggests. The Chinese and Korean populations of the Han Guo region are best understood as overlapping language-family communities in a mixed frontier society where substantial bilingualism is likely, especially in urban and governing life.

The capital, Insan, is Korean. This should not be treated as anomalous. The best explanation is that Insan became the capital through a founding political compromise: Han Guo governs through a Chinese institutional order from a Korean city. This implies that the Korean side of the union was foundational rather than marginal.

Han Guo likely took coherent republican form in the post–Xin Guo-war order. In the aftermath of the Likian victory and the reorganization of the Strait region, Chinese and Korean communities in this part of Valthera had strong incentives to combine their resources. Economic pressure, competition with larger neighboring powers, and shared Taizhouist religious life all encouraged durable political union. Han Guo is therefore best understood not as a conquest state, but as a negotiated republican compact.

Han Guo’s northwestern holdings are not a second heartland. The biome and political maps show that they sit on the far western edge of the habitable fringe beneath the glacier. They should be understood as remote extraction outposts—small, sea-linked, and economically useful—rather than as part of a broad northern territorial corridor.

Han Guo’s Chinese character is expressed not through a Chinese capital, but through its institutions, its religious life, and its duty to protect the sacred Taizhouist center at Shang.

### Working Canon Summary

Han Guo is a Chinese-led but deeply integrated Chinese–Korean republic: politically centered in the Korean city of Insan, socially bound by shared Taizhouist life, and historically shaped by the post–Xin Guo-war order. Its institutions are Chinese, its capital is Korean by founding compromise, and its legitimacy is reinforced by its guardianship of Quz Guo.

## Quz Guo

Quz Guo is a small sovereign Chinese Taizhouist state centered on Shang, the birthplace of Taizhouist philosophy and religion. It predates Han Guo and should be understood as one of the older city-states of the region rather than as a later creation of the republic.

Its continued separate existence is explained by sacred status rather than by raw power. Quz Guo is formally sovereign and governs itself internally, but its security and broader geopolitical shelter depend on Han Guo. The right comparison is not a normal minor coastal duchy, but a sacred polity whose independence is preserved because absorption into ordinary secular administration would diminish its religious standing.

Han Guo’s protection of Quz Guo is not merely a matter of convenience or treaty mechanics. It is a religious duty built into Han Guo’s self-understanding. Han Guo safeguards Shang and preserves Quz Guo’s autonomy in much the same way that a larger religious civilization protects its holiest center without reducing it to an ordinary province.

The resulting structure is dual but coherent: Insan is the political capital of the republic, while Shang is the sacred center of the wider Taizhouist world.

### Working Canon Summary

Quz Guo is a sovereign sacred Taizhouist state centered on Shang, the birthplace of the tradition. It predates Han Guo and remains formally independent, but its security and external position are bound to Han Guo, which acts as its protector because safeguarding Shang is part of the republic’s religious duty.

## Hayanguk

Hayanguk is a substantial inland Korean theocracy in western Valthera. It should be understood first as a **buffer state**: geographically between Korean, Chinese, and Dwarven spheres, and religiously between mainstream Korean faith and a frontier synthesis that its neighbors regard with suspicion.

Its defining religion is **Tongj Apostatism**, a Korean reinterpretation of Dwarven religious tradition blended with elements of the Witnesses of Dedeok. That makes Hayanguk not merely a Korean state with an unusual local cult, but a polity whose entire public character has been shaped by frontier religious synthesis. Tongj Apostatism is considered an apostasy by the Dedeokans and a heresy by the Dwarves, though the Dwarves are generally less alarmed by it and tend to regard Tongists as peculiar rather than intolerable. The sharper religious hostility comes from the Dedeokan side.

### Geographic Position

Hayanguk occupies an inland position in the south-central western part of Valthera. It does not possess open maritime access and has no ports. This is one of the most important facts about it. Hayanguk is not a naval state and should not be understood through the maritime logic that shapes parts of Chinese Valthera.

Its position places it between **Cheonguk** to the west and northwest, **Guan Guo** to the north and east, and **Han Guo** to the south. It also fronts on the rougher upland belt associated with the Dwarven corridor. This makes Hayanguk a hinge state. It sits where Korean lowland society, Chinese neighbors, and the upland Dwarven frontier all meet.

### Political Character

Hayanguk is a theocracy, but its most important political trait is not simple clerical rule. It is a state that thinks of itself as **beleaguered**. Its political culture is defensive, guarded, and deeply concerned with preserving the internal dominance of its religious order.

It does not read as missionary or outwardly ambitious. Its worldview is closer to that of a state that believes it has carved out a precarious space for its own survival and now must prevent outside pressures from corrupting or dissolving it. It wants to be left alone, but does not assume it will be.

### Demographic and Social Character

Hayanguk is large enough to matter. It has a population of roughly 2.53 million, making it a real regional power rather than a tiny frontier remnant. At the same time, it is overwhelmingly rural, with only a modest urban system and no dominant commercial metropolis. This suggests a stable agrarian society rather than a heavily urbanized religious-commercial state.

Its burgs are culturally entirely Korean, which sets it apart from the more visibly mixed settlement pattern of Han Guo. Religiously, however, it is not uniform in a simple sense. Tongj Apostatism is dominant, but other faiths are present within the state, which reinforces the idea that Hayanguk exists in a frontier zone under constant religious pressure rather than inside a sealed sacred core.

### Economy

Hayanguk appears economically solid, self-sufficient, and inwardly oriented.

It has no ports and is therefore not dependent on maritime trade. Its economy is best understood as primarily agrarian, supported by a heavily rural population. The maps also suggest a strongly forested environment, and a significant river system descending from the uplands into the settled zone. Taken together, this points toward an economy based on agriculture, forestry, river-linked internal transport, and trade with the Dwarven uplands.

Hayanguk is probably a secondary transit zone from east to west, but not a major long-distance commercial corridor. Most surrounding states have access to maritime outlets or other more efficient routes, so Hayanguk is unlikely to dominate continental trade. It is better understood as a state that can sustain itself and profit from local frontier exchange without becoming a great mercantile crossroads.

Its trade with the Dwarves is likely important. The clearest likely exchange is frontier complementarity: timber, food, and lowland goods flowing uphill; metals, tools, stonework, and upland products flowing down. Hayanguk probably has a significant timber industry, though it should be thought of as a major resource base rather than as a flamboyant export empire.

### Diplomatic Posture

Hayanguk maintains relations with outsiders in a manner best described as **polite but reserved**.

It is not isolationist in the absolute sense. It must deal with merchants, neighboring states, and Dwarven trade. But it treats outside contact as something to be managed and contained, not welcomed. Outsiders are expected to conduct their business under supervision and then leave.

This follows directly from the state’s sense of religious vulnerability. Hayanguk sees foreign influence as a potential threat to the internal hegemony of Tongj Apostatism. Its diplomacy is therefore formal, controlled, and rarely warm.

### Fortification and Military Character

Hayanguk is more fortified than average. About one-third of its burgs have walls, citadels, or both, which places it above the overall norm and above many neighboring states. This supports the image of Hayanguk as a defensive frontier polity rather than an expansionist power.

Its military character should therefore be understood as primarily protective. Hayanguk does not look like a conquering theocracy. It looks like a state that invests in fortification, local security, and the protection of key internal nodes because it expects pressure from outside.

### Working Canon Summary

Hayanguk should be understood as a Korean theocratic buffer state: inland, forested, defensively minded, and shaped by a frontier religious synthesis. It is economically self-sufficient, not commercially dominant; diplomatically polite but wary; and politically committed above all to preserving the internal dominance of Tongj Apostatism against outside influence.

## Cheonguk

Cheonguk is the largest Korean kingdom in western Valthera and should be understood as the **mainstream Korean anchor state** of the region. It is large, culturally uniform, coastal, and politically self-assured. Where Hayanguk represents the defensive frontier and Han Guo represents a mixed republican union, Cheonguk represents conventional Korean kingship at major-power scale.

### Geographic Position

Cheonguk dominates much of the western coast of Valthera. It has broad maritime frontage, multiple coastal inlets, and several ports, making it far more outward-facing than inland Hayanguk. At the same time, it is not a naval republic in the Han Guo mold. Its political character is still primarily that of a kingdom rooted in land, dynasty, and territorial order rather than in maritime commerce alone.

Cheonguk sits west and northwest of Hayanguk and helps define the western Korean core. It is geographically removed from the mixed Chinese–Korean transition zone that shapes Han Guo. This makes Cheonguk the clearest example of a large Korean state that is neither a frontier theocracy nor a confessional hybrid.

### Political Character

Cheonguk is best understood as a confident, status-conscious kingdom that presents itself as a respectable guardian of order. It is strong enough to behave pragmatically rather than nervously. Its diplomatic posture is likely formal, controlled, and accustomed to routine dealings with outsiders, but not especially warm.

The current capital is **Eoyangwan**. This should be treated as established for current canon development.

### Religion and Internal Settlement

Cheonguk is culturally unified but religiously divided. Its older core should be understood as **Dedeokan**, while its Tongj regions are later acquisitions.

The best explanation is that Cheonguk expanded into Tongj territory within the last century, during a period of general lawlessness in the inland frontier before Hayanguk existed as a coherent state. Those frontier acquisitions remained in Cheonguk’s hands at the end of the expansion, while the remaining Tongj regions later consolidated into Hayanguk.

This gives Cheonguk a clear religious settlement:
- the older core of the kingdom is Dedeokan
- the annexed frontier territories are Tongj
- Tongj worship is protected in those designated regions
- Tongj inhabitants enjoy full rights as subjects of the kingdom
- the crown limits the spread of Tongjism into the older Dedeokan heartland

Cheonguk therefore does not treat its Tongj territories as occupied or provisional. It regards them as fully and permanently part of the kingdom. Its policy is not persecution, but **territorial toleration under royal control**.

### Relationship to Hayanguk

Cheonguk did not seize these lands from a fully formed Hayanguk. It expanded during an earlier period of disorder in what later became Hayanguk’s sphere. Hayanguk emerged only after that frontier began to consolidate, forming a defensive Tongj buffer state from the remaining territories.

As a result, the two states have an asymmetrical relationship. Hayanguk would prefer those Tongj lands to lie within its own religious sphere, but it lacks the political will to challenge Cheonguk’s control. Cheonguk, by contrast, treats them as settled possessions and does not organize its policy around Hayanguk’s religious sensitivities.

### Economy and Maritime Orientation

Cheonguk has a substantial coastline and several ports, so it is more commercially open and externally connected than Hayanguk. Still, it should not be understood as a maritime mercantile state in the same way as Han Guo. The sea is an important instrument of exchange and contact, not the sole basis of its identity.

Its scale, coastline, and population make it one of the major Korean powers of Valthera. It is likely a balanced kingdom: agrarian at base, coastal in access, and politically integrated enough to remain stable despite internal confessional division.

### Working Canon Summary

Cheonguk should be understood as a large Dedeokan-centered Korean kingdom that expanded into Tongj territory during a recent period of frontier lawlessness. It now treats those lands as fully its own, protects Tongj worship within them, and limits Tongj expansion into the older heartland. With Eoyangwan as its current capital, Cheonguk stands as the mainstream Korean power of western Valthera: stable, status-conscious, and more confident than either Han Guo or Hayanguk in its own legitimacy.

## Hamcheon

Hamcheon is a small Korean Dedeokan coastal duchy on the southwestern edge of Cheonguk. It should be understood as a minor but durable polity whose survival depends less on power than on defensibility, cohesion, and political modesty.

### Geographic Position

Hamcheon occupies a narrow coastal wedge between Cheonguk and the sea. It has only one meaningful land neighbor—Cheonguk—and therefore exists almost entirely within Cheonguk’s strategic shadow. Its terrain is not merely flat coast: the duchy includes defensible upland ground behind the shoreline, giving it more internal resilience than its small size might suggest.

### Cultural and Religious Character

Hamcheon is strikingly homogeneous. Its known settlements are all Korean in culture and all follow the Witnesses of Dedeok. Unlike Han Guo or Cheonguk, it does not appear to be managing a major cultural or confessional divide. This makes Hamcheon a useful contrast state in western Valthera: small, simple, cohesive, and local.

### Fortification and Political Identity

Hamcheon is unusually heavily fortified for a state of its size. All of its known burgs possess walls, citadels, or both. This should not be treated as incidental. Fortification appears to be part of the duchy’s identity.

Hamcheon is best understood as a polity oriented toward **holding**, **withstanding**, and **surviving** rather than expanding. Its ruling class likely thinks in terms of continuity, security, and local endurance rather than grand ambition.

### Economy

Hamcheon’s economy is probably modest and regional. With a small population and only one port, it is not a major commercial or naval actor. The most likely economic base is a combination of local agriculture, fishing, forestry, small-scale coastal trade, and harbor and regional exchange functions.

It may matter as a local coastal gate, but not as a continental trade hub.

### Relationship to Cheonguk

Hamcheon is best understood as formally independent but heavily constrained by Cheonguk’s dominance of the surrounding region. It is not large enough to challenge Cheonguk and likely survives because annexing it would cause more disruption than benefit.

This makes Hamcheon neither a major client state nor a fully free actor in great-power terms. It is a genuinely independent minor duchy whose room to maneuver is narrow and whose security depends on remaining orderly, predictable, and unthreatening.

### Working Canon Summary

Hamcheon is a small, heavily fortified Korean Dedeokan coastal duchy that survives through caution, defensibility, and political modesty. It is formally independent, but its strategic environment is dominated by Cheonguk, which limits its room to maneuver without erasing its local autonomy.

## Guan Guo

Guan Guo is a cohesive Chinese kingdom in western Valthera whose importance comes less from spectacle than from position. It should be understood as a **river-gate toll kingdom**: a state whose capital, economy, and political tone are shaped by control of a major inland-to-sea water system.

### Geographic Position

Guan Guo occupies a central position in western Valthera between several important neighboring powers. It borders Longlin Guo to the north, Tengc to the east, Kaihui to the south and southeast, Han Guo to the southwest, and Hayanguk to the west and southwest. This places it at the meeting point of several regional systems without making it a frontier state in the Hayanguk sense.

The kingdom is organized around a very long and significant river that runs through much of its territory before reaching the sea near the capital, **Xuzhoubin**. This river leaves Guan Guo for part of its course and then returns, which makes Guan Guo’s control partial rather than absolute. Even so, the kingdom appears to hold some of the most valuable stretches of the system: the lower and reentry sections, the river mouth, and the easiest outlet to the coast.

### The Bay and the Outlet

Guan Guo’s strategic value is sharpened by the bay into which its river system empties. This is not just ordinary coastline. It is a sheltered inner basin where Longlin’s inland river world, Guan Guo’s lower-river customs zone, and Tengc’s eastern shore all meet before traffic reaches the open sea.

The heightmap suggests that the Longlin and Tengc shores of this bay are comparatively steep and elevated, while Guan Guo’s western and southwestern shoreline is lower and flatter. This likely explains why Guan Guo, not its neighbors, possesses the best natural port conditions in the basin. The capital, Xuzhoubin, sits exactly where one would expect a customs capital to sit: at the river mouth on the most usable low shore.

This means Guan Guo does not control the whole bay, but it does control the most favorable transfer point between inland river cargo and seaborne movement. That is a natural advantage, not merely a political accident.

### Economic Character

Guan Guo should be understood first as a **toll kingdom**. It likely profits from:
- customs and tolls on river traffic
- bonded storage and warehousing
- river policing and escorted movement
- transshipment between inland and coastal routes
- control of the coastal outlet at Xuzhoubin

The kingdom is not merely living off passing trade. The maps also suggest that Guan Guo possesses a substantial grassland interior, giving it a strong productive base of its own. This means Guan Guo is not just a middleman state but a kingdom with real internal economic strength, likely based on agriculture and pastoral production as well as river commerce.

### Water Orientation

Guan Guo should not be imagined as a blue-water maritime power. Its strength is better understood as **riverine and littoral**. It uses water as an instrument of internal integration and controlled transit rather than as the basis of broad seaborne dominance.

Its narrow coastal frontage is therefore not a weakness. It concentrates authority over the outlet rather than dispersing it across a large shoreline.

### Cultural and Religious Character

Guan Guo is one of the more internally cohesive states in western Valthera. It is culturally Chinese at both the state and settlement level, with only a trace of Dwarven presence in one burg. Religiously, its burgs are uniformly associated with the **Kaibeihuan Cult**.

This uniformity distinguishes Guan Guo from neighboring states such as Han Guo and Cheonguk, both of which manage more visible internal divisions. Guan Guo appears to be a kingdom whose ruling order does not spend much energy containing confessional fracture. That likely contributes to its reputation for stability and administrative seriousness.

### Political Tone

Taken together, the sources suggest that Guan Guo is an **orderly, transactional, and quietly strategic** kingdom. It is not the most culturally dramatic or politically flamboyant state in the region, but it is structurally important because it sits on a corridor that others need.

A state in this position would value:
- predictable flow
- enforceable toll rights
- stable borders
- customs enforcement
- river security
- practical agreements with neighbors

This likely makes Guan Guo more conservative and managerial than ideological. Its power comes from keeping movement taxable, orderly, and difficult to bypass.

### Working Canon Summary

Guan Guo is a prosperous Chinese toll kingdom built around partial control of a major inland-to-sea river system and the best natural outlet on a shared commercial bay. Its strength comes from combining a productive grassland interior with control of the lower and reentry stretches of a strategic trade artery. Cohesive in religion and culture, it is less dramatic than its neighbors but more structurally important than it first appears: a watchful, orderly, river-gate monarchy that profits from keeping movement predictable, taxable, and hard to bypass.

## Longlin Guo

Longlin Guo is a proud northern Chinese kingdom whose territory is large but whose meaningful life is concentrated in the southern belt. It should not be understood as a fully integrated state spread evenly across its whole map area. Much of its far north lies under icefield influence or in marginal, hard-to-settle country. Its actual political and economic core lies farther south, where its burgs, capital, and the river system converge.

### Geography and Settlement

Longlin controls a large share of the middle course of the major river system that eventually empties through Guan Guo’s bay. Its capital, **Andenan**, sits on the large lake just north of that bay and is also its only port. The placement of its six burgs makes it clear that Longlin is not organized around the frozen north, but around the southern river-facing zone where movement and settlement are viable.

This gives Longlin real leverage. It is not simply an upstream hinterland. It holds much of the commercially meaningful middle river and therefore influences access, cargo flow, and movement before goods ever reach Guan Guo’s lower customs zone.

### Communications and Constraint

At the same time, Longlin is **hamstrung by its own communications geography**. The maps suggest that there is no clear major road linking the capital to the northern burgs without traversing Guan Guo’s territory. Its bay shoreline also appears less naturally suited to major port development than Guan Guo’s lower western shore. As a result, Longlin controls much of the river but not the best maritime exit.

This creates a structural frustration: Longlin matters a great deal, but geography denies it a clean downstream advantage. It therefore has reason to be acutely sensitive to access, outlet options, and the profits captured by downstream states.

### Resource Frontier

Longlin’s northern and upland reaches are best understood as a **resource frontier**. Difficult overland movement through the ice-affected north makes the river corridor especially valuable. Settlements like **Xingzhou**, the first major settlement on the river in the more usable southern zone, are well placed to function as toll, logging, and transfer points where northern goods begin to enter organized downstream trade.

This gives Longlin an economic role distinct from Guan Guo’s. Longlin helps gather, organize, and move northern resources into the river system before Guan Guo monetizes them farther downriver.

### Relationship to Guan Guo and the Bay

Longlin and Guan Guo almost certainly operate under some kind of standing river arrangement. Their interests are too entangled for anything else to work. Longlin controls much of the middle course; Guan Guo controls the easier lower outlet and the best natural customs point at the bay.

Longlin likely sees Guan Guo as both necessary and overprivileged. Guan Guo profits from a downstream position Longlin cannot easily replace, while Longlin supplies traffic and access that Guan Guo cannot generate by itself.

Tengc provides a secondary alternative through **Siang**, but the heightmap suggests that Tengc’s bay shoreline is not as naturally favorable as Guan Guo’s. This means Longlin has options, but none that free it completely from dependence on downstream partners.

### Political Tone

Longlin’s diplomatic and political tone is therefore best understood as **proud, upstream, and resistant to dependence**. It is not passive. Its high expansionism and broad but thinly settled territory suggest a monarchy comfortable with frontier assertion and with claiming wide spaces even when it cannot densely integrate them.

It likely values:
- status
- freedom of movement
- multiple outlet options
- leverage over transit terms
- and recognition that its upstream role is indispensable

Longlin is probably capable of practical deals, but not relaxed ones. It is likely to resent downstream toll-taking even while relying on it.

### Working Canon Summary

Longlin Guo is a proud upstream frontier kingdom that controls much of the river’s usable middle course and derives leverage from being the first organizer of northern resources and traffic. Its real strength lies in the southern river-facing belt, not the ice-bound north. It depends on downstream outlets it does not fully control, which gives it a diplomatic tone shaped by status-conscious pragmatism and resistance to being made subordinate by Guan Guo or Tengc.
