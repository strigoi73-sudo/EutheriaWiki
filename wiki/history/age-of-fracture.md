# Age of Fracture

The Age of Fracture is the historical period that began around **876 LC**, when the [Veltric Empire](veltric-empire.md) lost dynastic continuity and the old imperial center failed to preserve political unity. It is not a single war, but a long phase of contested legitimacy, successor-state formation, and recurring institutional weakening across eastern Nereth.

## Public beginning of the Fracture

The immediate trigger was the death of the emperor in [Marcici](../places/marcici.md) without an heir. Because Marcici had long been the joint seat of both Emperor and Pontif, the crisis was political and ecclesiastical at the same time.

In the aftermath, the [Rocciaganel Church](../religions/rocciaganel-church.md) in Marcici declared itself the de facto governing authority of the Empire until lawful continuity could be restored. Many provincial elites regarded that move as usurpation rather than guardianship. That public declaration turned a succession crisis into the broader rupture now called the Fracture.

## Four phases

Public chronology in current canon treats the period from 876 LC to 1026 LC in four phases:

1. **Immediate Collapse (876 to ~900 LC)**: civil wars, competing claims, emergency rule, and the breakdown of centralized legitimacy.
2. **Wars of Settlement (~900 to ~950 LC)**: temporary wartime commands hardened into successor states and defended borders.
3. **False Stabilization (~950 to ~1000 LC)**: the largest wars diminished and a rough post-imperial order seemed to take shape.
4. **Deepening Fracture (~1000 to 1026 LC)**: trade routes became more contested, religious conflict sharpened, and new disturbances made the post-imperial order feel unstable again.

## Present character

The Age of Fracture should not be treated as a closed post-collapse chapter. In 1026 LC, the world is living inside a later and deeper phase of the same unraveling.

Successor states are old enough to possess their own dynasties, institutions, and regional loyalties, yet young enough to remain haunted by imperial precedent. The result is a political order that appears durable on the surface while still struggling over legitimacy, inheritance, and the meaning of the imperial past.
