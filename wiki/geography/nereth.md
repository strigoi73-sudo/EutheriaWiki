# Nereth

Nereth is the northeastern continent of Eutheria, a region of glaciated coasts, major interior mountain systems, fertile eastern lowlands, and politically consequential geographic separation. More than any other major continent except perhaps Valthera, Nereth's history is shaped by the way terrain divides populations and channels power.

It is best understood as a continent of broken continentality: a set of adjacent civilizational systems linked by trade, conflict, and geography, but not naturally unified into a single political world.

## Geographic structure

The northern coast is glaciated and severe, limiting dense habitation and pushing the major centers of settled life toward more temperate zones. Farther south and inland, Nereth rises into some of the highest mountains in the known world. These interior barriers are not incidental scenery. They are among the principal reasons the continent developed as a politically fragmented and regionally differentiated space.

A major mountain range separates the eastern heartlands from the western coastal regions. This divide has long constrained movement, slowed consolidation, and made control of passes strategically decisive. Dwarven realms, positioned in key upland systems, command many of the trans-mountain routes and therefore occupy an outsized place in the continent's political geography.

These mountains are not merely a barrier. They are the hinge through which west and east must negotiate with one another, and they are one of the main reasons no single Nerethan system has fully absorbed the rest of the continent.

## Regions and biomes

Nereth contains sharp ecological transitions.

- The far north grades into glacier, tundra, and taiga.
- The interior highlands combine severe elevation with forested and grassland zones.
- The eastern lowlands are more favorable to dense population, agriculture, and durable state formation.
- The western coast supports temperate deciduous forest and temperate rainforest systems tied to maritime contact.

This mixture makes Nereth neither uniformly harsh nor uniformly accessible. Instead, it is a continent of separated productive regions joined by difficult corridors.

## Peoples and civilizational pattern

Nereth includes Nordic-, Roman-, Italian-, Castillian-, German-, English-, French-, Portuguese-, and Anglo-Saxon-analog cultures, as well as Dwarven highland populations. These peoples do not occupy the continent as a single blended field. They are distributed through regions whose geography reinforces different historical trajectories, political traditions, and external orientations.

The result is a continent where cultural plurality and political fragmentation are mutually reinforcing. Shared continental space does not easily produce continental unity.

## Historical significance

Nereth's interior divide helps explain the long-term separation between western Nereth and the eastern regions once dominated by the Veltric Empire. Imperial influence in the east never translated into simple continental integration because the mountain systems limited how fully power, infrastructure, and identity could be unified across the whole landmass.

Even after imperial decline, those old structural separations continued to matter. Nereth's political history is therefore marked less by stable continent-wide rule than by alternating periods of regional consolidation, fragmentation, and selective cross-mountain competition.

The present-day balance has shifted as well. The old Likian trade system once entered through the south and moved eastward through imperial channels. After the Veltric collapse, western maritime systems gained practical weight, especially through [Lienia](../states/lienia.md) and the routes that feed the dwarven passes.

## Strategic character

Nereth matters because it combines productive eastern heartlands, difficult western access, maritime contact zones, and mountain-controlled interior routes. Powers that dominate one region of the continent do not automatically control the others.

This gives Nereth enduring strategic depth. It is a continent whose barriers preserve pluralism, whose passes create leverage, and whose geography ensures that history is repeatedly shaped by the question of connection versus separation.
