# Skrosenism

Skrosenism is the largest organized religion in the known world, with followers across three continents and especially deep roots among Flandric and related maritime peoples. Its durability comes in part from its decentralized character: the pantheon is shared, but local communities determine which deity stands at the center of worship.

## Founding tradition

The religion takes its name from Skrosen, remembered within the faith as prophet, priest, and king. Public scholarship ranges from literalist readings of his divine communion to more skeptical views that see a real historical temple-builder later mythologized into a foundational figure. The tension between reverent tradition and skeptical interpretation remains live within the religion because no central doctrinal authority exists to settle the question once and for all.

## Pantheon and practice

Skrosenism is a full polytheist pantheon religion whose deities map across the alignment structure. Good and lawful deities dominate mainstream practice, while Halmor anchors funerary tradition and the Grey Shore afterlife framework. The religion's afterlife teaching emphasizes custody of the dead rather than paradise or damnation: the dead are received, tended, and passed onward beyond the Grey Shore.

Institutionally, the religion has no single doctrinal head. Prestige flows through age, reputation, and temple importance rather than binding hierarchy. The oldest temples of [Garka](../states/garka.md) carry unusual weight in this prestige order, but they do not rule the faith in the manner of a centralized church.

## Cultural variants

The faith takes different tones in different societies. In [Likia](../states/likia.md), Skrosenism is often rationalist and maritime in emphasis, with strong concern for commerce, navigation, and the practical domains of the gods. In the Flandric heartland it is more closely tied to founding memory, temple antiquity, and regional sacred tradition. In all its variants, however, the religion remains remarkably stable in pantheon structure even as names, emphasis, and devotional style shift by culture.

## Clergy and localism

Because Skrosenism is decentralized, local temples and their senior clergy matter more than universal institutions. Communities often understand the religion through their own render, temple, saint tradition, funerary custom, and regional sacred sites rather than through a distant hierarchy. This localism is one of the main reasons the religion spread so widely without losing coherence.
