# Biomes of Eutheria

Eutheria spans glacial extremes, tundra, taiga, temperate forests, wetlands, grasslands, savannas, deserts, and tropical systems. Biome distribution is one of the deepest structural influences on the setting because it shapes where dense populations can form, what kinds of economies emerge, and which states can sustain large-scale power.

## Major biome patterns

Some biomes support concentrated population and durable state development more readily than others.

- **Temperate deciduous forest** is the most populous biome in the known world.
- **Temperate rainforest** can support high habitability and dense settlement where terrain permits.
- **Grassland** and **savanna** zones are especially important in Kasmora, where they support major agrarian and pastoral systems.
- **Tundra**, **hot desert**, and **cold desert** sustain much sparser populations.
- **Glacier** regions are effectively uninhabitable for normal large-scale humanoid settlement.

These differences matter politically. A state rooted in productive temperate or alluvial regions begins with very different structural advantages from one occupying severe uplands, deserts, or frozen margins.

## Continental distribution

[Valthera](valthera.md) and [Nereth](nereth.md) combine glaciated northern zones with more productive temperate lands farther south. Their political histories are therefore shaped by the contrast between harsh margins and viable agrarian cores.

[Kasmora](kasmora.md) contains the broadest concentration of highly productive inhabited biomes, especially plains, grasslands, savannas, and fertile river systems. This ecological wealth helps explain its demographic centrality and the coexistence of several major powers.

[Golsta](golsta.md) remains dominated by permanent cold conditions, glacial systems, and only narrow habitable margins, which is one reason it never developed as a normal humanoid continental sphere.

## Historical significance

Biome distribution helps explain some of the largest differences in the setting: why certain continents host dense civilizational competition, why some states become breadbaskets while others become maritime intermediaries, and why ecological extremes preserve frontier spaces or nonhuman dominance.

The map of biomes is therefore also a map of long-term historical possibility.
