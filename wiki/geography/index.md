# Geography

Eutheria is a world defined by oceans, continental separation, and strong regional environmental contrasts. Four major continents and the maritime system of [Oceanus Centralis](../glossary/oceanus-centralis.md) provide the basic physical frame within which trade, migration, state formation, and civilizational difference all occur. The known world is pre-industrial in character, so terrain still matters in immediate ways: mountain barriers shape political fragmentation, river systems structure agriculture and movement, and sea routes remain the only reliable means of long-distance intercontinental contact.

## Continental Structure

The known world is organized around [Valthera](valthera.md) in the northwest, [Nereth](nereth.md) in the northeast, [Kasmora](kasmora.md) in the southwest, and [Golsta](golsta.md) in the southeast. Each continent supports a different relationship between geography and civilization. Valthera combines lake systems, productive southern lowlands, and major mountain belts, which helps explain its mixture of maritime powers, inland corridors, and culturally distinct frontier zones. Nereth is more sharply segmented by mountains, especially the barrier between its eastern and western spheres, making geography one of the main reasons its historical development fractured so strongly.

Kasmora is the most agriculturally productive continent, with broad plains, reliable river systems, and deep soils that support large populations without producing a single lasting continental empire. Golsta, by contrast, remains largely inhospitable to humanoid settlement, its glacial and mountainous conditions preserving it more as a wilderness continent than a political one. The [Central Island Chain](central-island-chain.md) occupies an especially important position between Valthera and Kasmora, because the islands sit directly across the most valuable sea routes linking the inhabited continents.

## Seas, Routes, and Chokepoints

Oceanus Centralis is not simply water between landmasses. It is the world's central connective medium, crossed by currents, seasonal routes, island waypoints, and strategic passages that determine who can trade, who can project power, and who can isolate rivals. This is one reason states with modest land area can still matter enormously if they control maritime chokepoints, harbors, or transfer routes.

The contrast between continental interiors and coastal access is one of the most important geographic patterns in the setting. [Western Maritime Nereth](western-maritime-nereth.md) shows how a seaward orientation can produce a political world quite different from inland kingdoms beyond the same mountains. Regional studies such as [Rombevenia](rombevenia.md) and [Dudbury](dudbury.md) further illustrate that geography in Eutheria works at multiple scales at once: continents establish broad civilizational conditions, while local basins, coasts, forests, and route systems determine how people actually live within them.

## Climate, Biomes, and Settlement

Much of the setting's demographic distribution is best explained through climate history and biome patterning rather than through politics alone. The northern reaches of Valthera and Nereth remain shaped by glaciation, cold margins, and highland conditions, while southern belts support denser agriculture, urbanization, and long-term state development. [Biomes of Eutheria](biomes-of-eutheria.md) is especially useful here because it connects environmental zones to habitability, productivity, and frontier pressure across the world.

The Great Withdrawal after the end of the Ice Age still matters indirectly to the modern map. Forest expansion, newly habitable ground, shifting river systems, and the uneven retreat of ice created the physical basis for later migration, recovery, and settlement growth. Geography in Eutheria is therefore not static backdrop. It is an active historical force, one of the clearest reasons why the world contains both dense cores of civilization and broad seams of sparse control.
